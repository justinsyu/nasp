## Key Research and Content Themes

### 1. Integrated HSSP as an enterprise performance model

Shields consistently positions specialty pharmacy as a health-system operating model, not simply a dispensing channel. The strongest proof points emphasize integrated teams, EMR access, embedded pharmacists and liaisons, financial assistance, payer/LDD access, patient engagement, and outcomes measurement.

The 2025 outcomes report landing page describes a scale story: more than 2 million patients, 50+ integrated disease states, and nearly 80 health-system partners. The content is aimed at executives and specialty pharmacy leaders who need comparable benchmarks for access, adherence, time to therapy, and disease-state performance.

## Implied Objectives and Strategy

Shields appears to be using research and downloadable content for five linked objectives:

1. Prove the integrated HSSP model at enterprise scale. The outcomes reports and benchmark content position Shields as the largest or most comprehensive source of HSSP performance data.
2. Support health-system investment decisions. Cost of care, clinician utilization, prescription leakage, and financial performance content speaks to executives and finance leaders.
3. Win payer and LDD access. Payer white papers, access content, accreditation-related posters, and outcomes benchmarks create a rationale for network inclusion and manufacturer confidence.
4. Give service lines a clinical reason to engage. Disease-state assets translate the model into oncology, diabetes, rheumatology, HIV/HCV, CF, MS, GLP-1, and cardiometabolic care.
5. Create a repeatable content engine. Posters become white papers, case studies become landing pages, and outcomes reports become executive benchmark assets.

From an HSSP perspective, this is a mature content strategy. The remaining opportunity is not basic research generation; it is making the evidence more decision-grade for health-system leaders, payers, and accreditation/network stakeholders.

## Constructive Evidence Limitations

These limitations are framed as opportunities to improve confidence and usability for HSSP decision-makers:

- Many assets report strong descriptive outcomes, but denominators, cohort definitions, baseline periods, and follow-up windows are not always visible on landing pages or two-page outcomes sheets.
- Several disease-state posters and case studies are observational or QI-oriented. They are valuable for operational learning, but stronger comparison groups, risk adjustment, and sensitivity analyses would make them more credible for CFO, payer, and network access audiences.
- Attribution is sometimes implied rather than formally tested. For example, improved PDC, time to therapy, copay, or NPS are clearly aligned with the HSSP model, but some assets would benefit from a clearer counterfactual: what would have happened under external specialty pharmacy, pre-implementation workflow, or non-integrated care?
- Economic claims are compelling but could be standardized into a transparent HSSP value framework. Finance leaders will want clear separation of gross revenue, net operating income, drug margin, avoided medical cost, labor cost, technology cost, accreditation cost, and patient affordability impact.
- Patient experience and SDOH content could be strengthened by stratified analyses. Health-system leaders increasingly need to know whether specialty pharmacy improves access and outcomes equitably across payer type, race/ethnicity, geography, language, disease state, and financial assistance need.
- The corpus contains many strong assets, but the method quality varies by asset type. A publication roadmap could define which claims are suitable for marketing pages, which are suitable for conference posters, and which are ready for peer-reviewed or payer-facing evidence packages.

## HEOR Support Opportunities for an HSSP-Focused Vendor

### 1. Build a health-system specialty pharmacy value framework

Create a standardized model that links HSSP activities to outcomes by audience:

- Executives and finance: net operating income, leakage reduction, avoided utilization, labor productivity, service-line growth.
- Specialty pharmacy leaders: time to therapy, PDC, abandonment, PA turnaround, financial assistance, call center/access metrics.
- Service lines: disease-specific outcomes, clinical intervention value, provider satisfaction, clinic capacity.
- Payers/network access: total cost of care, utilization, adherence, quality metrics, data completeness.
- Accreditation/quality: patient experience, intervention documentation, safety, equity, continuous improvement.

### 2. Convert descriptive outcomes into decision-grade studies

For assets such as `Shields_WP_Diabetes_102022.pdf`, `Shields_WP_2025_GLP1_04.16.25.pdf`, and `Shields_WP_2025_Clinical-Interventions_10.14.25.pdf`, HEOR support could add protocolized cohorts, statistical analysis plans, baseline comparators, subgroup analyses, and sensitivity testing.

### 3. Create payer and network access dossiers

Shields already has the ingredients for payer-facing evidence: access metrics, adherence, claims-based total cost, PA/appeal outcomes, and disease-state outcomes. A vendor could package these into standardized payer dossiers for network inclusion, any-willing-provider discussions, LDD access, and value-based contracting.

### 4. Strengthen economic models for health-system finance

Build budget impact and ROI models that separate:

- Prescription capture and leakage
- Net specialty pharmacy contribution
- Staffing and technology costs
- Avoided utilization
- Clinic labor/time savings
- Patient affordability and financial assistance
- Scenario planning by disease state, payer mix, and LDD access

This would make the existing content more actionable for CFOs and strategy leaders.

### 5. Standardize benchmark definitions across the network

The `ASHP2024_PDC_FINAL.pdf` dashboard work is a strong start. HEOR support could expand this into a formal data dictionary and benchmark governance model covering adherence, persistence, time to therapy, abandonment, patient-reported outcomes, intervention acceptance, copay, and clinical outcomes.

### 6. Turn case studies into reusable evidence assets

Case studies such as Spartanburg patient satisfaction, CHRISTUS diabetes, SDOH, and growth planning could be strengthened with consistent before/after frameworks, implementation requirements, baseline metrics, operational context, and "transferability" notes for other health systems.

### 7. Build an equity and access analytics layer

The SDOH and affordability content creates a natural opening for HEOR support around equity analytics. This would help HSSPs demonstrate whether integrated specialty pharmacy improves access for patients who face higher financial, geographic, social, or payer-related barriers.

## Page-Ready Insights

1. Shields has the most mature HSSP evidence engine in this corpus, with content that spans clinical outcomes, access, affordability, total cost of care, clinician burden, patient experience, and network strategy.
2. The core Shields strategy is to reposition specialty pharmacy as a health-system performance platform: a way to improve care quality, retain volume, reduce leakage, expand access, and support enterprise financial goals.
3. The strongest research opportunity is not more posters for their own sake; it is converting existing descriptive and QI evidence into standardized, decision-grade HEOR assets for executives, payers, and accreditation/network stakeholders.
4. Shields' disease-state content is especially reusable. Diabetes, oncology, GLP-1/cardiometabolic, rheumatology, CF, MS, HIV, and HCV assets all follow a pattern other HSSPs could emulate: burden, barriers, intervention model, operational metrics, clinical outcomes, and patient value.
5. The total cost-of-care story is a major differentiator, but it would be stronger with transparent ROI models that separate pharmacy margin, avoided medical cost, labor savings, technology cost, and patient affordability impact.
6. Patient experience and SDOH content give Shields a mission-aligned story for quality leaders, but equity-stratified outcomes would make the case more relevant to health-system boards and population health teams.
7. A specialized HEOR partner could add the most value by creating a common HSSP measurement framework, strengthening comparative methods, packaging payer dossiers, and turning case studies into reusable evidence playbooks.
