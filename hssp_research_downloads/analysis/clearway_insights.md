## Key Research and Content Themes

Clearway's public evidence base is organized less like traditional conference research and more like an executive-facing HSSP value dossier. The dominant message is that a hospital-owned or hospital-integrated specialty pharmacy can become an enterprise asset that improves medication access, reduces leakage, supports 340B sustainability, improves provider experience, and creates margin that can be reinvested into mission-oriented care.

The strongest recurring themes are:

- Health-system financial sustainability through specialty pharmacy revenue capture, 340B optimization, reduced contract pharmacy dependence, payer/PBM access, and limited distribution drug access.
- Medication access operations, especially prior authorization turnaround, benefits investigation, copay/grant support, patient assistance, and liaison-led coordination.
- Embedded care-team model, with pharmacy liaisons and specialty pharmacists positioned as extensions of ambulatory clinics rather than as a standalone dispensing function.
- Mission alignment for safety-net and essential hospitals, especially serving Medicaid, Medicare, pediatric, rural, low-income, and medically complex patients.
- Accreditation and operational maturity, including URAC/ACHC accreditation, dashboards, pharmacy P&L methods, inventory controls, telephony, patient management software, and standardized monitoring.
- Strategic service-line expansion, including oncology, HIV, hepatitis C, inflammatory bowel disease, fertility, GLP-1 access, and cell and gene therapy readiness.

## Implied Objectives and Strategy

Clearway appears to be positioning itself as a health-system operating partner, not a narrow pharmacy vendor. The content is built for executive and pharmacy leadership audiences who need to justify investment, defend 340B strategy, improve access, reduce clinician burden, and produce measurable returns.

The implied strategy has four parts:

1. Use Boston Medical Center Health System as the proof-of-origin story. The BMC case study shows that the model was developed inside a safety-net health system and then commercialized for peer hospitals.
2. Convert specialty pharmacy from a cost center or outsourced function into a margin-generating service line. The OU Health, Signature Healthcare, Children's National, and BMC examples repeatedly frame specialty pharmacy as an enterprise asset.
3. Pair financial outcomes with access and mission outcomes. Revenue growth is consistently tied to patient assistance, affordability, vulnerable populations, provider burden reduction, or expanded services.
4. Package case studies for health-system decision-makers. The content is written in business-case language, with metrics on revenue, prior authorization turnaround, patient growth, accreditation, and operational controls.

### Boston Medical Center Health System Case Study

File: `66fb7bc11e664f364d47ba17_Clearway_Health_Boston_Medical_Center_Health_System_Case_Study.pdf`

This is Clearway's anchor asset because it connects clinical, operational, financial, and mission outcomes in one story. ## Constructive Evidence Limitations

These limitations do not weaken the business-development value of the content, but they indicate where HEOR support could strengthen Clearway's research quality and make the evidence more persuasive for sophisticated health-system audiences.

- Most assets are case studies, not structured outcomes studies. They are compelling for executives, but they generally do not specify study design, denominator definitions, inclusion/exclusion criteria, baseline periods, statistical methods, missing data handling, or comparison group construction.
- Several outcomes are multi-component business results without clear attribution. Revenue growth, claim eligibility improvement, patient growth, and access gains are likely influenced by staffing, contracting, EHR workflows, payer mix, market conditions, 340B rules, service-line growth, and health-system strategy. A stronger HEOR layer could separate association from plausible contribution.
- Financial results are prominent, but net value is less transparent. The content reports revenue and budget overperformance, but usually does not show implementation cost, staffing cost, technology cost, inventory carrying cost, working capital, bad debt, margin definitions, or sensitivity analyses.
- Clinical outcomes are strongest in the BMC case, but disease-specific measures could be standardized across the portfolio. For example, BMC reports PDC, SVR12, viral load suppression, and IBD clinical severity improvement, while other case studies emphasize access and revenue more than longitudinal clinical outcomes.
- Provider burden and operational efficiency are central claims, but measurement is often indirect. Prior authorization turnaround is well represented; clinician time saved, avoided rework, reduced call burden, staff productivity, and care-team satisfaction are not consistently quantified.
- Patient experience and equity are credible themes but could be measured more rigorously. The assets reference vulnerable populations, affordability, patient assistance, low health literacy, rurality, and access barriers, but do not consistently report stratified outcomes by payer, language, geography, race/ethnicity, income proxy, or disease state.
- Accreditation and payer/manufacturer access are framed as outcomes, but the causal pathway could be better documented. Clearway could show how accreditation, quality metrics, patient management infrastructure, and outcomes reporting affect payer network access, limited distribution drug access, and service-line growth.

## HEOR Support Opportunities From an HSSP Perspective

For a health-system specialty pharmacy vendor, the opportunity is not to produce pharma-style product value dossiers. The need is to help HSSPs prove the value of their operating model to health-system leadership, finance, pharmacy operations, service lines, payer/network stakeholders, quality/accreditation teams, and community mission leaders.

Highest-value HEOR opportunities:

- Build an HSSP value measurement framework that standardizes metrics across access, clinical, operational, financial, provider experience, patient experience, equity, accreditation readiness, and payer/LDD access.
- Convert case studies into structured evidence briefs with clear methods, denominators, time horizons, baseline periods, comparator definitions, and attribution logic while preserving executive readability.
- Develop net economic impact models for hospital-owned specialty pharmacy programs, including revenue, margin, avoided contract pharmacy fees, implementation costs, staffing, technology, inventory, patient assistance, working capital, and sensitivity analyses.
- Create disease-state outcome studies for priority service lines such as oncology, HIV, hepatitis C, IBD, fertility, GLP-1/metabolic disease, pediatrics, and cell/gene therapy readiness.
- Quantify provider burden reduction with workflow metrics: PA tasks shifted from clinics, days to therapy, appeals completed, calls avoided, staff time saved, referral leakage reduced, and refill/adherence interventions completed.
- Strengthen equity measurement by stratifying access, affordability, adherence, patient assistance, and clinical outcomes by payer type, geography, language, race/ethnicity where available, and social needs proxies.
- Create payer/network and accreditation evidence packets showing how HSSP quality infrastructure, care management, patient monitoring, and outcomes reporting support preferred network inclusion and limited distribution drug access.
- Build a repeatable case-study analytics backbone so every client story has a common evidence spine: problem, intervention, target population, operating model, metrics, baseline, follow-up, economic impact, patient impact, operational impact, and executive implication.

## Page-Ready Insights

1. Clearway's research/content strategy is built around the enterprise value of hospital-owned specialty pharmacy: better medication access, reduced administrative burden, stronger 340B performance, and revenue that can be reinvested into patient care.

2. The Boston Medical Center case study is the portfolio's anchor proof point because it ties financial growth to mission and clinical outcomes: specialty pharmacy revenue grew from `$20 million` to `$600 million`, while the program reports 95% PDC, `$30 million` in patient assistance, 2-day prior authorization turnaround, and disease-specific outcomes in HIV, hepatitis C, and IBD.

3. Children's National is Clearway's strongest forward-looking asset. It moves beyond classic specialty pharmacy operations into on-site pharmacy buildout, pediatric access, 340B resilience, accreditation, and cell/gene therapy financial and operational readiness.

4. Signature Healthcare shows how a community hospital can use an on-site pharmacy to reduce outsourcing, improve medication access, and generate measurable revenue, reporting `$31.7 million` in hospital revenue since opening and PA turnaround of 1.4 business days.

5. OU Health extends the value story into service-line strategy, especially fertility and statewide access. The local HTML evidence reports `$288 million` in revenue within two years, 4,524 patients served, 91% PA approval, and less-than-2-day PA turnaround.

6. Clearway's public evidence is commercially strong but methodologically uneven. A HEOR partner could help convert high-impact case studies into more defensible HSSP evidence by adding methods, denominators, baselines, comparator logic, net economic models, and outcome standardization.

7. The biggest unmet measurement opportunity is provider and operational burden. Clearway repeatedly claims reduced burden, but the public evidence could be stronger with quantified clinician time saved, clinic rework avoided, staff productivity, patient handoff reliability, and days-to-therapy improvements.

8. Clearway has a credible equity and mission story, especially for safety-net, pediatric, rural, and underserved populations. HEOR support could make that story more actionable by stratifying access, affordability, patient assistance, adherence, and outcomes across payer and social-risk groups.
