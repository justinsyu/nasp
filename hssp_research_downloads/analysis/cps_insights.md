## Key Research and Content Themes

### 1. HSSP outcomes measurement as the core proof system

CPS has built a visible outcomes-measurement content program around annual Patient Impact Reports and disease-state outcome studies. The strongest examples are:

- `2026-patient-impact-report.html` | URL: `https://perspectives.cps.com/2026-patient-impact-report`

- `2023-patient-impact-report.html` | URL: `https://perspectives.cps.com/2023-patient-impact-report`

## Implied Objectives and Strategy

1. Position CPS as a full-service partner for building, operating, optimizing, and proving the value of health-system specialty pharmacy programs.
2. Use Patient Impact Reports as a recurring flagship asset that converts internal network outcomes into market-facing benchmarks and best practices.
3. Use Therigy as the enabling platform for clinical dashboards, standardized documentation, benchmarking, and scaled outcomes research across HSSP partner sites.
4. Show health-system executives that HSSP investment can support revenue growth, patient experience, payer access, service-line alignment, accreditation, and cost containment.
5. Build credibility with pharmacy leaders through conference posters, published studies, disease-state protocols, and operational case studies.
6. Translate research into sales enablement: case studies, downloadable reports, webinars, issue briefs, and thought leadership pages all point toward program growth and optimization.

## Constructive Evidence Limitations

These are not criticisms of CPS. They are places where HSSP-focused HEOR support could make the evidence more useful for health-system decision-makers.

1. Many pages are landing pages for gated reports or posters rather than complete methods/results documents. This limits independent synthesis unless the downloadable forms are completed and full reports are obtained.
2. Several outcome-study pages provide background and objectives, but little detail on sample size, follow-up duration, statistical methods, missing data, comparator selection, or confidence intervals.
3. Much of the evidence appears to be QI, descriptive, or pre/post work. That is appropriate for HSSP operations, but stronger causal framing would help executives, payers, and service-line leaders interpret how much value is attributable to the HSSP model.
4. Clinical outcomes are often stronger than economic outcomes. The content references cost containment and revenue growth, but many clinical studies would be more persuasive with total cost of care, avoided utilization, labor cost, net contribution margin, and sensitivity analyses.
5. Patient-reported outcomes and SDOH/HRSN work are strategically important, but the corpus would benefit from clearer measurement standards, instrument selection rationale, and actionability thresholds.
6. Cross-site benchmarking is a major strength, but it needs careful risk adjustment and site-mix explanation to avoid misleading comparisons across health systems with different patient populations, payer mixes, staffing models, and service-line maturity.
7. The payer-access narrative is strong, but payer-facing evidence packages could be more explicit about which endpoints matter for network inclusion, preferred network negotiations, and LDD access.

## Page-Ready Insights

1. CPS has one of the clearest HSSP evidence engines in the corpus: annual Patient Impact Reports, outcome-study pages, poster announcements, case studies, and Therigy data assets all reinforce the same value story.
2. The strongest CPS message for health-system leaders is that HSSP outcomes measurement is not academic. It supports growth, payer access, service-line alignment, accreditation, patient experience, and financial performance.
3. Therigy is central to CPS's strategy because it turns HSSP operations into measurable, benchmarkable, and publishable evidence.
4. CPS's clinical evidence is broad, but the next step is economic translation: health-system executives need net financial impact, avoided utilization, staffing ROI, and total cost of care implications alongside clinical outcomes.
5. CPS already understands that payer access is an evidence problem. A stronger payer-facing evidence dossier could help convert outcomes work into network access leverage.
6. The annual Patient Impact Report series is a strong model for other HSSPs: package benchmarks, disease-state measures, patient/provider stories, and research highlights into a recurring market-facing asset.
7. Many CPS outcome assets are strong starting points but would be more useful with standardized methods, comparator logic, risk adjustment, and clearer interpretation guidance for non-research audiences.
8. The highest-value HEOR opportunity is not to make CPS's work more academic. It is to make the evidence easier for HSSP leaders, CFOs, service-line executives, payers, and quality teams to use in real decisions.
