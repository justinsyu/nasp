Evaluation of Autoinjector Safety Events in Health-System Specialty Pharmacy Patients

UK Specialty Pharmacy reviewed 2022 adult self-injectable dispensing and safety-event records to identify where autoinjector misfires clustered, whether patients experienced delayed therapy, and which risk signals should shape future counseling.

Research Question

What factors influence autoinjector safety events?

The study asked which patient, dispensing, and medication factors were associated with reported misfire events among adults filling self-injectable medications through a health-system specialty pharmacy.

  • Primary outcome: incidence of misfire events per 1,000 fills.
  • Secondary lens: odds of misfire by medication and demographic associations.
  • Clinical lens: whether a misfire produced a delay in care.

Evidence Flow

From dispensing history to an education target

1

Define cohort

Adults with at least one self-injectable medication dispense from 01/01/2022 to 12/31/2022.

2

Review records

Epic and Therigy were used to capture dispensing history, reported events, and patient demographics.

3

Count events

Misfires were normalized per fill and per 100 patients to compare across medication groups.

4

Model risk

Univariable logistic regression estimated odds of medication-specific misfire risk.

5

Translate action

Signals point toward proactive counseling for complex self-administered therapies.

Red markers identify products reported as statistically significant in the poster: evolocumab and fremanezumab-vfrm.

Signal interpretation: misfires were uncommon overall, but nearly half of reported events delayed therapy and the highest-confidence medication signals clustered in neurology and primary-care-adjacent products.

  • Evolocumab: 7 incidents across 1,067 fills; OR 6.31 (95% CI 2.45-16.26).
  • Fremanezumab-vfrm: 3 incidents across 532 fills; OR 4.04 (95% CI 1.15-14.12).
  • No demographic factor reached statistical significance among the 19 misfire reports.

Conclusion

Use the specialty care model before the next misfire

  • 1Move beyond dispense-only education by reinforcing administration technique for autoinjectors with higher observed risk.
  • 2Prioritize neurology and primary care patients receiving nonspecialty medications with complex self-administration requirements.
  • 3Build follow-up touchpoints that capture device issues early, before they create missed or delayed therapy.

Objective

Identify avoidable misfire trends

The poster aimed to identify trends in self-injectable misfire events and use those patterns to develop a targeted educational intervention to reduce avoidable misfires.

Study Boundaries

Adult 2022 dispensing cohort

Patients 18 years old or younger were excluded because parent or guardian administration could confound event attribution. Demographics included age, ethnicity, gender, payor, preferred language, and race.
