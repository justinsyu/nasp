The Impact of a Health System Specialty Pharmacy Disease Management Program on Diabetes Mellitus

Objective

Measure whether high-touch pharmacy management moved diabetes outcomes.

The poster compared A1c reduction for SOMC patients enrolled in Chronic Disease Management pharmacy services versus patients not enrolled, and evaluated adherence when specialty pharmacy liaisons and pharmacists managed diabetes medications.

1

Enroll adults with baseline and at least one 3-month A1c between July 2020 and March 2021.

2

Segment into high-risk enrolled, lower-risk enrolled, and non-enrolled comparison groups.

3

Provide refill calls, compliance check-ins, medication access support, drug utilization review, counseling, and escalation for high-risk patients.

Methods snapshot

Retrospective outpatient cohort

Design Adult patients enrolled in CDM pharmacy services from July 2020 to March 2021 with baseline and at least one 3-month A1c.
Groups High-risk enrolled with baseline A1c > 10; lower-risk enrolled with baseline A1c < 10; patients who chose not to enroll.
Measures A1c lab values, enrollment dates, high-risk support program graduation dates, and time from enrollment to graduation.
Analysis Average A1c reduction from baseline to most recent follow-up after at least three months.

Conclusion

Program effects clustered around A1c, savings, adherence, and expansion.

Greater A1c reduction

Enrolled patients had larger A1c decreases than non-enrolled patients, especially high-risk patients receiving added education.

Cost savings estimate

The high-risk A1c reduction translated to an estimated $1,619 per year, or about $135 per month, in diabetes-related cost savings.

Adherence signal

CDM-enrolled patients had a PDC of 0.91, above the national diabetes medication PDC range cited in the poster.

Next steps

The team planned continued diabetes outreach, additional family health clinic expansion, and growth into other disease states.
