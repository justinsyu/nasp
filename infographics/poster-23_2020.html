Multiple sclerosis disease-modifying therapy adherence and hospital costs

A retrospective MarketScan analysis used Pharmacy Quality Alliance adherence criteria to test whether consistent adherence to non-infused multiple sclerosis DMTs was associated with 2-year health care cost and utilization outcomes.

Evidence flow

How the adherence signal was constructed

Commercially insured MS cohort Adults in MarketScan with continuous enrollment and no death or hospice indication.
Non-infused DMT fills At least two fills totaling 56 or more days of supply from the PQA medication list.
PDC measured annually Each patient had a 365-day follow-up period from index and a second follow-up year.
Three adherence cohorts Consistently adherent, adherent in one year, or non-adherent in both years.
Adjusted outcome models General linear and logistic models controlled demographics, geography, DMT switching, mail order, insurance change, and associated therapy classes.

Adherent both years 60.6%

Patients maintained PDC ≥ 80% across both follow-up years.

Mixed adherence 26.5%

Patients met the adherence threshold in only one of the two years.

Non-adherent both years 12.9%

Patients did not reach PDC ≥ 80% in either year.

Adherence fell in year 2: percent adherent dropped from 80.6% to 67.0% while PDC decreased by 7.3% (p<0.0001).

Conclusion

Adherence delivered a hospital-utilization signal

DMT medication adherence using the PQA PDC ≥ 80% threshold was associated with lower hospitalization costs, fewer admissions, and shorter stays over 2 years compared with non-adherent cohorts, even though pharmacy costs were higher.
