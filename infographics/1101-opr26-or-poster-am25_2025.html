Effects of HIV Mode of Transmission on Medication Adherence in Children and Youth Using a Specialty Pharmacy

A single-center St. Jude analysis asked whether vertically versus horizontally acquired HIV changed oral antiretroviral therapy adherence after patients moved into specialty pharmacy services.

Main signal Adherence improved, but virologic response split by transmission route.

Both groups had higher mean PDC after specialty pharmacy enrollment. The horizontal group showed lower mean viral loads, while the vertical group had persistently higher viral loads despite higher PDC.

Objective

Evaluate the impact of HIV mode of transmission and specialty pharmacy use on oral ART adherence among patients under 24 years of age.

The poster frames adherence as proportion of days covered, then pairs that measure with routine HIV viral load monitoring.

PDC% = total days ART available / total study days x 100

Transmission groups

Vertical

  • In utero
  • During delivery
  • Breastmilk exposure

Horizontal

  • Injection drug use or rare infected blood products
  • Unprotected sexual intercourse with an infected partner

1. Cohort defined

Single-center retrospective analysis of patients age 2 to 23 years living with HIV.

2. Baseline captured

Pre-transition adherence and viral load were measured before specialty pharmacy enrollment.

3. Pharmacy transition

All patients moved into specialty pharmacy services in July 2020.

4. Follow-up compared

PDC and viral loads were compared across the July 2019 to July 2021 window.

5. Clinical pattern

Mode of transmission appeared to modify the relationship between adherence and viral suppression.

Conclusion Specialty pharmacy may support ART adherence, but vertically acquired HIV may involve more complex treatment challenges.

Adherence implication

Children and youth living with HIV may adhere better to oral ART when using a specialty pharmacy compared with a traditional outpatient pharmacy.

Clinical implication

Higher viral loads among patients with vertically acquired HIV, despite higher adherence, may reflect earlier ART exposure or other clinical factors that complicate response.
