NASP 2022 poster 49-2022 | Hepatitis C treatment outcomes

Outcomes in patients referred to a pharmacist-managed hepatitis C clinic

St. Elizabeth Healthcare evaluated whether a pharmacist-run Medication Management Clinic paired with a Specialty Pharmacy could improve hepatitis C treatment completion and sustained virologic response among referred patients.

Among patients who returned for sustained virologic response testing, nearly all had undetectable viral load. The operational opportunity is the large group that finished treatment but never completed SVR labs.

Integrated care model

Referral moved through a pharmacist clinic and specialty pharmacy access workflow.

Provider referral

Gastroenterology or infectious disease provider referred eligible adults under collaborative care.

Initial MMC visit

Pharmacists assessed drug interactions, education, immunizations, labs, and therapy orders.

Specialty access

Pharmacy team managed prior authorization, appeals, funding support, and dispensing.

Follow-up

Four-week clinic follow-up addressed viral load, liver function, adherence, and side effects.

SVR assessment

Provider follow-up 12 weeks after treatment assessed cure through SVR laboratory testing.

Follow-up gap

SVR lab capture was the main leak in the care cascade.

41%
  • 419 completers obtained SVR testing after treatment.
  • 289 completers did not obtain SVR labs.

The poster's future-impact recommendation was to add a 12-week post-treatment visit with the pharmacist-run clinic instead of relying only on provider office follow-up.

Objective and interpretation

Testing whether the clinic-pharmacy partnership improved cure and completion.

Objective: Determine whether combining a pharmacist-managed hepatitis C clinic with a specialty pharmacy improves treatment cure rates compared with other studies.

Study design: Retrospective observational review of referred adults from October 1, 2018 through September 30, 2021, summarized with descriptive statistics.

Conclusion: The clinic and specialty pharmacy model helped improve SVR and completion rates relative to literature benchmarks, while lab follow-up remained a major improvement target.

Background

DAA therapy is preferred for HCV, but cost, payer rules, access barriers, and loss to follow-up can interrupt care.

Model

Specialty pharmacists and technicians mitigated treatment barriers while MMC pharmacists managed clinical follow-up.

Completion

Treatment completion reached 95% among patients who initiated DAA therapy.

Cure

SVR was documented in 99% of patients who completed post-treatment laboratory testing.

Next step

Clinic-based 12-week post-treatment follow-up could improve SVR lab completion.

Bottom line

The integrated clinic-pharmacy pathway performed well when patients stayed connected.

The poster's central story is a high-performing treatment pathway with a documentation bottleneck at the SVR lab step. Completion and cure among tested patients exceeded the cited real-world ranges, but more than two in five treatment completers lacked confirmatory SVR labs.
