NASP 2019 | Cone Health Specialty Pharmacy

Impact of Ambulatory Clinical Pharmacist Interventions on Outcomes in the HIV Population

A retrospective review followed uncontrolled HIV patients seen by clinical pharmacy practitioners at Cone Health's Regional Center for Infectious Diseases, asking whether an integrated clinic and specialty pharmacy model could move patients toward viral suppression.

Methods as evidence flow

From uncontrolled viral load to post-intervention assessment

Identify uncontrolled patients

Eligible patients had at least one HIV RNA viral load above 20 copies/mL before the pharmacy practitioner visit.

Document CPP encounter

The cohort required at least one documented visit with a Cone Health clinical pharmacy practitioner.

Measure after the visit

Each patient needed at least one viral load collected after the CPP encounter during the study period.

Classify outcomes

Primary success was undetectable viral load; secondary measures captured intervention type and pharmacy capture.

Patients coinfected with hepatitis C were excluded from the analysis.

Primary outcome

Nearly one-third of a difficult-to-treat cohort reached undetectable viral load

Primary endpoint: 31.4% achieved HIV RNA below 20 copies/mL after pharmacist intervention; 68.6% remained detectable during the study window.

Because the cohort began with uncontrolled HIV, the result is best read as a focused service outcome among higher-need patients rather than a clinic-wide suppression estimate.

The poster also reported a 97.2% adherence rate and a median 157 days to undetectable viral load among patients who reached suppression.

Conclusion

Integrated pharmacy support targeted the patients who needed more than routine counseling

Clinical pharmacy practitioners cared for a large share of the clinic's difficult-to-treat patients, and the integrated approach with a health-system specialty pharmacy supported viral suppression work through access, medication optimization, adherence support, and clinical follow-up.
