Persistence to HIV PrEP Filled Through an Integrated Health-System Specialty Pharmacy Compared With External Pharmacies

In a single-center retrospective cohort from the Vanderbilt PrEP Clinic, patients whose PrEP prescriptions were filled through the integrated health-system specialty pharmacy remained on therapy longer than patients filling at external pharmacies.

Objective

Measure whether the pharmacy model changed persistence

Clinical question

Persistence to HIV pre-exposure prophylaxis during periods of increased HIV acquisition risk is important to preventing new HIV acquisitions. The study compared persistence when emtricitabine-tenofovir disoproxil fumarate prescriptions were filled by an integrated health-system specialty pharmacy versus external pharmacies.

Primary endpoint

Time from first prescription generated to patient-reported discontinuation or last prescription generated plus day supply.

Methods snapshot

Single-center retrospective cohort with prescription-based follow-up

Design Retrospective cohort comparing integrated HSSP fills with external pharmacy fills.
Sample Adults initiating PrEP with emtricitabine-tenofovir disoproxil fumarate.
Period Enrollment from September 1, 2016 to March 31, 2019.
Outcomes Persistence, proportion of days covered, and reasons for non-persistence.

Integrated HSSP model

The intervention was a coordinated service layer around PrEP access and monitoring

Conclusion

Integrated specialty pharmacy support was associated with higher PrEP persistence

Patients receiving PrEP in a multidisciplinary clinic were better maintained on therapy when prescriptions were filled through the integrated health-system specialty pharmacy rather than external pharmacies.

The poster's pattern suggests the service bundle around access, refill coordination, monitoring, and education may be the differentiator when adherence among active users is already high.
