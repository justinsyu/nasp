Pharmacist Impact in a Multiple Sclerosis Clinic

A New York health system-based MS center compared medication access before and after integrating an ambulatory care pharmacist into prior authorization, appeal, counseling, and clinic-support workflows.

Evidence flow

Embedding the ACP created a clearer path from medication request to access resolution.

Clinic flags need

Clinic staff notified the hospital-based pharmacy team that a PA was needed.

Criteria check

Eligible MS and related medication requests moved into pharmacy-led PA submission.

Approval route

When approved, the team notified patients where they could fill per insurance.

Appeal route

When denied, the ACP formulated appeals and conducted peer-to-peer support as needed.

Objective

Measure access impact beyond a single turnaround metric.

The poster evaluated PA turnaround time, PA approval rates, appeal approval rates, and clinic satisfaction after introducing an ambulatory care pharmacist into MS medication access work.

  • Population included patients prescribed DMTs and selected neurologic or related agents.
  • Study design compared pre-implementation and post-implementation time periods.
  • Clinic staff survey data captured perceived value of pharmacist services.

Conclusion

The ACP role was associated with better operational and clinic-facing outcomes.

The authors concluded that ACP incorporation improved PA turnaround, increased PA and appeal approvals, and created high clinic satisfaction, with expansion opportunities in clinic initiatives, documentation visibility, drug information, and teach appointments.
