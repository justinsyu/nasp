Health System Specialty Pharmacy Integration Impact on Fill Time

Parkview Health and Trellis Rx evaluated whether integrating a health system specialty pharmacy into a genitourinary oncology clinic shortened the business-day interval from a new oral oncolytic order to patient receipt.

Objective and methods

Define access as new order to patient receipt, then compare operational eras.

The poster's objective was to compare time to access oral oncolytic treatments before and after health system specialty pharmacy integration. Access time was defined as the difference in business days between a new order and the patient's receipt of the ordered product.

Included

  • Renal cell carcinoma or prostate cancer diagnosis code.
  • Associated prescription for one of 12 listed genitourinary oral anticancer agents.
  • First prescription order between November 2018 and October 202- as shown in the extracted poster.

Excluded

  • Patients under 18 years old.
  • Patients actively breastfeeding.
  • Patients incarcerated.

Conclusion

The access-time conclusion was directional and operational.

Integration of a health system specialty pharmacy resulted in a 15% reduction in patient access time, demonstrating the impact HSSP can play in avoiding treatment delays.

  • The internal-fill analysis produced the larger effect: 43% shorter median access time.
  • The post-integration distribution lowered Q1, Q3, mean, median, and minimum values.
  • Outside specialty pharmacy fills carried a longer median and multiple high outliers.

Poster context

Why time-to-fill mattered in this study.

The background framed oral oncolytic access as a source of patient and prescriber anxiety, citing prior median access-time estimates ranging from four to twelve days. The study positioned HSSPs as a way to overcome prior authorization, financial toxicity, pharmacy review, and delivery barriers.
