Evidence-based ulcerative colitis assessment can fit specialty pharmacy workflows

A targeted review, expert council, and 16-system database analysis identified a practical dashboard set for UC monitoring, then showed a large gap between available evidence and real-world structured assessment capture.

Three-step evidence flow

1

Targeted literature review

PubMed evidence from the prior 5 years on UC assessment, monitoring, and treatment outcomes.

2

Evidence Council

Five specialty pharmacy and IBD subject matter experts reviewed findings in a 2-hour virtual session.

3

Database prevalence analysis

Recommended parameters were checked in de-identified TSOC data from 16 health systems.

33 Mayo score reports

Mayo score or variations were the most frequently reported disease activity tools.

28 Endoscopic subscore reports

Mayo endoscopic subscore was the common marker for endoscopic improvement or healing.

13 + 13 Biomarker reports

Fecal calprotectin and C-reactive protein were each reported in 13 publications.

Recommended dashboard domains

From symptom capture to objective monitoring

The Evidence Council described high variability in UC assessment and recommended simple, standardized dashboard inputs that specialty pharmacies could use to support treat-to-target care.

Clinical activity Modified Mayo score, PRO2, rectal bleeding, and stool frequency.
Objective markers Fecal calprotectin and C-reactive protein.
Resource use Inpatient and emergency department visits.
Treatment burden Inpatient and outpatient steroid use.

Conclusion

Specialty pharmacies can help close the UC monitoring gap.

Evidence-based approaches for UC disease assessment and treat-to-target care are available, but inconsistent adoption limits outcome analyses. A disease-specific assessment dashboard could standardize PRO, quality-of-life, biomarker, utilization, and steroid-use capture.

Implementation signal

Standardize first. Council members prioritized consistency across clinical practice sites.
Keep tools simple. Ease of implementation into electronic health records was identified as a real-world barrier.
Make pharmacy data actionable. Specialty pharmacies are positioned to collect PRO and quality-of-life data needed for treatment decisions.

Method details used for extraction

TSOC data were drawn from electronic health records and pharmacy dispensing software as of January 3, 2025.

Patients with UC had an inpatient or outpatient encounter with primary diagnosis code K51.

Disease assessments from clinical notes were extracted using regular expressions for specific text patterns.
