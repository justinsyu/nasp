NASP 2021 / Poster 07

Nonadherence phenotype tool improves outcomes

Optum Frontier Therapies piloted an 18-question phenotype assessment to identify why rare-disease patients on orphan drugs were not taking therapy as prescribed, then matched each patient to targeted pharmacist and coaching interventions.

Objective

Turn nonadherence from a general problem into an addressable phenotype.

The poster describes a pilot tool designed to screen for common nonadherence patterns and make it easier for coaches and pharmacists to select corrective interventions for rare and complex therapy patients.

Methods

The pilot linked screening, phenotype assignment, intervention selection, and MPR follow-up.

Search evidence

The team reviewed literature for common nonadherence phenotypes that could guide intervention selection.

Screen patient

An 18-question tool incorporated Morisky Medication Adherence Scale and Readiness Assessment Ruler elements.

Assign phenotype

Patient-specific barriers were identified, including access, cost, regimen complexity, and readiness patterns.

Match support

Coaches and pharmacists used the Accurate Adherence Accelerator intervention assessment tool.

Measure outcome

MPR was measured for 12 months before screening and again after a 12-month intervention.

Problem defined

Medication nonadherence carries a large cost burden and contributes to preventable hospitalizations.

Phenotypes screened

The assessment looked for patient-specific patterns rather than assuming a single reason for nonadherence.

Interventions matched

MPR improved

Next step

The authors planned continued monitoring and evaluation with each new rare and complex therapy.

Results

The pilot showed improvement across adherence, hospitalization, and satisfaction signals.

  • Ten patients on orphan drugs participated for 12 months.
  • No patients were admitted for an unplanned hospitalization during the program.
  • All patients gave a 5 out of 5 satisfaction rating.

Interpretation

Promising signal, small pilot.

The outcome pattern supports continued use and evaluation, but the poster's sample size was limited to 10 patients and did not include a control group in the extracted methods.

Conclusion: an 18-question nonadherence phenotype tool helped pharmacists and coaches individualize support for rare-therapy patients, producing an 11.6% group adherence increase in the initial 10-patient pilot.
