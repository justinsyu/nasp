Correlates and barriers to medication adherence in multiple sclerosis patients and their impact on clinical outcomes

Yale New Haven Health evaluated whether medication possession ratio for oral or injectable disease-modifying therapies tracked with patient factors and MS-related clinical outcomes in a specialty pharmacy population.

Objective

Find patient-level adherence signals before outcomes worsen

Investigators examined the correlation between medication adherence and clinical outcomes, while also identifying potential barriers among MS patients receiving disease-modifying therapy prescriptions from the Yale New Haven Health outpatient specialty pharmacy.

Methods

Data moved from specialty pharmacy touchpoints to correlation testing

Define cohort

Adult MS patients filling oral or injectable DMT prescriptions at YNHHS OPS in Hamden, Connecticut.

Measure MPR

Retrospective medication possession ratios were calculated from pharmacy dispensing records.

Capture context

Pharmacist semi-annual follow-ups contributed subjective adherence and outcomes data.

Link outcomes

Hospital admissions and ED visits were obtained from the system-wide electronic health record.

Test association

Chi-squared and t-tests compared nominal and continuous variables in RStudio.

Table pattern extraction

What the poster tables imply at a glance

BMI

The only patient-characteristic difference flagged in the MPR <90% versus MPR ≥90% table was BMI (p=0.05).

Not linked

Age, gender, race, smoking, alcohol use, dose frequency, route, side effects, insurance, copay assistance, and patient copay were not significantly correlated with adherence.

PRO gap

No significant correlation was found between adherence and patient-reported missed school, work, planned events, or healthcare visits.

Conclusion

Adherence, mental health, and utilization travel together in the low-MPR tail

  • Nonadherent DMT patients were associated with more hospital admissions or ED visits due to MS symptoms documented in the EHR.
  • The authors emphasized mental health because increasing depressive symptoms correlated with decreasing medication adherence.
  • Medication frequency, route, cost, and individual patient factors may still matter clinically, but were not correlated with adherence in this cohort.

Limits and next step

Interpret as a single-center signal

The study was retrospective and single center, included a small population, and captured ED/hospital admissions only within the Yale New Haven Health database.

Future work proposed by the authors: conduct additional initiatives to improve adherence among MS patients receiving DMTs.
