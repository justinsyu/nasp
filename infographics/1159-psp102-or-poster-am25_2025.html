Wilson Disease: Where Hepatology and Neurology Collide

A patient case showing how rare disease education can connect low ceruloplasmin, copper abnormalities, hepatic fibrosis, movement disorder, altered mental status, and ocular signs into one diagnostic frame.

Core question
How can cross-specialty signals speed recognition?
Hepatic pattern Transaminitis, low ceruloplasmin, high copper, fibrosis, biopsy support.
Neurologic pattern Gait disturbance, tremors, altered mental status, MRI findings, ICU escalation.

Objective, methods, results, conclusion

Objective: Use a complex Wilson disease presentation to demonstrate why rare disease education should bridge hepatology and neurology.

Methods: Case timeline abstraction from November 2023 through April 2024, organized into parallel hepatology and neurology tracks.

Results: Diagnostic evidence accumulated across specialties: ceruloplasmin of 6 and 8, 24-hour urine copper of 320, serum copper of 35, F3 fibrosis, ocular rings, and biopsy consistency with Wilson disease.

Conclusion: Earlier recognition depends on treating hepatic, neurologic, and ocular clues as a connected pattern rather than separate workups.

Two-track evidence flow

Hepatology and neurology evolved in parallel

Month Hepatology track Neurology track
Nov 2023
Initial hepatic abnormality Transaminitis and very low ceruloplasmin appeared in a 32-year-old patient with tremors and elevated liver enzymes.
Movement disorder presentation Gait disturbance, tremors, impaired balance, shuffled gait, and broad MRI considerations started the neurologic track.
Dec 2023
Chronic liver signal Abdominal imaging suggested chronic liver disease, gallbladder sludge, possible shock liver, and F3 fibrosis.
Parkinsonism considered Lumbar puncture, Sinemet initiation, long-term care discharge, and worsening mental status marked ongoing uncertainty.
Jan 2024
Wilson disease moved front and center Copper testing, biopsy, trientine start, PEG placement, and biopsy consistency with Wilson disease clarified the diagnosis.
Clinical decline escalated Worsening mental status and neurologic decline led to transplant facility recommendation and ICU transfer for intubation risk.
Feb 2024
Treatment logistics mattered Transplant facility acceptance, formulary limits, ocular rings, and changes between trientine and D-penicillamine shaped care.
PLEX repeated Plasmapheresis was administered six times between February 6 and February 15.
Apr 2024
Step-down discharges The patient discharged from transplant facility to outside facility, then to long-term care facility.
Slow neurologic recovery Mental status slowly improved, but the patient remained unable to make medical decisions.
