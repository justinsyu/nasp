A Comparison of Viral Suppression Rates of Patients Receiving Long-Acting Injectable ART Versus Oral Therapy

A retrospective chart review compared cabotegravir/rilpivirine long-acting injectable ART with oral ART across Maxor National Pharmacy Services facilities, focusing on viral suppression, CD4 count, quality of life, and adherence signals.

Primary question

Does delivery route track with suppression? Objective: determine undetectable viral load rates and CD4 count at 60 days, with secondary assessment at 180 and 365 days plus adherence and patient-reported measures.

Results signal

Viral suppression favored the injectable cohort at both reported follow-up points.

Evidence flow

How the chart review was assembled

1

Network source

Data came from applicable Maxor National Pharmacy Services facilities in a corporation managing more than 60 pharmacies nationally.

2

Eligibility frame

Adults with a first documented dispense of long-acting injectable or oral therapy in the internal medical record were considered.

3

Comparison design

Patients were randomly selected for a one-to-one cohort ratio, with a target of up to 50 patients per cohort.

4

Descriptive analysis

Viral load, CD4 count, adherence, depression, quality-of-life, labs, insurance, and related chart variables were summarized descriptively.

Therapy exposure

Injectable ART patients received cabotegravir/rilpivirine; oral ART was primarily bictegravir/emtricitabine/tenofovir alafenamide.

Quality of life

Average quality-of-life ratings were 9 in both groups at baseline, 60 days, and 180 days where reported.

Depression screen

PHQ-2 averages were 0 where available; 60-day PHQ-2 scores were not available in the poster table.

Adherence signal

MMAS-4 averages were similar, but reported missed doses were higher with oral therapy through 180 days.

Study limits

Requested lab dates were not always available, and the planned target of 50 patients per cohort was not reached.

Conclusion

Concise interpretation

  • Long-acting injectable ART showed higher viral suppression at 60 days and 180 days in this descriptive cohort comparison.
  • CD4 averages declined by 180 days in both cohorts but remained above 200 cells/mm3.
  • Oral therapy patients reported more missed doses within 180 days, despite similar MMAS-4 averages.

Clinical context

Why this poster matters

The authors framed the work around identifying opportunities to optimize HIV care, assess quality of life, and evaluate cost-effectiveness considerations for cabotegravir/rilpivirine versus oral ART.
