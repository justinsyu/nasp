Access and monitoring workflow

Pharmacists connected benefit access, lab surveillance, and right-sized refill quantities.

Referral and access

After treatment referral, pharmacists completed chart review, benefits investigation, prior authorization, appeals, and financial assistance when needed.

Initial delivery

Patients received counseling and an initial 28-day supply of 112 tablets once access was secured.

CMV monitoring

Before refills, pharmacists reviewed labs and clinical context until two undetectable CMV levels at least 5 days apart or transplant-team discontinuation.

Waste control

When lab timing and viral load supported it, pharmacists arranged the smallest refill quantity needed rather than defaulting to another 28-day supply.

Objective, methods, conclusion

The study frames pharmacists as access stewards and waste-reduction partners.

  • Objective: Evaluate the specialty pharmacist role in maribavir access, treatment monitoring, and medication-waste reduction.
  • Methods: Single-center retrospective cohort using electronic medical record and specialty pharmacy management system data.
  • Population: Post-transplant CMV infection or disease refractory to prior ganciclovir, valganciclovir, cidofovir, or foscarnet treatment, with or without resistance.
  • Conclusion: Pharmacists helped secure timely access and used CMV monitoring to tailor refill quantities, avoiding unnecessary high-cost medication fills.

Care gap

Maribavir is a high-cost, limited-distribution therapy requiring frequent CMV lab monitoring.

Access work

Pharmacists handled benefits investigation, prior authorization, appeals, and financial assistance.

Monitoring trigger

Refill alerts prompted chart review for updated labs and clinical information before continued treatment.

Right-sized fills

Dispense quantities were reduced when CMV level and timing to next lab made smaller fills clinically appropriate.

Value signal

Ten final-course reductions produced $119,517 to $149,397 in estimated avoided cost.
