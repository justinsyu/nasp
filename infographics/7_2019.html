Management of Specialty Drugs, Specialty Pharmacies, and Biosimilars in the United States

An online survey of U.S. medical and pharmacy directors explored how plans manage specialty products, specialty pharmacy networks, biosimilar coverage, member education, and expected savings.

Objective

What did payor leaders expect to change?

The poster sought a current read on how U.S. plans manage specialty pharmacy products and biosimilars, then compared those views with earlier surveys to identify shifts in coverage, co-pays, savings expectations, and education strategies.

Specialty spend pressure

Specialty pharmaceuticals reached 44.5% of non-discounted spending, up from 31.5% in 2013.

Network restriction softened

Reported specialty pharmacy restrictions fell to 58% of plans, compared with 81% the prior year.

Co-pay logic shifted

Plans increasingly used group and benefit design to determine specialty product co-pays.

Biosimilar policies varied

Most expected discounted co-pays, while others varied by approval timing or restricted indications.

Education remained central

Prescriber mailings, differential co-pays, and patient mailings were the leading education tactics.

Conclusion

Policy design was adapting to specialty-drug growth

Health plans anticipated continued growth in specialty pharmacy expenditures, with biosimilars expected to provide some relief.

Medical and pharmacy directors described evolving policies around benefit design, specialty pharmacy products, and biosimilars to balance access and cost.

The practical work ahead centered on formulary management, patient and prescriber education, and operational readiness for biosimilar substitution.
