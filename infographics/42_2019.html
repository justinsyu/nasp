Emergent DMTs Were Associated With Lower MS-Related Utilization in Managed Medicaid

A retrospective claims analysis compared treatment-naive adults with multiple sclerosis who initiated an established or emergent disease-modifying therapy, then tracked relapses and MS-related hospitalizations for 1 year.

Objective

Compare MS-related healthcare use after DMT initiation

The poster asked whether managed Medicaid patients diagnosed with MS had different 1-year rates of MS-related hospitalizations and relapse events after initiating emergent versus established DMTs.

  • Source: Inovalon MORE2 Registry administrative claims database.
  • Eligibility: adults enrolled in Managed Medicaid with MS diagnosis evidence and continuous medical and pharmacy coverage.
  • Exposure: first established or emergent DMT fill between May 2013 and June 2016, excluding DMT polytherapy.
  • Adjustment: regression models controlled for age, gender, Charlson index, and geographic region.

Study design

Baseline control, index DMT, 12-month outcome window

Adjusted results

Emergent DMT users had lower outpatient relapse and hospitalization rates in preferred models

0.87 Outpatient relapse events

95% CI 0.80-0.96; p = 0.006 in the zero-inflated negative binomial model.

0.74 MS-related hospitalizations

95% CI 0.55-0.99; p = 0.048 in the zero-inflated negative binomial model.

0.79 Inpatient relapse events

95% CI 0.61-1.00; p = 0.055 in Poisson models, described as non-differential.

Relative risk below 1.00 favors emergent DMTs; established DMTs were the reference group.

Evidence gap

Trials suggested emergent DMT advantages, but real-world comparative data in managed Medicaid were limited.

Eligibility lens

Adults needed MS diagnosis claims, managed Medicaid enrollment, and continuous medical and pharmacy coverage.

Outcome capture

The analysis counted MS-related hospitalizations plus outpatient and inpatient relapse events during follow-up.

Statistical lens

Models adjusted for age, gender, Charlson index, and region, then tested zero-inflated specifications.

Interpretation

Emergent DMTs were associated with fewer hospitalizations and outpatient relapses within 1 year.

Conclusion

Lower utilization signal, not a randomized comparison

The authors concluded that emergent DMT users had significantly lower rates of MS-related hospitalizations and outpatient relapse events than established DMT users in the year after initiation.

Inpatient relapse events were non-differential, and the claims-based design means the signal is associative after adjustment rather than causal.
