NASP 2024 | OPR46-RP | Oncology specialty pharmacy

Evaluation of pharmacist-driven clinical interventions in oncology and hematology patients

West Virginia University Medicine Specialty Pharmacy reviewed oncology patient-management activities to classify what specialty pharmacists documented, where documentation was incomplete, and how much time clinical work required.

Objective

Classify the clinical work

Primary objective: classify types of clinical activities performed by specialty pharmacists caring for patients with cancer. Secondary objective: quantify pharmacist time spent completing those activities.

Method

Retrospective documentation review

Included oncology PMP patients with recorded clinical activities and one or more classifications or reasons for intervention in Therigy STM between Jan. 1 and June 28, 2024.

Evidence flow

Clinical role

Specialty pharmacists were active across medication safety, adherence, education, and care coordination for oncology patients.

Safety first

Safety was the most frequently documented drug-therapy reason, including toxicity management, interactions, lab review, and dose changes.

Coordination load

Non-financial care coordination nearly matched safety as a top documented reason for intervention.

Documentation gap

Incomplete activity documentation limited classification and became a major operational finding.

Next step

The poster recommends standardizing intervention classification and evaluating time by subclassification and outcomes.

Conclusion

The review makes specialty pharmacy work visible, but also exposes a documentation standardization need

Source conclusion: oncology specialty pharmacists completed 629 clinical activities in the study period; safety and non-financial care coordination were the most common documented reasons; median activity time was 15 minutes; capecitabine had the most associated activity among top medications; future work should refine classification and connect interventions to outcomes.
