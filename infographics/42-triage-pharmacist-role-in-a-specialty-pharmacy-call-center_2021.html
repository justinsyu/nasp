NASP 2021 | Poster 42 | Specialty pharmacy call center

Impact of a Triage Pharmacist Role on Clinical Intervention Activities in a Specialty Pharmacy Call Center

Wake Forest Baptist Medical Center evaluated whether assigning a triage pharmacist to the specialty pharmacy call center improved the speed of resolving pharmacist-escalated clinical interventions.

  • Triage pharmacist

2021
Call Center

Christine Barthen; Jennifer Young; Kathy Bricker; Helen Northrup; Kyle Hansen

A dedicated triage role shortened average clinical intervention completion from 3.3 to 2.2 days while volume increased from 391 to 1,130 interventions.

Post period

Completion time -1.1 days

Average time to complete an intervention decreased from 3.3 to 2.2 days (p=0.002).

First outreach -0.68 days

Time to first outreach fell from 0.93 to 0.25 days and stayed under one day (p<0.05).

Intervention volume 2.9x

Completed activities increased from 391 pre-triage to 1,130 post-triage.

Acceptance 89%

Clinical intervention acceptance remained essentially unchanged: 89.3% vs 89.1%.

Objective

Test whether role clarity improved clinical intervention flow.

Before the triage pharmacist role, pharmacist-escalated call center activities did not have a dedicated pharmacist owner. The study assessed completion speed, first outreach, therapeutic and intervention-category timing, follow-up plan creation, specialty pharmacy discontinuations, and resolution status.

Pre-triage

No dedicated triage pharmacist

Escalations reached pharmacists through protocol, but no single pharmacist role was assigned to address the clinical intervention queue.

Post-triage

Queue ownership and warm transfer path

Urgent issues were warm-transferred and non-urgent activities were worked by the triage pharmacist in the intervention queue.

Escalation protocol

A defined queue converted escalation into pharmacist-owned work.

Patient care activity

A technician identifies a concern requiring pharmacist escalation.

Clinical intervention

The technician adds a Clinical Intervention-Patient activity in TherigySTM.

Urgency split

Urgent cases route to immediate pharmacist support; non-urgent cases enter queue-based review.

Warm transfer

Urgent issues are transferred to the triage pharmacist or an available pharmacist.

Queue resolution

The triage pharmacist works the Clinical Intervention-Patient queue to address concerns.

Secondary signals

Acceptance stayed stable while workflow differences emerged.

Pre: 89.3% accepted. Post: 89.1% accepted. The difference was not significant (p=0.937).

Therapeutic category, clinical intervention category, and clinical outcome category timing differed significantly after role implementation.

  • Patient Care Plans remained similar after implementation (p=0.608).
  • Patients discontinued from specialty pharmacy services differed after implementation (p=0.004).
  • Clinical intervention categories included questions, therapy changes, therapy interruptions, medication questions, and non-adherence.

Conclusion

Delineating a pharmacist triage role within a specialty pharmacy call center supported a more efficient workflow for triaging and resolving clinical concerns.

Context

Why the role mattered

The call center launched in 2018, and the pharmacist triage role followed in 2019 to improve workflow by enabling timely resolution of clinical intervention activities identified by pharmacy staff.

Discussion

Operational implication

The role was a proactive response to increasing patient volumes. The authors noted that further review of discrete documentation fields in TherigySTM may streamline future reporting across multiple pharmacists serving in the triage role.
