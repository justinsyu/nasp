NASP 2021 abstract 61

Rates and reasons for medication switching and cycling in rheumatoid arthritis

At two Vanderbilt rheumatology clinics with integrated specialty pharmacists, investigators followed adult RA patients after bDMARD or tsDMARD initiation to quantify therapy changes, reasons for change, and predictors of first switch or cycle.

Objective

Measure how often RA patients changed advanced therapies, and why.

The poster assessed rates and reasons for switching and cycling in patients with rheumatoid arthritis treated in clinics with integrated specialty pharmacists.

Operational definitions

Two routes away from the first prescribed therapy

Switch

Change to a medication with a different mechanism of action.

Cycle

Change to a medication with the same mechanism of action.

Methods

Retrospective cohort from pharmacy fills to first therapy change

Adults with RA

Included patients had a rheumatoid arthritis diagnosis and were treated by one of two VUMC rheumatology clinics.

Eligible fills

Patients filled at least two eligible bDMARD or tsDMARD prescriptions through the study workflow.

Index windows

Clinic 1 index dates began July 1, 2013; Clinic 2 began January 2, 2015; both ended June 30, 2017.

Outcome capture

The primary outcome was rate and reason for switching or cycling bDMARDs or tsDMARDs.

Time-to-event

Cox proportional hazards and Kaplan-Meier methods assessed predictors and continuation on the first medication.

Main pattern

60%+

For both switch and cycle events, loss of efficacy plus no efficacy accounted for the majority of documented reasons.

Advanced RA therapy context

bDMARDs and tsDMARDs are standard-of-care options, but many patients change therapy within the first two years.

Integrated pharmacist setting

Clinic-embedded specialty pharmacists support access, adherence, education, and therapy-change navigation.

Observed change rate

Two hundred four patients had 216 switch events and 93 cycle events across follow-up.

Reason hierarchy

Loss of efficacy was the most common reason for both switches and cycles.

Predictor signal

Starting on an IL-6 inhibitor was associated with more than double the hazard of changing therapy compared with TNFi starts.

Conclusion: Many patients with RA changed bDMARD or tsDMARD therapy, most often because efficacy was inadequate or adverse effects were intolerable; integrated specialty pharmacists remain positioned to support education, mitigation, access, and decision-making when a switch or cycle is needed.
