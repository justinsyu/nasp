Barriers to Growth Hormone Access in Patients at an Academic Medical Center

Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy evaluated which patient, payer, and authorization factors shaped the time from treatment decision to human growth hormone insurance approval.

  • Retrospective cohort
  • Pediatric endocrinology
  • Integrated specialty pharmacy
  • Insurance access
Median initiation time 8

days despite prior authorization, appeals, testing, and patient assistance hurdles.

Study sample 234

Pediatric patients prescribed growth hormone for an FDA indication.

Median age 11

Years, with IQR 8 to 13.

Prior authorization 229

Patients required an initial authorization step.

Additional testing 9%

Required insurer-directed testing before approval.

Objective

Measure what slows approval after the decision to treat.

The study assessed factors affecting time to human growth hormone approval using an integrated specialty pharmacy model at Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy and Vanderbilt Health Systems.

  • Retrospective cohort analysis of January 2018 through December 2019 treatment starts.
  • Primary measure: days from treatment decision to insurance approval.
  • Analysis used descriptive statistics and Cox proportional hazard modeling.

Evidence Flow

The access pathway widened after the initial prior authorization decision.

1. Prior authorization
167 approved 63 denied

229 patients required prior authorization; 5 did not.

2. Appeal
30 approved 23 denied

Among 53 appeal cases, 40% required a second or third appeal level.

3. Patient assistance
30 approved 4 cash pay

Cash pay was decided only after all approval steps were exhausted.

Each added access step changed the time scale from days to months.

Barriers

The highest-friction cases were tied to coverage criteria and extra testing.

52% Prior authorizations denied because diagnosis was not covered.
43% Denied for not meeting criteria or formulary alternative requirements.
40% Appeal patients needing a second or third appeal level.

All patients initiated therapy. Integrated health-system specialty pharmacy support helped patients start treatment with a median initiation time of 8 days.

Conclusion

Access support worked, but idiopathic short stature and additional testing signaled longer approval times.

Despite multiple barriers to growth hormone approval, the health-system specialty pharmacy model moved patients through authorization, appeal, assistance, and cash-pay decisions. The authors identified patients with idiopathic short stature and patients whose insurers required additional testing as groups likely to experience longer access times.
