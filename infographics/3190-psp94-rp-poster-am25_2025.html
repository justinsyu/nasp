Methods and implementation evidence

Four implementation phases converted interest into an injection visit

Exploration

Oct 2024-Jan 2025

Reviewed needs, alternatives, champions, California pharmacy authorities, legal interpretation, internal priorities, revenue streams, and clinic operating model.

Preparation

Jan-May 2025

Held weekly meetings, built SOPs and detailed workflow, configured Epic referrals and visit types, trained pharmacists, purchased supplies, and secured approvals.

Implementation

June 2025 onward

Adapted medication acquisition, shifted care space through urgent care partnership, separated screening from injection visits, and administered the first patient dose.

Sustainment

June 2025 onward

Continued pharmacist confidence-building, supply ordering routines, revenue-cycle monitoring, and volume/capacity planning as interest and new medicines grow.

Results pattern

The pilot exposed operational dependencies before scale-up

  • Clinic-administered medication procurement differed from routine pharmacy acquisition.
  • Patients with BMI above 30 required 2-inch needles, which were not part of usual supply assumptions.
  • EMR privileges differed for staff pharmacists versus ambulatory care pharmacists, affecting facility fee billing.
  • Maintenance-dose patients needed a new pharmacist-led order to operate fully under SB 159/339.
  • Three-site STI testing required separate supply planning because the lab did not provide kits.
  • Post-administration billing required clarification during pilot operations.

Conclusion and lessons

A pharmacy-led injection clinic can expand access, but the build is cross-functional

Start with infrastructure, not only clinical protocol.

Referral routing, visit types, privileges, smartphrases, scheduling, medication acquisition, and supplies all shaped whether pharmacists could deliver the service.

Interpretation of pharmacy authority matters locally.

Organization-specific legal and operational interpretation of SB 159/339 was favorable for applying pharmacist PrEP authority to injectables.

Slow pilots make hidden dependencies visible.

Low initial volume helped staff test workflow, coaching, billing, and supply assumptions, though revenue patterns will take longer to mature.

Training must continue after go-live.

Ongoing practice, SME support, and debriefing were needed to build pharmacist confidence with provider-administered LA-PrEP.

Clinic burden can shift without fragmenting care.

A pharmacy clinic inside a community pharmacy offers a pathway for clinic-administered medications while supporting access across sites.

Teamwork was the core implementation technology.

The work depended on pharmacy leadership and staff, health IT, HIV clinic and primary care champions, legal input, urgent care partners, and subject matter experts.
