Primary result

The pharmacist CPA pathway compressed the wait from referral to therapy start.

The poster framed health system specialty pharmacy pharmacists as a bridge in the HCV care continuum, reducing delay while keeping prescribing, monitoring, access work, education, and dispensing connected.

Hepatitis C therapy access process

A referral can branch through GI/ID prescribing or the HSSP CPA, then reconverge through access services.

Referral date

HCV treatment need enters the care pathway and establishes the clock for access timing.

Prescriber route

Therapy is prescribed either by GI/ID or by the HSSP pharmacist under CPA authority.

Access work

Benefits investigation and financial assistance work are coordinated by the HSSP.

Education and dispense

Patients receive education and medication through onsite specialty pharmacy services.

Treatment start

The endpoint captures when therapy begins, not only when the prescription is written.

Methods

Single-center retrospective comparison across 2021 referrals.

Pharmacists used Arbor, a proprietary health system specialty pharmacy platform, and the electronic health record to collect prescriber status, referral date, and treatment start date.

  • Included adults over 18 receiving HCV treatment dispensed through the HSSP.
  • Excluded patients receiving only non-dispensing HCV services such as prior authorization support or monitoring.
  • Excluded health-system-provider prescriptions filled outside the HSSP medication channel.

Discussion and conclusion

HSSP CPAs can close a treatment-start gap for HCV patients.

The authors concluded that HSSP collaborative practice agreements play a pivotal role in decreasing HCV treatment start times, helping patients avoid longer waits that can follow referral to a gastroenterology appointment.

The poster also flags a key limitation: the evaluation was small, and larger follow-up studies are needed to isolate the unique impact of health system specialty pharmacies on HCV access to care.
