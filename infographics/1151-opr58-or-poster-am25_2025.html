Analysis of a Health System Specialty Pharmacy Model

A health system specialty pharmacy replaced prescription-count thinking with a task-time productivity model, pairing electronic medical record wrap-up tags with staff-estimated median minutes to benchmark clinical, operations, and shipping work.

Objective

What counts as productivity when work is not only dispensing?

The poster's objective was to develop and evaluate internal criteria for measuring pharmacist and technician productivity in specialty pharmacy work, including clinical, operational, and shipping tasks.

Model Visual

Evidence Flow

From staff knowledge to a productivity dashboard

1

Define the gap

Prescription volume missed productivity outside of dispensing.

Team members estimated average minutes needed for specialty pharmacy tasks.

3

Add missing tasks

A second survey captured additional activities such as 340B, training, referrals, and communication.

Wrap-up tags were paired with median time values and hierarchical combinations.

5

Classify work

Hours were grouped into clinical, operations, and shipping categories.

6

Benchmark staffing

One employee was benchmarked at a minimum of 160 productive hours per month.

Discussion

What the model solved

  • It gave the team a foundation for evaluating clinical, operations, and shipping productivity.
  • It made specialty-pharmacy-specific work visible beyond new and refill prescription counts.
  • It created a benchmark that can be trended to support staffing projections.

Conclusion

A useful benchmark, with room to mature

The model provided an internal productivity framework for a health system specialty pharmacy, but the authors noted the need for longer-term evaluation and better capture of clinical interventions, precepting, continuing education, conferences, and other care-supporting work.
