Evaluating Appeal Outcomes for Insurance Non-Preferred Dermatology Specialty Medications

Northwestern Medicine Specialty Pharmacy reviewed dermatology prescriptions that required at least one appeal after prior authorization denial for formulary preference. The appeal pathway overturned 22 of 41 denials, with most approvals occurring at the first appeal level.

Purpose

Measure whether persistence after a formulary denial changes access.

The study evaluated appeals submitted by NMSP for dermatology specialty medications not included on an insurer's formulary preference list.

Methods

System-wide retrospective observational review.

  • Identify dermatology specialty prescriptions submitted to NMSP from January 1 through August 1, 2024.
  • Include cases requiring at least one appeal after an initial PA denial tied to formulary preference.
  • Track appeal outcome, escalation level, decision timing, payer type, and patient fallback pathway.

Primary endpoint: appeals overturned after first, second, or external appeal for initially denied dermatology specialty medications.

Access barrier

Specialty dermatology medications may be denied when excluded from the insurer's formulary preference list.

Operational burden

Appeals require administrative follow-up and can delay patient care if the denial is not overturned.

Appeal effect

In this review, 22 of 41 initially denied cases were ultimately approved after appeal.

Residual barrier

Nineteen upheld cases needed alternate medications, assistance programs, or other follow-up pathways.

Practice signal

A structured and persistent appeal process can recover access for many patients despite formulary exclusions.

Conclusion

Appeals were an effective access strategy, but not a complete fix.

The appeal process overturned more than half of the PA denials in this cohort, and the poster notes that most approvals were granted after first-level appeal.

For patients whose appeals were upheld, fallback planning remained important: medication switches and patient assistance programs were the most defined routes.

Limitations

Interpret with small-sample and documentation constraints.

  • Retrospective study with small sample size (N = 41).
  • Differences in appeal letter documentation were not studied.
  • Technician notes were used to determine appeal dates when letters were missing in the electronic health record.
