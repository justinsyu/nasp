Evaluating patient-reported adherence and outcomes in specialty disease states

A dual-site health-system specialty pharmacy initiative examined whether monthly patient-reported refill assessments could connect missed doses, medication tolerability, perceived effectiveness, and emergency room or hospital use.

Methods

Two HSSPs converted routine refill outreach into a structured outcomes dataset.

Select clinics

Patients came from rheumatology or multiple sclerosis specialty clinics.

Apply fill rules

Included patients had at least three fills, excluding any individual refill above a 30-day supply.

Call monthly

Specialty pharmacy teams proactively completed clinical assessments before refilling medication.

Ask PRO questions

Questions covered missed doses, side effects or tolerance, perceived effectiveness, and ER or hospital use.

Objective

Examine associations between patient characteristics, adherence, tolerance, perceived effectiveness, and HCRU.

Setting

Two health-system specialty pharmacies used monthly refill assessments as the data source.

Main outcome

Most assessments reported no missed doses, no tolerability issues, favorable effectiveness, and no HCRU.

Risk signal

Tolerability issues and lower perceived effectiveness were associated with missed doses.

Practice implication

Routine PRO collection can flag patients who may benefit from targeted adherence or tolerability follow-up.

Conclusion: In rheumatology and MS specialty pharmacy patients, monthly refill assessments showed high reported effectiveness and low missed-dose, tolerability, and HCRU rates, while poorer tolerability or lower perceived effectiveness identified patients more likely to report missed doses.
