Evaluation of a Pharmacy Delivered Oncofertility Intervention

A descriptive retrospective review examined whether specialty pharmacy touchpoints could surface fertility preservation conversations for new-to-therapy oncology patients receiving gonadotoxic treatment before treatment starts.

Evidence flow

How the pharmacy intervention turned eligibility into a time-sensitive counseling prompt

Identify risk

Patients were new to oncology therapy, younger than 50, and receiving treatment with gonadotoxic potential.

Ask directly

The prompt introduced reproductive endocrinologists and asked whether fertility preservation had been discussed.

Capture response

Responses were grouped as Yes, No, or Not Necessary to quantify uptake and unmet counseling opportunity.

Route if needed

Patients answering No were directed to schedule a consult quickly, including virtual options through the Walgreens App.

Assess value

The poster frames pharmacy teams as an added resource for awareness, risk counseling, and financial assistance navigation.

377 patients said their oncologist had not discussed fertility preservation.
  • ASCO and NCCN recommend offering fertility preservation services before oncologic treatment begins.
  • The intervention was designed to preserve patient choice without delaying cancer treatment.
  • Future evaluation can measure understanding, engagement in reproductive risk counseling, outcomes, and satisfaction.

Objective

Measure utilization and awareness creation

The study aimed to understand use of the Connected Care Oncology Fertility Preservation intervention among eligible newly diagnosed or new-to-therapy cancer patients.

Future work will assess whether the intervention improves understanding of fertility preservation options, counseling engagement, patient outcomes, and satisfaction.

Conclusion

Pharmacy teams can reinforce fertility preservation counseling.

The findings support prior evidence that opportunities remain to counsel oncology patients on fertility preservation options.

Pharmacists may help identify eligible patients, educate on reproductive risk and medication effects, and connect patients to resources that reduce barriers.
