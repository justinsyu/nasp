NASP 2022 poster 10-2022 | Oral oncology adherence

Impact of COVID-19 on Oral Oncolytic Adherence

CVS Health assessed whether patients initiating oral oncolytics during the first COVID-19 year differed from pre-COVID initiators in optimal adherence, persistence, and digital platform use. The headline: COVID initiation itself had minimal adjusted impact, while the intensity of digital engagement separated adherence odds.

COVID study split

Initiation shifted, adherence barely moved.

Patients were split at March 1, 2020 and followed for one year. Post-COVID initiators had slightly higher unadjusted optimal adherence and more persistence signals, but COVID initiation was not associated with optimal adherence after adjustment.

Unadjusted difference: p = 0.036. Adjusted post-COVID initiation OR: 1.06 (95% CI 0.96-1.16).

Engagement ladder

The adjusted model was not monotonic until high use.

Engagement categories were defined by total interactions over follow-up: none, low (<28), moderate (28-105), and high (>105). Low and moderate engagement had lower odds than no engagement, while high engagement had higher odds.

Objective and conclusion

What the poster claims

Objective

Examine the impact of COVID-19 on adherence to oral oncolytic agents in a large health plan with a significant digital health platform.

Interpretation

  • COVID had minimal effect on oral oncolytic adherence after accounting for other factors.
  • Post-COVID initiators had fewer therapy changes and more fills, suggesting stronger persistence.
  • Digital engagement appears threshold-dependent: high engagement increased adherence odds, while low and moderate engagement did not.

Bottom line

COVID timing was a small signal; engagement intensity was the sharper signal.

In this large oral oncology cohort, pre/post COVID initiation groups had nearly identical mean MPR and similar digital enrollment. The practical finding is more nuanced than "digital is better": high digital interaction volume was associated with greater adjusted adherence odds, while low and moderate engagement were associated with lower odds than no engagement.
