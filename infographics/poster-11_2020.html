NASP 2020 Poster 11

Evaluating prescription outcomes for specialty agents used to treat dermatologic conditions

A Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy quality improvement initiative mapped how dermatology prescriptions moved through prior authorization, what clinical documentation supported access, and where pharmacist clarification was still needed.

Objective

Evaluate prescription outcomes for specialty medications and quantify time to insurance approval.

Methods

Retrospective cohort of specialty agent-naive adults prescribed specialty therapy in Vanderbilt outpatient dermatology.

Cohort

Mean age was 55 years; 57% were female, 86% Caucasian, and 64% had psoriasis.

Process

Benefits investigation, prior authorization, appeals, education, monitoring, and affordability support were pharmacist touchpoints.

Interpretation

High access was achieved, but inconsistent objective documentation created additional clarification work.

Clarification mix

More than half of prescriptions needed additional clarification before PA completion.

7 Medication prescription clarifications
5 Prior treatment clarifications
3 Clinical marker clarifications

Median time to approval was 11 days when clarification was required versus 7 days when it was not; reported P=0.65.

Pharmacist role

The poster frames access as a clinical, operational, and patient-facing workflow.

Access and affordability

Secure insurance approval, route prescriptions, coordinate outside pharmacies, and support financial assistance.

Education

Counsel patients and inform prescribers about specialty medication options for therapy selection.

Monitoring

Review labs, assessment findings, comorbidities, other medications, adherence, efficacy, and safety.

Cohort pattern

Psoriasis and biologic agents dominated the sample.

Domain Most frequent categories
Diagnosis Psoriasis 18 (64%); atopic dermatitis 5 (18%); hidradenitis suppurativa 5 (18%)
Medication Adalimumab 13 (46%); dupilumab 5 (18%); ustekinumab 5 (18%)
Insurance Commercial 18 (64%); Medicare 10 (36%)

Conclusion

Provider education was the next step.

Pharmacist-driven prior authorization management can achieve a high specialty medication access rate in dermatology.

However, less than half of patients had documented body surface area or disease severity, and variable documentation delayed access by creating provider clarification loops.

The authors recommended education on insurance approval documentation elements to improve prospective clinical data capture.
