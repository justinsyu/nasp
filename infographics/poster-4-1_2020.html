NASP 2020 / Poster 4-1

Assessing reasons for primary medication non-adherence in oncology specialty medications

Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy reviewed specialty oral oncolytic prescriptions to test whether a claims-style primary medication non-adherence definition reflected true patient-level non-initiation after clinical review.

Objective

Identify why adult patients prescribed specialty oral oncolytics appeared not to start therapy.

The study tested whether PMN status based on prescription and fill timing accurately represented true primary medication non-adherence, then categorized the reasons behind both misidentified and true PMN cases.

Algorithm structure

PMN was first defined mechanically, using timing windows around the index prescription.

180d Lookback window Minimum period before the index prescription in which a patient could revert to naive status.
2d Duplicate window Time in which two sequential prescriptions could be considered duplicate orders.
30d Fill window Duration in which an eligible prescription needed a fill to avoid PMN status.
PMN Eligibility rule No oncology fill in the lookback window, no duplicate within two days, and no external SP reroute within two days.

Define PMN

Use lookback, duplicate, and fill windows to classify PMN-eligible prescriptions.

Identify signal

The algorithm flagged 198 of 1,004 eligible prescriptions as potential PMN.

Review context

Clinical review separated true non-initiation from reroutes, manufacturer fills, duplicates, and other explanations.

Confirm true cases

Only 37 prescriptions represented true PMN after adjudication.

Reframe metric

Raw PMN alone is a limited quality metric for specialty pharmacies.

Methods

A single-center cohort focused on adult oncology prescriptions sent to VSP in 2018.

  • Prescribed by a Vanderbilt University Medical Center provider.
  • Prescribed to an adult patient.
  • Sent to Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy between January and December 2018.
  • Reviewed for fill timing, duplicate prescriptions, rerouting, and clinical context.

Conclusion

Traditional PMN logic overestimated the true non-adherence burden for oral oncolytics.

Because most potential PMN was not true PMN, the authors cautioned against using raw PMN alone as a specialty pharmacy quality metric.

Bottom line: a 19.7% algorithmic PMN rate shrank to 3.7% after clinical adjudication, showing that oncology specialty pharmacy PMN measures need chart-level context to distinguish non-initiation from alternate fulfillment and care-plan changes.
