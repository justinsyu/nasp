Beyond the prescription: the specialty clinical pharmacist's role in managing advanced lipid therapy

Geisinger describes a system-wide pharmacist-led lipid management model designed to remove access barriers, coordinate PCSK9 inhibitor starts, monitor response, and return stable patients to annual provider follow-up.

Objective

Show how embedded pharmacists turn a complex therapy start into a managed pathway

The poster's stated aims were to describe a system-wide pharmacist-led lipid program, highlight how patients are identified and referred from primary care or cardiology, and demonstrate how pharmacist involvement can improve provider access, adherence, and use of specialty pharmacy services.

Methods and care process

From referral to annual provider follow-up

1

Identify

Primary care or cardiology identifies a patient needing advanced lipid lowering therapy.

2

Refer

The patient is referred to a cardiology pharmacist working under a collaborative practice agreement.

3

Coordinate access

The pharmacist orders labs, documents prior therapy, and submits prior authorization materials.

4

Initiate

Labs are reviewed, copay assistance needs are assessed, and future lab and visit timing is scheduled.

5

Monitor

Follow-up labs occur 12 weeks after PCSK9i start, with an appointment 2 weeks after labs are complete.

6

Complete

LDL goal attainment and tolerability are confirmed before annual provider follow-up or discharge.

Referral sources

  • Primary care identified patients
  • Cardiologist identified patients
  • Shared handoff to cardiology pharmacist

Management routes

  • Cardiologist physician champion oversight
  • Referring physician oversight
  • Pharmacist-driven medication and access management

Results pattern and conclusion

The poster frames pharmacist co-management as a way to reduce friction around PCSK9i therapy

Patient satisfaction

Expected improvement through clearer access support, follow-up, and medication coordination.

Access barriers

Prior authorization, step therapy, and high out-of-pocket costs are built into pharmacist workflow.

Therapy delays

Coordinated labs, documentation, PA submission, and copay review are intended to speed initiation.

Long-term outcomes

Next steps include assessing cardiovascular outcomes, cost savings, and expansion to new clinics.

Provider access Reduced patient touchpoints free cardiology clinicians for care that requires physician involvement.
Specialty pharmacy leverage Dispensing and monitoring are connected to medication access work rather than handled as separate tasks.
Program expansion The model is proposed for additional clinics and other therapy areas with pharmacist-physician collaboration.
