Appropriate Prescribing of Antiemetics with Oral Oncolytic Therapy

A single-center quality improvement review at the Hospital of the University of Pennsylvania mapped where oral oncolytic therapy and supportive-care prescribing fell out of sync, then identified the chart-confirmed patients who lacked antiemetic orders.

Objective

Find gaps between oral chemotherapy and antiemetic prescribing

The poster reviewed appropriate antiemetic prescribing with oral oncolytic therapy in the ambulatory setting.

It also evaluated whether increased pharmacist involvement could improve medication safety and vigilance around concurrent supportive-care prescribing.

Single center

Quality improvement study at one academic medical center.

Identify OOT

Included oral oncolytics requiring antiemetic prophylaxis per guidelines.

Background risk

Oral oncolytics can require daily dosing across a treatment cycle, shifting supportive-care monitoring into ambulatory workflows.

Guideline gap

Antiemetic guidance exists, but order-set bundling is easier for parenteral chemotherapy than independently ordered oral therapy.

Local context

The institution had not yet embedded oral therapies into order templates, so antiemetics were often ordered separately.

Observed mismatch

After exclusions, 86 patients needed closer review for discordance between oral therapy and antiemetic prescribing.

Safety opportunity

The authors proposed pharmacist-supported prescribing and counseling workflows through the electronic health record.

Conclusion

Pharmacist involvement was the proposed safety lever

  • Oral oncolytic therapy carries unique prescribing risks because supportive care may not be bundled with treatment orders.
  • The authors proposed a program to increase pharmacist involvement in oral chemotherapy prescribing and counseling through the EHR.
  • Future metrics included appropriate supportive-care medications, hospitalizations, and quality of life.
