NASP 2020 Annual Meeting & Expo

Rates, reasons, and timing for treatment discontinuation in patients prescribed denosumab

A retrospective cohort study at an academic health center examined 534 adults who received at least two denosumab doses, focusing on why therapy stopped, whether patients transitioned to another osteoporosis treatment, and how fractures clustered around discontinuation.

Objective

Three discontinuation questions

The poster asked clinicians to look beyond whether denosumab stopped and capture the clinical context around that stop.

  1. How often did treatment discontinuation occur, and what reasons were documented?
  2. What proportion of patients transitioned to an alternative therapy after discontinuation?
  3. How frequently did fractures occur, and did they happen before or after discontinuation?

Interpretation

Pharmacist opportunity starts before the missed injection

  • Denosumab discontinuation was most often attributed to adverse effects, making tolerability monitoring a central persistence strategy.
  • Cost, insurance, transportation, and scheduling barriers were smaller but actionable contributors to discontinuation.
  • Most discontinuers did not transition to alternative therapy, despite known fracture risk when denosumab is stopped without follow-on treatment.

Poster conclusion: pharmacists can educate patients on adherence, help mitigate adverse effects, and recommend alternative therapy when tolerability requires modification.

Methods

Retrospective cohort design

Population: Adults with at least two denosumab doses from 2010 to 2018.

Measures: Demographics, treatment dates, fracture dates, discontinuation status, reasons for discontinuation, and alternative therapy transition.

Clinical context: Denosumab is administered every six months; stopping without alternative therapy may increase fracture risk.

Future direction

Move from description to intervention

The poster calls for additional research to design and test pharmacist interventions that promote adherence and persistence in osteoporosis treatment.

Priority targets suggested by the data include adverse-effect triage, insurance navigation, appointment logistics, and planned transition pathways.
