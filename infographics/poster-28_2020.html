The Impact of University Hospitals Specialty Pharmacy Hepatitis C Patient Management Program

A retrospective chart analysis examined how an integrated specialty pharmacy managed hepatitis C therapy access, monitoring, adherence, and sustained virological response compared with patients routed outside the UHSP dispensing model.

Why it mattered

HCV burden created a pharmacy access problem, not only a clinical one

The poster framed hepatitis C as a high-burden, high-cost disease state where specialty pharmacy coordination could affect prior authorization, grant support, refills, adherence monitoring, and laboratory follow-up.

Objective and method

Compare integrated UHSP management with non-UHSP dispensing pathways

Objective: describe the impact and success of UHSP in managing HCV patients, then compare outcomes for patients whose HCV medications were filled externally or not dispensed through UHSP.

Design: retrospective chart analysis of UHHS patients captured through the ambulatory electronic medical record.

Included: adults with documented chronic HCV infection and direct-acting antiviral prescriptions written during the 6-month study window.

Excluded: patients who never started HCV therapy at UH, declined insurance requirements, discontinued early due to non-compliance, left UHHS, were pregnant, or were younger than 18 years.

Conclusion

Integrated specialty pharmacy was positioned as a practical HCV access model

The authors concluded that UHSP's HCV Patient Management Program successfully supported therapy access, monitoring, adherence, and documented SVR12 in a real-world health-system specialty pharmacy setting.

Reported strengths included access to medical records, insurance and copay information, and an integrated specialty pharmacy model. Limitations included small sample size, single-center design, 6-month study duration, retrospective methods, and PDC measuring possession rather than administration.
