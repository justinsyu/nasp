Objective

Make small specialty-condition cohorts analytically usable

The poster argues that sample size is not the only route to statistical power. For conditions like HCV, more descriptive data per person and models that fit skewed, zero-inflated cost data can improve the signal available from smaller populations.

Methods

Three analytic lenses, same HCV comparison

1
Unadjusted means Simple group averages compared with t-tests.
2
Basic descriptive regression Two-stage model controlling for age, gender, region, and Charlson Comorbidity Index.
3
Comprehensive descriptive regression Basic controls plus race, salary, full- or part-time status, and exempt or non-exempt status.

Outcomes captured

Direct and indirect cost signals were modeled together

  • Direct costs: medical and prescription spending, adjusted to September 2013.
  • Indirect costs: sick leave, short-term disability, long-term disability, and workers' compensation from payroll records.
  • Lost time: absence durations aggregated by initiation year.
  • Eligibility: all subjects had at least 1 year of continuous eligibility.

Practice implication

Database selection should weigh components, not just N

Comprehensive descriptive information consistently outperformed basic descriptive regression models and non-adjusted methods in this poster's sensitivity analysis.

The authors conclude that real-world research programs should evaluate sample size, analytic methodology, and available data components together, especially for specialty pharmacy conditions with naturally smaller populations.

Smaller cohort

HCV employees were sampled at N=50 with 3x non-HCV controls.

Richer variables

Salary, job status, race, region, demographics, and comorbidity were available for modeling.

Two-stage model

Logistic regression followed by generalized linear models handled zero and skewed outcomes.

Cost outcomes

Medical, pharmacy, workers' compensation, and short-term disability estimates were compared.

Higher precision

The poster reports more precise results and a 50x reduction in standard errors.
