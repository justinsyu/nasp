Comparative Cost-Effectiveness Study of Hyperkalemia Management With Patiromer

A matched Medicare Advantage claims analysis compared hyperkalemia patients exposed to patiromer with similar patients not receiving patiromer. At 6 months, the patiromer cohort showed lower total mean costs despite higher outpatient pharmacy spending.

Evidence Flow

From hyperkalemia event to 6-month cost comparison.

Identify events

Medicare Advantage patients with a hyperkalemia event and potassium at least 5.0 mEq/L.

Define exposure

PAT index was a patiromer dispensed date; NoPAT index was a hyperkalemia diagnosis date.

Match cohorts

Propensity score matching and coarsened exact matching balanced baseline variables.

Follow forward

Follow-up ran from index until disenrollment, death, study end, treatment switch, or discontinuation.

Compare cost

Total, inpatient, ED, outpatient, and pharmacy costs were measured at 6 months post-index.

Clinical problem

Hyperkalemia can limit RAAS inhibitor use and is associated with serious cardiac risk.

Claims lens

The analysis used deidentified national commercial and Medicare Advantage claims.

Matched comparison

PAT and NoPAT groups were matched on demographics, comorbidities, potassium, and medication factors.

Cost offset

Pharmacy costs rose, while inpatient, ED, and outpatient medical costs fell more.

Study caution

The poster describes observational associations, not causal proof.

Conclusion

Six months of continuous patiromer therapy was associated with lower total mean cost in this matched claims study.

  • Mean total 6-month cost was 27% lower for PAT versus NoPAT.
  • Medical-cost reductions exceeded the increased outpatient pharmacy budget.
  • The authors called for further studies to confirm the observed cost-effectiveness signal.
