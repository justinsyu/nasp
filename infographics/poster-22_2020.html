Modeling Rates of Primary Medication Nonadherence With Specialty Oncolytic Agents

Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy modeled how lookback, duplicate, and fill-window assumptions change the reported rate of primary medication nonadherence for oncology specialty prescriptions. The key message: method choice materially changes the metric.

Study objective

Test how PMN methodology moves the reported outcome.

  • Understand how different PMN calculation methods impact results.
  • Define a probable rate range for PMN among oncology specialty agents.
  • Compare shorter and longer assumptions for lookback, duplicate, and fill windows.

Methods extracted

Three adjustable windows created the full model grid.

1. Start with claims

Specialty oncolytic prescriptions sent to an integrated specialty pharmacy.

2. Filter context

Prescriber specialty review removed likely non-oncology prescriptions.

3. Apply windows

LBW, DW, and FW values were varied across all combinations.

4. Calculate PMN

Eligible prescriptions without timely fills became PMN events.

  • FWLonger fill windows lowered PMN rates; this was the strongest driver.
  • DWLonger duplicate windows lowered PMN rates by excluding more duplicate prescriptions.
  • LBWChanging the lookback window had minimal impact in this dataset.
PQA calculation result 16%

The endorsed 180-day lookback, 30-day duplicate, and 30-day fill-window method produced one of the lowest PMN rates in the model set.

Conclusion

Report PMN with the method, not just the number.

The PQA-endorsed PMN calculation produced a 16% rate in this analysis. A shorter fill window increased PMN rates most sharply, while duplicate-window assumptions also affected results.

For PMN to function as a specialty pharmacy reporting metric, the calculation method needs to be stated comprehensively.
