Patterns of on-demand medication use during long-term C1-INH (SC) prophylaxis.

In the COMPACT open-label extension, patients receiving FDA-approved subcutaneous C1-inhibitor 60 IU/kg twice weekly had low rescue-medication use despite being permitted to treat any hereditary angioedema attack on demand.

Objective and method

Measure whether prophylaxis lowered the need for acute rescue treatment.

The analysis examined on-demand medication patterns among patients treated with C1-INH (SC) 60 IU/kg in a multicenter randomized open-label extension. Eligible patients had at least 4 attacks over 2 consecutive months; on-demand options included plasma-derived C1-INH, recombinant C1-INH, icatibant, ecallantide, and fresh frozen plasma.

Screening

Up to 4 weeks to confirm recurrent HAE attack history and study eligibility.

Randomization

126 patients were assigned to C1-INH (SC) 40 IU/kg or 60 IU/kg.

Treatment

Twice-weekly prophylaxis continued for 52 weeks, with up-titration allowed.

US extension

US patients could continue up to 140 weeks, enabling the >2-year subgroup view.

Endpoint

Exploratory endpoint: rescue medication recorded between attack start and end dates.

Nearly 4 in 10 attacks were not treated with on-demand medication.

Conclusion

The poster frames reduced rescue use as a cost-effectiveness input.

The authors concluded that on-demand medication use remained consistently low during long-term prophylactic therapy with C1-INH (SC) 60 IU/kg.

Two-thirds of patients used on-demand medication less than once per year, and only 38% of patients had any attack treated with rescue medication.
