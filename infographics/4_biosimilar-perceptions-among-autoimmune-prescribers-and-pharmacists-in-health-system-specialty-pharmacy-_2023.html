Biosimilar perceptions among autoimmune prescribers and health-system specialty pharmacists

A CPS Solutions survey explored how prepared and comfortable autoimmune prescribers and specialty pharmacists felt as the first wave of self-administered, pharmacy-dispensed autoimmune biosimilars approached the US market.

Objective

Build and deploy a survey measuring biosimilar knowledge and perceptions.

The study assessed autoimmune prescribers and health-system specialty pharmacists across the United States, focusing on knowledge, perception, and comfort with self-administered biosimilars.

  • Prescriber inclusion: rheumatology, dermatology, and gastroenterology clinicians managing autoimmune biologics.
  • Pharmacist inclusion: autoimmune clinic pharmacists managing patients prescribed biologic products.
  • Analysis: descriptive counts and percentages for each survey item.

Method signal

3-part

Each survey captured demographics, biosimilar knowledge, and Likert-scale perception and comfort measures.

Survey workflow

From launch context to respondent perceptions.

Market context Ten self-administered autoimmune biosimilars expected in 2023.
Survey development Two surveys created in January 2023 for prescribers and pharmacists.
Clinical refinement Five iterations plus four expert reviews for clinical relevance.
Email fielding Initial email plus three weekly reminder notifications.
Descriptive readout Counts and percentages summarized readiness and comfort gaps.

Discussion and conclusion

Knowledge gaps were the main intervention target.

  • The study identified biosimilar knowledge gaps and hesitancies among both specialty prescribers and pharmacists.
  • Education needs included the FDA review and approval process for biological, biosimilar, and interchangeable products.
  • Health-system specialty pharmacists were positioned as a practical group to close gaps and encourage acceptance.

Launch pressure

Ten self-administered autoimmune biosimilars were expected to expand patient options and cost-saving potential.

Measurement gap

Knowledge and perceptions of pharmacy-dispensed biosimilars were limited across health-system stakeholders.

Survey readout

Top-two-box comfort was moderate, but large somewhat and low-comfort segments remained.

Use-case clarity

Payer mandates and new starts produced stronger biosimilar prescribing or recommendation intent.

Action path

Focused education can support more confident conversations, recommendations, and acceptance.

Conclusion: as pharmacy-dispensed autoimmune biosimilars entered practice, the survey found moderate readiness but meaningful hesitancy, pointing to targeted education and pharmacist-led support as practical levers for adoption.
