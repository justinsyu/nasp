Rheumatoid arthritis and non-infused biologics: adherence, healthcare cost, and utilization

A retrospective claims study tested whether the PQA PDC-RA adherence metric identified meaningful differences in adjusted medical cost, hospitalizations, and inpatient length of stay among RA patients using non-infused biologics.

Objective

Validate whether PDC-RA tracks outcomes that matter

The poster examined associations between the PQA proportion of days covered metric for non-infused RA biologics and three downstream outcomes: total medical cost, hospitalizations, and inpatient length of stay.

Methods

Claims-to-outcomes evidence flow

Build cohort

MarketScan Commercial and Medicare supplemental claims from 2019-2020, continuous enrollment, RA diagnoses, and non-infused PDC-RA medication criteria.

Classify adherence

PDC >=80% defined adherence. The combined mean PDC was 80.9%, with all included patients showing prior RA diagnosis or biologic medication evidence.

Control risk

Models included payer, age, sex, region, metro status, comorbidity, inflammatory conditions, COVID-19, surgeries, PPO coverage, generic count, and retail ordering.

Model outcomes

General linear models with gamma or logit links estimated adjusted medical cost, PDC prescription cost, admission odds, and LOS.

Significant covariates

Adjusted models captured clinical and plan context

  • Medical costs increased with age, female sex, multiple generics, COVID-19, comorbidity, inflammatory conditions, surgery, metropolitan location, southern region, and commercial payer type.
  • Admission odds increased with age, comorbidity, COVID-19, inflammatory condition, and southern region, while commercial payer type was associated with lower odds.
  • LOS increased with age, comorbidity, COVID-19, and surgery.

Interpretation

PDC-RA behaved like a useful reporting metric

After adjustment for many observable influences, adherence to non-infused RA biologics was associated with lower medical costs, lower hospitalization odds, and shorter stays. The authors conclude that the PDC-RA methodology can support yearly reporting when filters account for alternative indications, late starts, and medication switching.

Conclusion

Adherence signaled lower downstream utilization.

The central finding is not simply that adherent patients used more specialty medication. It is that adherence aligned with lower adjusted medical cost and inpatient burden in a large real-world RA cohort.
