Assessing Patient-Reported Outcomes within an Integrated Health System Specialty Pharmacy

Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy evaluated whether missed doses, side effects, and perceived medication effectiveness were associated among specialty medication patients during monthly refill questionnaires.

Objective and methods

A three-month refill touchpoint became an outcomes sensor

1

Included patients receiving two or more specialty medication fills through Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy.

2

Certified pharmacy technicians completed at least two monthly refill questionnaires from January through March 2020.

3

Specialty pharmacists reviewed patient-reported side effects, missed doses, and effectiveness as needed.

Association signals

When a patient reported a problem, the next question mattered

Patients reporting side effects were more likely to also report a missed dose.

No side effects to higher effectiveness 7.49x

Patients with no side effects were more likely to report higher perceived effectiveness.

No missed doses to higher effectiveness 2.13x

Patients with no missed doses were more likely to report higher perceived effectiveness.

Conclusion

Targeted monitoring signal

Specialty pharmacies should consider focused follow-up when patients report side effects or missed doses, because both were associated with perceived medication effectiveness.
