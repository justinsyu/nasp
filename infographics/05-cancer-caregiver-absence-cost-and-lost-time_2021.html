A Workpartners Research Reference Database analysis followed employees whose spouses had cancer claims, comparing sick leave, disability, and workers' compensation use against a 2001 baseline.

The poster's central warning: constant salary-replacement assumptions can distort absence cost estimates across benefits and years.

Objective

Replace broad absence assumptions with benefit-specific evidence

The study compared all-cause sick leave, short-term disability, long-term disability, and workers' compensation utilization for employees whose spouses had cancer.

  • Employees were identified annually through spouse medical claims in AHRQ oncology categories.
  • Each benefit was tracked for claim filing, mean days of leave, and median payments as a percent of salary.
  • Results were indexed against the 2001 baseline to show how time, benefit type, and payments diverged.

Indexed results

By 2019, disability burden rose faster than disease prevalence

Prevalence stayed near 3%, but the indexed absence and payment measures moved sharply. Long-term disability showed the largest growth in mean days of leave, while workers' compensation filing fell below baseline.
