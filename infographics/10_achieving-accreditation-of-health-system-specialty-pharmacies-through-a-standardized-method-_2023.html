Achieving Accreditation of Health System Specialty Pharmacies Through a Standardized Method

Shields Health Solutions evaluated a repeatable, collaborative method for helping health system specialty pharmacy partners achieve dual initial accreditation and first-attempt reaccreditation.

Results signal

Standardization converted a complex accreditation process into a repeatable operating model.

The poster reports first-attempt success across initial accreditation and reaccreditation, while maintaining year-over-year consistency despite accreditation standard changes and COVID-19 during the study period.

Evidence flow

The five-part method is a continuous accreditation readiness loop.

Shields' method pairs frequent partner touchpoints with policy and non-policy procedure development, a proprietary tracker, organized document maintenance, and mock survey preparation.

  • Objective: Evaluate and analyze the standardized method and support dialogue about pathways to specialty pharmacy accreditation.
  • Analysis period: Organizational data were reviewed from January 1, 2018 through January 31, 2023.
  • Exclusions: Partners with prior accreditation were excluded from the initial accreditation group; partners not yet due or pursuing independently were excluded from reaccreditation analysis.

Outreach

Establish consistent touchpoints with the health system partner to coordinate readiness and accountability.

Develop and review

Create new and review existing policy, non-policy, and procedure materials.

Track progress

Use a proprietary tracker to monitor completion of policies, procedures, and non-policy requirements.

Manage and maintain

Keep documents organized, accessible, maintained, and compliant over the three-year accreditation cycle.

Prepare

Review materials and conduct mock audits to prepare the pharmacy team for survey activity.

Methods

Metrics were selected from organizational accreditation data.

  • Median time to dual initial accreditation was evaluated for partners with and without an operational pharmacy at time zero.
  • First-attempt success was assessed for dual full accreditation and full reaccreditation.
  • Application status was reviewed for partners due for ACHC and URAC reaccreditation.

Conclusion

The method appears adaptable for health systems pursuing specialty pharmacy accreditation.

The authors concluded that a standardized, collaborative method supported first-attempt initial accreditation and reaccreditation success, while remaining consistent during changing standards and the COVID-19 period.

The process is presented as a practical framework health systems can adapt for accreditation and reaccreditation readiness.
