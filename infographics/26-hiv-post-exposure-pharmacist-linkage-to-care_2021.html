HIV Post-Exposure Prophylaxis Alerts in Emergency Departments

A health-system specialty pharmacy built an urgent EHR alert so pharmacists could reach patients starting HIV PEP in two Twin Cities emergency departments, solve access barriers, and coordinate follow-up.

Daniel Jude, PharmD, AAHIVP, CSP; Jessica Mourani, PharmD

Evidence flow

An EHR signal converted urgent ED prescribing into specialty pharmacy action

ED order placed

An ED provider ordered antiretroviral therapy for PEP or urgent HIV-related need.

Priority alert fires

A novel urgent alert in the EHR notified the onsite specialty pharmacist by mobile app.

Pharmacist connects

The pharmacist visited or called the ED to provide counseling and assess barriers.

Access support starts

The pharmacist coordinated with the specialty pharmacy liaison, ID clinic, and SANE team.

Follow-up pathway

Patients were routed to dispensing and planned follow-up at 5 days, 6 weeks, and 3 months.

Prescription capture and follow-up

Filling within the health system was tied to lower loss to follow-up

40.5% Lost to follow-up among captured fills

32 of 79 health-system pharmacy fills were lost to follow-up at some point.

80% Lost to follow-up when not captured

8 of 10 non-captured prescriptions were lost to follow-up.

44 Captured fills not lost

55.7% of the captured-fill cohort remained in follow-up.

Practice insights

Small workflow details mattered for vulnerable patients

  • 4 of 5 benefits-counselor referrals resulted in active state-sponsored insurance.
  • Drug interactions were commonly cation chelation issues or regular NSAID use.
  • Nausea was common and often self-limiting after therapy initiation.
  • Privacy risk from automated ED mailings prompted proactive disclosure-prevention support.

Conclusion: specialty pharmacy capture and a priority EHR alert helped identify ED patients starting PEP, connect them to medication access and adherence support, and reduce loss to follow-up compared with prescriptions filled outside the health system.
