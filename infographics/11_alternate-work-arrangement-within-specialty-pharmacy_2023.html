Alternate Work Arrangement within Specialty Pharmacy

Yale New Haven Health tested whether specialty pharmacy call center staff could move to a remote work model while preserving accreditation service levels: speed of answer below 20 seconds and abandonment rate below 2%.

Operating model

Remote verification became a service-level experiment.

Eligible staff with more than six months in role received equipment and completed a remote test shift before entering a phased deployment. The work design kept new technicians and periodic pharmacist rotations onsite to protect the pharmacist-to-technician ratio.

Interpretation

The operating lesson was gradualism, not remote work alone.

  • Phase I showed stress, with several weekly speed-of-answer values over 20 seconds and one abandonment spike to 2.5%.
  • The team extended Phase I by four weeks to separate remote-work effects from holiday call volume.
  • By Phase III, reported averages were 15 seconds and 1.1% abandonment, supporting the conclusion that a phased approach can preserve service levels.

Barriers

Constraints the poster surfaced

  • Staff movement into other health-system roles required requisitions and backfilling.
  • Holiday closures created bursts of incoming calls.
  • Equipment budget limited deployment pace.

Next direction

Scale the model carefully.

The poster's future direction was to adapt the remote work model for all retail pharmacy locations within the health system, using the specialty pharmacy deployment as the template.

Bottom line

A remote specialty pharmacy call center can keep accreditation metrics intact when rollout is staged.

The Yale New Haven Health team moved from a small remote pilot to a 70% offsite work arrangement and reported Phase III averages of 15 seconds speed of answer and 1.1% abandonment, both within the poster's stated accreditation thresholds.
