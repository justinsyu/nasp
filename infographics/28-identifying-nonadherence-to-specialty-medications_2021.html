Identifying nonadherence to specialty medications

Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy tested whether patients with PDC below 90% were truly nonadherent or instead had appropriate, clinically explainable medication gaps that claims data could not see.

Objective

Separate true nonadherence from appropriate specialty-medication gaps.

Claims-based adherence metrics can detect refill gaps, but they cannot tell whether a patient stopped therapy inappropriately, held therapy for infection or surgery, changed therapies, filled elsewhere, or discontinued under clinical direction.

Methods

A clinical pharmacist review converted a claims-data flag into an adherence classification.

Import

Patients with eligible medications, health-system specialty pharmacy dispensing, and PDC below 90% were imported.

Exclude

Records were removed for outside prescribers, planned discontinuation, death, multiple same-clinic specialty meds, or timing conflicts.

Review

Pharmacists reviewed patient health records to identify and categorize the reason behind each apparent gap.

Classify

Appropriate reasons became misidentified nonadherence; unexplained gaps became true nonadherence.

Confirm

A sample of 115 true-nonadherence patients was contacted to confirm reasons for nonadherence.

Claims flag

PDC below 90% identified patients with refill gaps, but not the reason for those gaps.

Record review

Pharmacists used health records to classify appropriate versus unexplained therapy gaps.

Appropriate gaps

Clinical reasons, discontinuation, outside fills, and therapy changes explained many low-PDC cases.

True barriers

Memory problems and inability to reach patients were the most common confirmed true-nonadherence reasons.

Practice implication

Specialty adherence measurement should account for clinically appropriate gaps before labeling patients nonadherent.

Conclusions

A single pharmacy database does not capture the full medication story.

Claims data can miss outside fills, medication changes, discontinuation, and clinically appropriate holds. The authors conclude that reasons for true and misidentified nonadherence vary across patients and clinics.

Future directions

Better detection should target true gaps.

The poster calls for improved methods to identify true nonadherence and further research on reducing memory-related gaps and problems reaching patients.

Conclusion: pharmacy claims alone substantially overcalled specialty-medication nonadherence; pharmacist review showed that clinically appropriate gaps should be incorporated into adherence evaluation.
