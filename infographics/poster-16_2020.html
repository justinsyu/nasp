Expansion of outpatient pharmacy services through rapid-start antiretroviral therapy

Yale New Haven Health mapped two infectious disease clinic pathways and two outpatient pharmacy sites to build a rapid-start ART program designed to put medication in hand on the day of HIV diagnosis.

Objective

Turn fragmented first-contact workflows into a Day 1 medication pathway.

Objective 1: Identify and address barriers to initiating a rapid-start ART program.

Objective 2: Develop new workflow processes across pharmacy sub-specialties for successful implementation.

Eligible patient profile

  • New HIV diagnosis
  • Likely adherent by provider assessment
  • Ready to initiate therapy

Excluded scenarios

  • Active opportunistic infection or high suspicion
  • Emotional reluctance to initiate therapy

5

Develop the communication and monitoring plan.

Results

Five barriers defined the operational redesign.

Prescriber coverage

No readily available prescriber existed for on-call requests.

Financial resources

Prescription coverage resources were not yet coordinated across workflows.

Delivery speed

Medication delivery needed to meet a 1 to 2 hour timeliness goal.

Processing flow

Medication processing needed optimization between OPS and AWC.

Point personnel

The RSAT program needed dedicated point personnel for management.

Medication processing workflow

Pharmacy sub-specialties were linked into one rapid handoff.

CPhT = certified pharmacy technician; OPS = Outpatient Pharmacy Services; AWC = Apothecary & Wellness Center; M2B = Meds-to-Beds.

Need

WHO and HRSA recommendations supported immediate ART initiation, but YNHH had no established RSAT program.

Mapping

Two clinics had different initial workflows, including delayed MD and prescription timing at Nathan Smith Clinic.

Optimization

On-call prescriber coverage, assistance resources, and pharmacy processing were redesigned as connected work.

Implementation

The program used existing OPS, AWC, HIV pharmacist, CPhT, and Meds-to-Beds capacity to speed medication access.

Next study

Future analyses were planned for time to viral suppression and retention in care before and after implementation.

Conclusion

Successful RSAT implementation was the reported endpoint.

The poster concluded that YNHH implemented a successful RSAT program for patients to receive drug in hand on the day of diagnosis.

The authors anticipated enhanced access to vital ART within comprehensive HIV care, with clinical outcome evaluation planned for future work.
