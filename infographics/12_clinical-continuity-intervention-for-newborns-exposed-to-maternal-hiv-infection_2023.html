Clinical Continuity Intervention for Newborns Exposed to Maternal HIV Infection

A neonatal unit and integrated specialty pharmacy workflow was designed to send exposed newborns home with the full antiviral course, regardless of insurance status, while pairing dispensing with layered medication education.

Evidence flow

The intervention turned a discharge risk point into a closed-loop medication handoff.

Order

Clinician uses the NEO IP HIV Prophylaxis and Treatment order set in the electronic health record.

Route

The prescription is sent to Outpatient Pharmacy Services at YNHHS and flagged for processing.

Process

OPS reviews the prescription, determines the billing process, and coordinates with the inpatient pediatric pharmacy team.

Teach

OPS provides initial telephone education; pediatric pharmacists provide hands-on administration teaching.

Discharge

The patient and family leave the hospital with the full course of antiviral therapy in hand.

Continuity design

Four stakeholder lanes carried one patient-facing goal.

The methods centered on stakeholder alignment, electronic ordering improvements, and billing resolution so the discharge medication supply did not depend on insurance status at the bedside.

Clinicians Initiate the neonatal HIV prophylaxis and treatment order set.
Care access Care management and financial access counselors help resolve coverage and discharge barriers.
OPS team Receives flagged prescriptions, processes the medication, and provides initial education.
Pediatric RPh Receives delivery, brings therapy to the bedside, and teaches administration.

Why the gap mattered

The poster framed early continuity as prevention.

Birth exposure risk Maternal HIV exposure creates a need for prompt, correctly administered neonatal antiviral prophylaxis.
4-6 week course The complete therapy window extends beyond the hospital stay, making discharge logistics central to care.
Education need Poor health literacy and medication administration errors were named peri-discharge barriers.
Continuity target The full-course supply and pharmacy teaching were intended to reduce interruption opportunities.

Limitations

The model depended on a highly integrated system.

  • Workflow design required collaboration among stakeholders with competing priorities.
  • Electronic health record restrictions shaped the implementation path.
  • The intervention leveraged an existing specialty pharmacy team dedicated to infectious disease medications.

Future direction

Scale and follow longer-term outcomes.

The authors proposed expansion to remaining delivery networks in Yale New Haven Health System, CarePathway implementation to streamline ordering, and evaluation of long-term outcomes including infant HIV seroconversion.

Bottom line

Specialty pharmacy integration converted discharge medication access into a measurable continuity intervention.

By aligning neonatal units, inpatient pharmacy, outpatient specialty pharmacy, IT, and billing stakeholders, Yale New Haven Health reported complete 2023 antiviral order capture and early outcome signals of full adherence, full therapy completion, and no transmission in the reported cohort.
