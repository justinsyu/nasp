Moving toward meaningful patient outcomes in rare disease: an achondroplasia case study

Optum Frontier Therapies reframed achondroplasia treatment evaluation from an approval-centered growth velocity endpoint toward a patient-first outcomes model that captures complications, function, pain, mental health, and medical interventions.

Central question

What is the most important outcome to collect for patients considering treatment for achondroplasia?

Why the question mattered

The approval endpoint did not fully answer the community's decision problem.

Trial lens

Vosoritide approval relied on growth velocity data from the pivotal clinical trial.

Care lens

Patients, caregivers, and prescribers wanted to understand whether treatment could reduce serious achondroplasia-related complications and interventions.

Model lens

The case study sought to build a shared-decision model grounded in the outcomes that stakeholders described as meaningful.

Methods as an evidence flow

Stakeholder input converted a narrow endpoint into a broader outcomes map.

Start with the evidence gap

Approval data established efficacy on growth velocity, but not the full lived burden of achondroplasia.

Sample trial prescribers

A simple randomization method selected 10% of prescribers from the pivotal phase 3 trial for interviews.

Screen advocacy groups

The team used NCATS-approved advocacy groups and Little People of America to identify patient-community input.

Interview real experts

Input came from geneticist, pediatrician, orthopedic surgeon, patient support, and biotech council perspectives.

Results

Six outcomes emerged as the decision-ready set.

Stakeholders prioritized outcomes that reflect day-to-day burden and serious medical complications, not only linear growth. The depression scales were specifically advocated for by Little People of America.

Conclusion

Patient voice became the model architecture.

By collaborating with patients, caregivers, key opinion leaders, and prescribers, Optum Frontier Therapies created a patient-first outcomes model intended to help people make decisions about achondroplasia treatment.

Research posture

The poster positioned the model as ongoing work.

The team described continued research with patient advocacy groups, payers, prescribers, and manufacturers to inform the approach and preserve the voice of the patient.

References cited in the poster include the pivotal vosoritide phase 3 trial and shared decision-making literature.

Objective set

The poster's three-part aim

01 Review the evidence gap

Compare drug-approval trial data with the outcomes patients, caregivers, and prescribers wanted.

02 Design a new decision approach

Use stakeholder input to shape a model that supports patient care choices.

03 Achieve meaningful outcomes

Apply the model to first-in-class achondroplasia therapy in a way that measures what matters.
