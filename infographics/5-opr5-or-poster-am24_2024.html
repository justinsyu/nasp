Beyond Viral Load Suppression: Role of Health System Specialty Pharmacy in HIV Management and Opportunistic Infections Prevention

A multicenter retrospective descriptive study evaluated how a pharmacist-supported HIV specialty care management program paired viral load monitoring with immune competence tracking.

Objective

Move the endpoint beyond viral load alone

The study assessed whether a clinical pharmacist supported HIV program helped patients achieve viral load suppression while maintaining immune competence, measured through CD4 monitoring.

Evidence flow

Pharmacist-supported HIV monitoring loop

1

Enroll and baseline

Patients enter the specialty care management program with baseline viral load and CD4 counts documented.

2

Monitor every 3 to 6 months

Pharmacists follow adherence, viral load, and CD4 labs to keep clinical risk visible.

3

Act on risk signals

Low CD4 prompts discussion of opportunistic infection prophylaxis with the patient and provider.

4

Simplify when appropriate

Virally suppressed patients interested in long-acting ART receive education and care-team coordination.

Pharmacist action

Long-acting ART transition was a targeted simplification pathway

3%

Among virally suppressed patients, 3% (n=17) were supported in transitioning to long-acting injectable ART while maintaining viral suppression and CD4 count above 200.

  • Pharmacists provided education, adherence support, lab monitoring, therapy modification coordination, and adverse event management.
  • The poster frames opportunistic infection prophylaxis as a key pharmacist-supported intervention when CD4 values fall below 200.
  • A noted limitation is that other pharmacist roles, including statin therapy, weight management, and cardiovascular risk mitigation, were not fully captured.

Conclusion

Specialty pharmacists expand HIV care from medication access to longitudinal risk management

The poster concludes that integrated health system specialty pharmacists are positioned to support comprehensive HIV management through adherence reinforcement, medication and disease education, lab monitoring, therapy changes, opportunistic infection prophylaxis, and care-team coordination.
