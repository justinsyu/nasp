NASP 2020 Poster-32

Development of an evidence-based specialty pharmacy program for hemophilia

UMass Memorial and Shields Health Solutions described how a pharmacist-nurse hemophilia program was built by translating guidelines, payer requirements, accreditation standards, and meaningful clinical outcomes into a patient management platform and outreach workflow.

Objective

Translate standards into a hemophilia care system

Rather than reporting a drug outcome study, this poster documents the process for designing and implementing a comprehensive specialty pharmacy program for patients with complex hemophilia treatment regimens.

Evidence flow

From requirement gathering to a working care model

Search the evidence

Literature from January 1, 2010 onward was reviewed to identify clinically meaningful hemophilia outcomes and definitions.

Collect operating standards

MASAC guidance, commercial payer requirements, and accreditation documentation standards were compiled into a build list.

Configure the PMP

Required questions, assessments, interventions, and outcome fields were embedded in the internal patient management platform.

Deliver customized care

A hemophilia nurse and specialty pharmacists used the platform to track outcomes, guide outreach, and coordinate infusion support.

New start workflow

Structured assessments anchor the program

  1. 1Initiate onboarding assessment for the patient.
  2. 2Complete clinical initial assessment.
  3. 3Evaluate referral need for home infusion nursing services.
  4. 4Continue clinical follow-up assessment every 6 months.
  5. 5Open patient care initial activity for ongoing management.

Refill workflow

Refills become clinical checkpoints

  1. AStart with patient care refill activity and screen for a recent bleed.
  2. BIf a bleed occurred, complete a bleed assessment in the PMP and adjust care if needed.
  3. CIf a refill is needed, perform outreach and assess clinical status through required PMP questions.
  4. DCoordinate assay management to reduce variance between ordered and dispensed dose.
  5. EConfirm infusion supplies, home nursing needs, future follow-up, or supply reconciliation.

Requirement architecture

Three external lenses shaped the internal platform

The program did not rely on a single requirement source. It combined payer expectations, accreditation documentation, and outcomes judged clinically meaningful for hemophilia management.

Home infusion layer

Operational support was contracted, not assumed

Two home infusion nursing agencies preferred by regional patients were identified. The specialty pharmacy established contracts, and the hemophilia nurse referral packet included demographics, medication or supply orders, and intravenous access details.

Conclusion

A systematic build creates the quality foundation

Gathering requirements from payors, accreditation organizations, and clinical best practices provided the foundation for the hemophilia program. Ongoing evaluation of outcomes was positioned as the next step to validate quality care delivered by the hemophilia nurse and specialty pharmacists.
