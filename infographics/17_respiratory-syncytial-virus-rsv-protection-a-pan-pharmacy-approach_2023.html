Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV) Protection: A Pan-Pharmacy Approach

Driscoll Children's Hospital paired specialty and inpatient pharmacy pathways so high-risk pediatric patients across South Texas could receive palivizumab through either pharmacy or medical benefits.

Process design

A single clinic order could follow either benefit pathway

1

High-Risk clinic initiates

Clinic providers send palivizumab prescriptions to the specialty pharmacy for intake and benefits investigation.

2

Specialty pharmacy route

If fillable under the pharmacy benefit, specialty pharmacy obtains prior authorization, dispenses the dose, and delivers it back to clinic.

3

Inpatient pharmacy hand-off

If not fillable by specialty pharmacy, the case moves to inpatient pharmacy for medical-benefit pre-authorization and dispensing.

4

Clinic administration

Doses are administered in the High-Risk clinic, with subsequent doses scheduled during the RSV season.

Dispensing and revenue pattern

Most doses flowed through specialty pharmacy; revenue skewed inpatient

  • 326 doses (90%) were filled by specialty pharmacy through the pharmacy benefit.
  • 36 doses (10%) were filled by inpatient pharmacy through the medical benefit.
  • Revenue inverted: specialty pharmacy represented 36% of revenue while inpatient pharmacy represented 64%.

Why it mattered

Regional access needed a pharmacy model beyond usual pediatrician-managed dosing

Before the 2022-23 RSV season, pediatricians across South Texas were responsible for obtaining and administering palivizumab, and many high-risk patients did not receive indicated prophylaxis.

DCH's inpatient pharmacy, specialty pharmacy, and High-Risk clinic created a coordinated service line for a hospital serving 31 counties across 33,000 square miles.

Conclusion: leveraging both specialty and inpatient pharmacies enabled more high-risk patients to receive beneficial RSV prophylaxis and created a repeatable model for other dual-benefit medications.
