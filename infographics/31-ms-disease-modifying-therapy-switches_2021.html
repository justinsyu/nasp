Changes in disease-modifying therapies in patients with relapsing multiple sclerosis

Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy reviewed long-term DMT switching in an integrated health-system specialty pharmacy cohort, finding that one in four patients switched therapy and that insurance, clinical decline, and side effects shaped most transitions.

Objective

Evaluate the long-term rate and reasons for DMT switching in a specialty pharmacy cohort.

The review followed patients receiving RMS therapy through Vanderbilt's integrated specialty pharmacy model, then classified switch patterns by original therapy, post-switch therapy, timing, reason, and initiating role.

Methods

Single-center retrospective review using clinical, pharmacy management, and claims data.

Design

Single-center, retrospective review of RMS patients treated through Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy.

Inclusion

RMS diagnosis, at least three DMT fills during May-Oct 2017, and Vanderbilt MS clinic prescriber.

Sources

Electronic health record, specialty pharmacy patient management system, and pharmacy claims.

Conclusion

Therapy transitions were frequent and operationally complex.

  • DMT switches occurred frequently in RMS patients and were most often attributed to insurance formulary change, clinical decline, or side effects.
  • Patients commonly switched to higher efficacy monoclonal antibody infusion medications when clinical decline was documented.
  • Integrated specialty pharmacists played a key role in identifying the need for change and coordinating care during transitions.
