Follow the Script: Assessing Access Time and Adherence in Patients with MS

This retrospective analysis compared health system specialty pharmacy fills with external pharmacy fills for self-administered disease-modifying therapies. The signal was consistent: HSSP patients reached therapy faster and maintained stronger adherence.

Barrier

High costs, limited distribution networks, and prior authorizations can delay DMT access.

Intervention lens

Health system specialty pharmacy services integrate medication access and clinical follow-up.

Measured access

Average script-to-mouth time was 8 days for HSSP and 24 days for external pharmacies.

Measured adherence

New-start PDC was 0.97 with HSSP versus 0.89 externally; overall PDC was 0.96 versus 0.94.

Implication

Earlier treatment and stronger adherence may help reduce the risk of MS progression.

Extracted objective and conclusion

What the poster set out to test.

Objective: Evaluate script-to-mouth time for MS DMTs and compare adherence rates between HSSP and unaffiliated external pharmacies.

  • HSSP dispensed nearly all prescriptions in less time than the external pharmacy average and Q3.
  • HSSP patients more consistently had PDC in the 90% to 100% range.
  • Access could not be assessed when the initial written date was unavailable in the EHR or from the external pharmacy.

Bottom line

Health system specialty pharmacy was associated with faster access and higher adherence.

The poster concludes that MS patients enrolled in HSSP services had script-to-mouth time more than three times faster and achieved higher adherence than patients filling through external pharmacies. Because DMT benefit depends on early and consistent treatment, the access and PDC findings point in the same clinical direction.
