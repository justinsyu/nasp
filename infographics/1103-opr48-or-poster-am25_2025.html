Objective

Measure the uncompensated clinical work that persists when the HSSP cannot dispense

The poster set out to quantify the time, intervention type, and labor costs tied to health-system specialty pharmacy pharmacists' documented actions for patients who were not filling prescriptions through the HSSP.

  • Care continuity and safe medication use remained pharmacist responsibilities even when prescriptions moved to outside pharmacies.
  • The analysis focused on pharmacist-documented "quick actions" captured in an electronic health record flowsheet.

Methods

From routine EHR documentation to a labor-cost estimate

Capture quick actions

Pharmacists categorized action type, outcome, and time spent as part of routine workflow for non-HSSP patients.

Review 3 months

Documented actions from July 1 through September 30, 2024 were reviewed for descriptive analysis.

Convert time to cost

Total pharmacist time was multiplied by an hourly labor rate of $71.43, including fringe.

Poster conclusion

External pharmacy routing did not remove the health-system pharmacist workload

  • HSSP pharmacists frequently assisted non-HSSP patients to support continuity of care and safe medication use.
  • When the HSSP could not dispense specialty medication, the resulting workload represented more than $3.25 million in uncompensated labor.
  • The highest-volume subcategory was medication access coordination, followed by therapy monitoring interventions and prescription preparation.

Setting

Academic medical center and Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy health-system specialty pharmacy.

Sample

Patients not filling with the HSSP who had at least one documented quick action.

Primary lens

Action type, action outcome, and pharmacist time spent on non-HSSP patient support.

Top result

Medication review and care coordination accounted for 1,945 selected categories.

Implication

Dispensing access barriers can shift substantial clinical coordination costs back to health systems.
