Trajectory of Asthma Control Test Scores Among Patients Treated Within the Specialty Pharmacy

Fairview Pharmacy Services evaluated how ACT scores changed after specialty pharmacy dispensing and which care characteristics aligned with being well controlled or achieving a minimally important difference.

Evidence flow

From specialty pharmacy encounter data to personalized asthma follow-up.

1

Identify eligible patients

Age 12 or older, asthma diagnosis, at least one 2023 specialty pharmacy dispense, baseline ACT, and follow-up ACT 30 to 365 days later.

2

Classify ACT control

Scores were grouped as very poorly controlled under 16, not well controlled 16 to 19, or well controlled 20 to 25.

3

Compare care factors

Chi-squared tests and logistic regressions assessed adherence, insurance, therapy status, medication switches, and triggers.

4

Target assessment intensity

ACT trajectory, nonadherence, insurance type, and triggers can shape counseling and reassessment frequency.

Interpretation: improvement depended on both clinical starting point and care context, so the poster frames ACT follow-up as a trajectory, not a single end-state.

Insurance pattern

Insurance was similar at baseline but meaningful at follow-up.

  • Commercial43.9%
  • Medicaid47.4%
  • Medicare8.8%
  • Commercial insurance was associated with follow-up control and meeting MID.
  • Insurance was associated with adherence; 85.7% of patients with PDC below 0.8 were on Medicaid.
  • The poster points to social determinants of health as a likely context for poorer asthma outcomes.

Table pattern

Conclusion

ACT trajectories can make pharmacy follow-up more specific.

  • Most patients were well controlled or met MID at follow-up within the specialty pharmacy.
  • Commercial insurance patients were most likely to achieve MID and remain adherent.
  • Incorporating insurance, nonadherence, triggers, and ACT trajectory into counseling may improve personalization.

MID was defined as an ACT improvement of at least 3 points, based on Schatz et al., J Allergy Clin Immunol, 2009.
