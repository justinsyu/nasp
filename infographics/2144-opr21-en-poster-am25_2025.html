Post-launch adverse events reported for long-acting injectable buprenorphine

A retrospective FAERS analysis compared adverse event reporting rates during the first 12 months after US commercial launch for RBP-6000 and CAM2038, two long-acting injectable buprenorphine products used for moderate to severe opioid use disorder.

Objective

Convert spontaneous AE reports into comparable launch-period reporting rates

  • Analyze adverse event terms reported in at least 5% of all case reports for either product.
  • Calculate rates per 1000 patients treated and per 1000 units sold using estimated launch-period denominators.
  • Report AE terms with a reporting rate of at least 1 for one or both products.

Evidence flow

From FAERS case reports to normalized product comparison

1

Define launch windows

Each product was assigned its first 12 months after US commercial launch.

2

Filter AE terms

Terms appearing in 5% or more of reports were retained for analysis.

3

Estimate exposure

Patients treated and units sold were estimated from public, specialty pharmacy, and distributor data.

4

Normalize rates

Reporting rates were calculated per 1000 units sold and per 1000 treated patients.

per 1000 units

Withdrawal syndrome and injection-site pain were the highest RBP-6000 reporting rates per 1000 units sold; corresponding CAM2038 rates were 1.1 and 0.3.

Conclusion signal

Reported AEs were infrequent across both launch periods

  • The analysis describes generally low spontaneous AE reporting rates for both long-acting injectable buprenorphine products.
  • The highest rates clustered around withdrawal syndrome and injection-site reactions.
  • CAM2038 had lower rates than RBP-6000 for most listed AE terms, while drug ineffective and therapeutic response shortened were closer across products.

Interpretation guardrails

Spontaneous-report data have built-in uncertainty

  • FAERS reports do not establish that the product caused the reported adverse event.
  • Some adverse events may be underreported or inconsistently submitted.
  • Exposure denominators were estimated and may be affected by incomplete capture, duplication, or lack of linkage to administration.
  • Early launch-period reporting can be influenced by stimulated reporting patterns such as the Weber effect.

Background

Two LAIB medications are approved for moderate to severe opioid use disorder: monthly RBP-6000 and weekly/monthly CAM2038.

Methods

FAERS reports were analyzed retrospectively during each product's first 12 months after US commercial launch.

Denominators

The poster estimated 6,464 RBP-6000 and 24,805 CAM2038 treated patients, with 16,770 and 94,826 units sold.

Results

Withdrawal syndrome and injection-site pain were the largest reporting-rate categories for RBP-6000.

Conclusion

Overall AE reports for both products were infrequent and may inform real-world safety discussions for LAIB treatment.
