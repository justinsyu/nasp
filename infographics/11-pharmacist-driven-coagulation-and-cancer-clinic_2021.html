Direct Oral Anticoagulant Management Among Cancer Patients and Implementation of a Pharmacist-driven Coagulation and Cancer Clinic

Atrium Health translated oncology pharmacy, specialty pharmacy, and anticoagulation-clinic expertise into a subspecialized Cancer and Coagulation Clinic designed to manage DOAC treatment, LMWH monitoring, therapy duration, and VTE prophylaxis decisions for complex cancer patients.

Cancer and Coagulation Clinic workflow

Referral moved patients from oncology identification to pharmacist-authored anticoagulation plans

Identify

Cancer patient with diagnosed VTE is identified by a clinic pharmacist or provider.

Refer

Referral routes the patient to CCC for DOAC initiation, LMWH anti-Xa monitoring, or VTE treatment-duration review.

Schedule

Anticoagulation-clinic staff schedule the visit and alert the CCC pharmacist about the new referral.

Evaluate

The pharmacist assesses therapy, duration, interactions, labs, bleeding risk, adherence, and patient-specific concerns.

Close loop

A consult is sent to the referring physician, followed by CCC follow-up, CCC discharge, or transition to the general anticoagulation clinic.

Results

The launch established a cross-practice pharmacy model

  • Outpatient anticoagulation, specialty pharmacy, and hematology/oncology groups collaborated on a novel service.
  • The clinic was the first subspecialized clinic launch by the integrated specialty pharmacy service.
  • The model shifted specialty pharmacy toward practice-based integrated care.
  • Updated evidence and guidelines made pharmacist-led DOAC optimization and prophylaxis screening timely.

Referral template pattern

Structured intake

  • Medication: apixaban, dabigatran, edoxaban, rivaroxaban, or TBD
  • Indication: problem-list VTE and malignancy details
  • Risk: bleeding/clotting history, stroke/TIA, falls, HAS-BLED context
  • Therapy length: 3, 6, 12 months, lifelong, or other
  • Bridge therapy: enoxaparin, fondaparinux, heparin, or other when relevant

Visit-note pattern

Clinical review

  • Adherence, bleeding, hospitalizations, falls, dyspnea, swelling, cost, and procedures
  • Weight, height, BMI, blood pressure, creatinine clearance, hemoglobin/hematocrit, AST, and ALT
  • Medication reconciliation and drug-interaction assessment
  • Bleeding precautions, fall-risk education, procedure recommendations, labs, and follow-up

Next outcomes

Measurement plan

  • Recurrent VTE
  • Minor, moderate, and major bleeding events
  • Hospitalization
  • Pharmacist intervention evaluations
  • Periodic VTE-risk screening and possible expansion to other sites

Conclusion: a pharmacist-driven Cancer and Coagulation Clinic can convert fragmented anticoagulation decisions into a repeatable oncology-facing workflow, while future screening and outcomes capture will test its impact on prophylaxis adoption, safety, and recurrent VTE.
