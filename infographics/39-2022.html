NASP 2022 / Poster 39-2022

Real-world IV edaravone use was associated with longer survival in ALS.

A retrospective Optum Clinformatics Data Mart analysis matched adults with ALS who initiated IV edaravone to non-IV edaravone controls, then compared all-cause mortality and median survival through March 31, 2021.

Evidence flow

Claims data were filtered, matched, and tested for survival signal.

1

Start with Optum CDM

Administrative claims from a geographically diverse database with more than 65 million unique lives over 13 years.

2

Identify ALS adults

Adults ≥18 years with ALS diagnosis codes and either IV edaravone initiation or no IV edaravone treatment.

3

Match cohorts

Nearest-neighbor 1:1 propensity score matching balanced demographics, disease duration, insurance, riluzole, and clinical markers.

4

Model survival

Kaplan-Meier and log-rank testing visualized survival; shared-frailty Cox regression estimated hazard ratio.

5

Stress-test result

IPW sensitivity analysis evaluated potential unmeasured confounding and noninformative censoring assumptions.

Conclusion

Real-world survival findings complemented the pivotal functional-decline trial.

Patients with ALS prescribed IV edaravone demonstrated increased survival compared with matched patients not prescribed IV edaravone.

The authors connected this survival result with the phase 3 finding that IV edaravone slowed ALSFRS-R functional decline by 33% at 24 weeks compared with placebo.

IV edaravone median 29.5 months, 95% CI 25.4-35.9

Limitations

Claims data sharpened scale but constrained interpretation.

The study was limited to commercially covered patients and relied on administrative coding, enrollment, and claims-captured covariates.

Propensity matching adjusted measured differences, but unmeasured confounding could not be fully eliminated in a nonrandomized design.

Why it matters

Rare-disease real-world evidence can extend what short trials cannot show.

The authors framed the analysis as a complement to prospective trials, especially where rare diseases make survival endpoints difficult to evaluate in conventional clinical studies.
