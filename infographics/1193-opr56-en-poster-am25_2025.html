Health-system specialty pharmacists as early identifiers of hereditary ATTR amyloidosis

A single Ohio health-system quality improvement initiative used pharmacist EHR review, red-flag scoring, referral, provider education, and follow-up to surface patients at higher risk for hATTR amyloidosis.

Conclusion

Pharmacist-led surveillance converted diffuse red flags into actionable referrals.

The initiative increased disease awareness, genetic testing, and diagnosis. Sustained pharmacist education helped providers recognize high-risk patients and move patients through evaluation.

Because specialty pharmacists routinely combine medication expertise, longitudinal follow-up, and EHR risk assessment, the poster positions them as key interdisciplinary partners in rare-disease identification.

Pharmacist operating model

Four repeatable actions carried the intervention.

Assess

Review EHR histories for red-flag signs and symptoms in patients with carpal tunnel syndrome and progressive polyneuropathy.

Prioritize

Apply a scoring tool so higher-risk patients rise to the top of pharmacist follow-up lists.

Refer

Route high-scoring patients to providers in neurology or cardiology clinics for targeted evaluation.

Educate and follow

Continue provider education and track evaluation outcomes, including genetic test orders and completion.

Methods extracted from poster

Study structure

  • Single health-system QI initiative in Ohio.
  • Conducted from April 2024 to April 2025.
  • Random sample of 350 patients selected for baseline review.
  • Higher total red-flag scores indicated greater hATTR risk and prioritized referral.

Additional education effect

Beyond the referred cohort

  • Ongoing pharmacist education supported additional provider evaluation.
  • Six additional patients were evaluated, completed genetic testing, and fed into the diagnosis workflow.
  • The poster frames education as a sustained practice change rather than a one-time screen.
