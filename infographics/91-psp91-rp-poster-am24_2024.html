Evaluating the impact of a pharmacist in optimizing inflammatory bowel disease treatment

A retrospective chart review examined medication access and dose optimization for adults with moderately to severely active Crohn's disease initiated on risankizumab-rzaa between June 2022 and June 2024.

Why access support mattered

Advanced IBD therapy sits behind payer and cost barriers

Risankizumab-rzaa joined the IBD treatment pathway after its June 2022 Crohn's disease approval, but the poster frames access as a multi-step process shaped by prior authorization, step therapy, formulary exceptions, site-of-care requirements, and patient cost.

2Appeals, second-level appeals, and peer-to-peer review

Evidence flow

Chart review translated access work into measurable decision points

01

Identify eligible patients

Adults 19 years and older with moderately to severely active Crohn's disease initiated on risankizumab-rzaa.

02

Track request path

Medication request and approval dates were abstracted to quantify approval timing.

03

Capture payer friction

Prior authorization, appeals, second-level appeals, and peer-to-peer steps were recorded.

04

Measure optimization

Standard induction, SQ approval, escalation, re-induction, and time to escalation were planned outcomes.

Conclusion signal

Pharmacists are positioned as access navigators for refractory IBD care

The authors anticipate that fuller results will highlight the clinical specialty pharmacist's role in breaking down payer and affordability barriers so providers can optimize therapy before surgical intervention.

Interpretation guardrails

What the poster does and does not yet claim

The result section states that the project is currently in progress. The available findings support a descriptive access-and-escalation snapshot, not a completed effectiveness or approval-rate analysis.

  • Approval percentages and average approval time were planned primary outcomes.
  • Additional authorization steps were planned secondary outcomes.
  • The reported counts already show substantial escalation needs among a refractory IBD population.
