Methods flow

Criterion sampling, semi-structured collection, deductive CFIR coding

Recruit implementation insiders

All clinical pharmacists, IHSSP leaders, and physician champions involved in CPPA implementation were recruited regardless of data saturation.

Capture structured narratives

Two study team members conducted focus groups and interviews using a semi-structured guide developed from CFIR domains.

Map experience to CFIR

Recordings were transcribed from Microsoft Teams and deductively coded by two researchers in Dedoose.

Identified themes

The qualitative signal centered on fit, acceptance, and support

Patient care

Participants believed CPPAs shortened the path from treatment decision to specialty medication access and reduced repeated provider outreach.

"It helped us get the patient the drug sooner."

Acceptability

Clinics accepted pharmacist ordering and prescribing support as a practical extension of specialty medication management.

"Universally accepted by our group."

Compatibility

Existing pharmacist-physician relationships made CPPAs feel like an added authorization layer rather than a disruptive redesign.

"I was already very actively engaging and relying on a pharmacist."

Implementation support

Leadership, clinic leads, and resident support helped clinics handle logistics, education, and setup work.

"It would have been hard to do."

Conclusion

Integrated specialty pharmacy structure made CPPA adoption easier

Because pharmacists and pharmacy technicians were already embedded in clinics to manage specialty medications, CPPAs could be integrated into established workflows while improving perceived quality, efficiency, and team acceptance.
