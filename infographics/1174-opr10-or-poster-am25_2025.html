Evaluating pharmacist-led interventions focused on infection management in inflammatory conditions

WVU Medicine Specialty Pharmacy reviewed pharmacist interventions for patients receiving biologic or small molecule therapy, isolating how often active or suspected infections prompted management guidance.

Clinical premise

Immunomodulators create a recurring infection-management decision point.

Objective and methods

Count how often pharmacists intervene when infection risk appears.

Identify patients

Included psoriasis, psoriatic arthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, spondyloarthritis, and other inflammatory conditions.

Screen interventions

Selected records where pharmacist documentation mentioned suspected or confirmed infection.

Classify action

Grouped guidance into holding therapy, already-held therapy, infection follow-up, and not holding.

Map context

Compared intervention incidence across disease states and drug classes.

Acceptance signal 100%

Every pharmacist recommendation documented in the infection-management cohort was accepted, supporting the operational role of HSSP pharmacists during acute infection questions.

Conclusion

Specialty pharmacists functioned as a practical infection-management checkpoint.

The poster concludes that infection-related interventions were common enough to be operationally meaningful, and that accepted pharmacist guidance may help optimize care and prevent infection-related complications.

Limits and next questions

The signal is promising, but longer follow-up is needed.

Short review period: October 1 through December 31, 2024. No long-term clinical impact data after therapy holds. Future work: frequency of flares after holds, infection severity requiring holds, and outcomes when therapy is not held.
