Accreditation opens a small but high-value LDD channel.

Shields Health Solutions analyzed 25 accreditation-restricted limited distribution drugs across accredited health-system specialty pharmacies and found that a modest share of specialty volume carried a disproportionate revenue signal.

Methods

Retrospective fill-data analysis across accredited health-system specialty pharmacies.

Define the restricted set

Researchers identified 25 LDDs requiring specialty pharmacy accreditation to obtain access.

Aggregate dispensing data

All SHS Specialty Drug List medication fills from calendar years 2023 and 2024 were analyzed.

Limit to comparable sites

The cohort included 35 SHS-supported health-system pharmacies with at least one SP accreditation.

Measure volume and value

Primary endpoints were revenue, number of fills, and patients serviced, reported as weighted averages.

Note access caveats

Some LDDs may also have required dual accreditation or payor network participation.

Operational Meaning

Accreditation is an access infrastructure investment.

URAC or ACHC accreditation can position health-system specialty pharmacies to participate in payor and LDD networks, expanding access to medications that may otherwise sit outside the integrated-care model.

Study Boundaries

Network rules still matter.

The analysis did not independently stratify dual accreditation or payor-specific participation requirements, so the observed signal should be read as accreditation-associated access rather than accreditation alone.

Conclusion

Small volume, meaningful patient access, outsized financial importance.

Accreditation-restricted LDDs accounted for a small percentage of total specialty dispensing but nearly 10% of revenue, supporting the authors' conclusion that accreditation helps health-system SPs expand access to high-value medications.

  • Strategic value: LDD access can extend integrated specialty pharmacy care to more patients while preserving a high-value revenue stream.
  • Patient-care context: the poster cites evidence that integrated health-system specialty pharmacy care can reduce total cost of care by 13%.
  • Future analyses: stratify outcomes by accreditation type, payor network rules, and pre- versus post-accreditation access.
