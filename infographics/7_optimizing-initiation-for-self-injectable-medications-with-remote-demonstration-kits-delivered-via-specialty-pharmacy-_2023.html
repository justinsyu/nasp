NASP 2023 exploratory pilot

Optimizing initiation for self-injectable medications with remote demonstration kits

A specialty pharmacy distributed Remote Demonstration Kits (RDKs) with the first dose shipment to test whether at-home practice could accelerate first injection and support early adherence.

  • Self-injection training
  • Specialty pharmacy
  • Randomized SoC vs SoC + RDK
  • 14-day biologic dosing schedule
2023
Authors
Glynn, Greenwood, Prajapati, Xu, McLeroy, Reynolds
Organizations
ZS Associates; Noble International

RDK contents included IFU, an injection demonstration device, and a video shipped with the first dose.

Study design

Specialty pharmacy as the training kit delivery channel

Eligible patients

Naive to self-injection and prescribed one of three biologics with a 14-day dosing schedule.

Randomized arms

Patients were equally assigned to standard of care or standard of care plus RDK.

Same-day shipment

The RDK was shipped by specialty pharmacy on the same day as the patient's first medication dose.

PRO capture

Patient-reported outcomes collected the first self-injection date and treatment experience.

Refill signals

Pharmacy refill data informed adherence and persistence endpoints across early therapy.

Speed to first dose

First injection happened sooner with RDK support

SoC only 8.8 average script-to-injection days
SoC + RDK 5.4 average script-to-injection days

Reported one-tailed p value: 0.08 at the 0.1 confidence level.

Good PDC was defined as >80%; persistence excluded patients discharged between refills due to co-pay and side effects.

Treatment group experience

Patients used and reused the kit

  • 88% used either the training device or video for first self-injection.
  • 88% reused the RDK for re-orientation during later doses.
  • 94% had a positive perception of the kit.

Interpretation

Promising signal, early evidence base

The poster concluded that specialty pharmacy-delivered RDKs may help patients initiate self-injectable therapy faster and improve short-term adherence.

  • Small sample size warrants larger follow-up studies.
  • Script-to-injection days were unavailable when the first injection date was missing from survey data.
  • The pilot's short duration limited conclusions about longer-term adherence impact.
