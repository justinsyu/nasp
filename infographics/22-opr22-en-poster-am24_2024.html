Collaborative Practice Agreements in an Integrated Health-System Specialty Pharmacy

A Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy retrospective cohort analysis asked whether clinic-specific CPAs reduced short-window specialty medication represcriptions and shifted prescribing work to pharmacists.

Objective

Did CPAs reduce specialty medication represcriptions?

The study evaluated whether CPA implementation lowered the rate of represcriptions, defined as another prescription from the same clinic for the same patient and any specialty medication within 14 days of the index prescription.

Single center Retrospective cohort Clinic-specific index dates 14-day represcription window
Primary result No significant reduction

Represcription rates were 38% before CPA implementation and 41% after implementation, with p=0.445.

Conclusion

Short-term reduction was not observed

  • No reduction in represcriptions was seen during the first 6 months after CPA implementation.
  • Early post-implementation workflows were still stabilizing, with internal miscommunication noted among post-CPA reasons.
  • Longer follow-up is needed to understand whether CPAs reduce represcribing burden over time.

What the data suggests

CPAs may shift work before they change rates

The short-term primary outcome was neutral, but pharmacist prescribing volume shows CPAs can expand pharmacist participation in specialty medication management. The poster frames future assessment around longer-term workflow maturation rather than immediate rate change.
