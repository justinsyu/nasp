Influence of seasonal changes on adherence in rheumatoid arthritis patients

CVS Health researchers studied whether northern-hemisphere seasons influenced medication adherence among U.S. adults newly diagnosed with rheumatoid arthritis and initiating subcutaneous or oral therapy.

Methods

A claims-based cohort was stratified into meteorological seasons and modeled with adjusted linear regression.

Identify RA cohort

Adults with newly diagnosed RA, defined using ICD-10 codes M05.X and M06.X.

Capture therapy starts

Subcutaneous or oral RA medications included TNF-alpha, JAK, IL-6, T-cell blocker, and folate analog classes.

Assign seasons

Medication history was grouped into spring, summer, fall, and winter by northern-hemisphere calendar dates.

Measure adherence

Monthly PDC was calculated between the first and last fill across the year after therapy start.

Model effects

Linear regression estimated seasonality effects, with p-values below 0.05 considered significant.

Drug-class sensitivity

Winter effects were not uniform: selected therapies showed stronger seasonal drops.

TNF-alpha inhibitor -0.03

Winter beta, p<0.001; spring also lower than fall with beta -0.04.

JAK inhibitor -0.02

Winter beta was not statistically significant, p=0.30.

IL-6 inhibitor -0.14

Winter beta, p=0.04; the largest reported winter coefficient by class.

T-cell blocker -0.11

Winter beta, p<0.001; predicted PDC declined from 81 to 79.

Folate analog -0.02

Winter beta, p=0.01; smaller but statistically significant decline.

Climate stratification

The negative winter association was limited to humid and cold climate regions.

Region-stratified regression showed winter was significant in humid and cold regions, but not dry or marine regions.

Season hypothesis

RA symptoms are suspected to be linked with exposure to colder environments.

Adherence gap

Prior seasonality studies focused on RA activity rather than medication adherence.

Winter dip

Predicted PDC reached its lowest value in winter, and winter was significant in the adjusted model.

Class matters

TNF-alpha, IL-6, T-cell blocker, and folate analog results showed significant seasonal findings.

Region matters

Humid and cold climate strata carried the observed negative winter association.

Conclusion

Medication adherence decreased in winter months, but seasonality depended on class and context.

The authors concluded that seasonality influences adherence for specific seasons, with drug-specific effects rather than a uniform decline across all RA therapies.

Poster details

Presented at NASP 2023 in Grapevine, Texas.

Authors included Cody Schmidt, Kunal Patel, Samantha Delgado, Guy Cozzi, Elisea Avalos-Reyes, W. Cliff Rutter, Rashmi Grover, Lucia Feczko, Will Cavers, and Kjel Johnson.

Takeaway: in a 3,710-patient RA cohort, winter was the only season with a significant overall PDC decrease, and the practical signal was strongest for selected therapy classes and for patients living in humid or cold climates.
