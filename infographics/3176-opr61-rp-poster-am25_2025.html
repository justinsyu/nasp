Validation of a Pharmacist Monitoring Tool for Patients with Inflammatory Bowel Disease

UW Health is testing whether the pharmacist-built gastro-intestinal metric (GIM) Score can standardize specialty pharmacy assessments, identify suboptimal medication efficacy, and align with physician assessment of IBD control.

Objective

Validate a pharmacist-specific IBD monitoring score

Compare baseline GIM Score assessments with physician office-visit assessments, then estimate a cutoff for IBD stability and evaluate responsiveness when disease activity changes.

Disease activity signals

  • General well-being
  • Abdominal pain
  • Bowel frequency
  • Blood in stool
  • Symptom attempts

Medication signals

  • Work/school productivity
  • Next dose awareness
  • Missed doses
  • Adverse effects

Methods

Prospective cohort embedded in specialty pharmacy follow-up

Enroll

Recruited through MyChart and phone, consented, flagged, and added to a study episode.

Baseline GIM

Study team administered the GIM Score by phone within one week of the GI office visit.

Provider visit

GI assessment captured questionnaire data, medication changes, labs, drug levels, and procedures.

Follow-up

Repeat GIM Score at 8 weeks for stable patients or 4 weeks for unknown stability.

Eligibility frame

Study population required GI linkage, adherence, and specialty pharmacy participation

Included Excluded
Established with a UW Health gastroenterologist and office visit scheduled during the study period. Pediatric patients younger than 18 years.
GI prescriber for adalimumab or biosimilar, certolizumab, ustekinumab, risankizumab, tofacitinib, upadacitinib, or ozanimod. Pregnant patients or prisoners.
Adherent to IBD medication with PDC greater than 0.7 and enrolled in the UW Health Specialty Pharmacy Medication Management Program. Patients unable to complete required clinic or follow-up assessment steps.

Discussion

Operational lessons from an embedded monitoring study

  • Recruitment integration into specialty pharmacy workflows supported enrollment.
  • Collaboration with gastroenterology clinic providers and staff increased research visibility.
  • Limited GI clinic access created a barrier to gathering office-visit data.
  • Patient engagement may require telephone, EHR messaging, and letters rather than one contact channel.

Conclusion signal

A pharmacist lens can make IBD monitoring more medication-aware

The poster frames the GIM Score as a practical validation target: physician tools help assess disease state activity, while a pharmacist-specific tool adds medication efficacy, adherence, adverse-effect, and follow-up signals needed for specialty medication optimization.
