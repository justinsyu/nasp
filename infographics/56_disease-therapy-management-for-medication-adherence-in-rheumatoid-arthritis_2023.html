NASP 2023 poster 56

Disease therapy management for medication adherence in rheumatoid arthritis

A prospective randomized trial tested whether pharmacist-led specialty pharmacy support improved adherence for adults with rheumatoid arthritis starting self-injectable biologic therapy.

Methods

1:1:1 Patients were assigned across three pharmacy care models after insurance-based routing and randomization.

Enrollment: October 2019 to September 2022.

Three levels of pharmacy support

External specialty pharmacy

Standard of care varied by outside pharmacy when insurance required external biologic dispensing.

Integrated pharmacy standard care

Pharmacist calls before therapy and first refill, followed by technician refill outreach at month 3.

Face-to-face plus monthly calls

Baseline counseling, medication reconciliation, adherence barrier discussion, injection training, and monthly follow-up calls.

Interpretation pattern

Adherence improved with integrated dispensing

Patients filling injectable biologics through the integrated health-system specialty pharmacy were more adherent than patients filling externally.

Virtual and face-to-face models performed similarly

Groups 2 and 3 had comparable outcomes across tested endpoints, suggesting value from the integrated model itself.

Clinical improvement may need larger studies

Disease activity markers numerically improved, but clinical index comparisons did not reach observed between-group differences.

Conclusion

Pharmacy-based disease therapy management strengthened biologic adherence and satisfaction.

Up-front education and closer follow-up through an integrated specialty pharmacy program may help streamline access, address medication barriers, and improve care outcomes for patients with rheumatoid arthritis.
