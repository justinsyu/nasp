14-day pharmacist check-ins were associated with fewer oral anticancer therapy discontinuations.

A retrospective multicenter Trellis Rx partner health-system study compared oral anticancer agent discontinuation before and after a technology-facilitated pharmacist contact protocol within 14 days of therapy initiation.

Objective

Compare discontinuation before and after pharmacist-led early contact.

Patients starting oral anticancer agents were evaluated for discontinuation rates and reasons before and after implementation of a protocol requiring pharmacist contact within 14 days of therapy initiation.

Early access turned pharmacist support into a defined intervention window.

Targeted reasons contracted

Discontinuations for drug intolerance, patient choice, or unreachable patient decreased from 513 to 220.

Adverse effects were actionable

Follow-up created a place to manage side effects before they became therapy-stopping events.

HSSP reach mattered

The protocol paired clinical counseling with technology-supported tracking across partner health systems.

Secondary patterns shifted

Interaction-related discontinuations rose as documented reasons, suggesting additional pharmacist intervention points.

Conclusion

HSSP pharmacists can reduce OAA discontinuation by embedding early counseling and mitigation into launch workflows.

Methods

Retrospective, multicenter, observational comparison.

Patients were stratified into pre-protocol and post-protocol cohorts, and discontinuation reasons were taken from pharmacist documentation in the Arbor specialty pharmacy technology platform.

Primary endpoints focused on overall discontinuation plus drug intolerance, patient choice, and patient unreachable: the reasons most directly influenced by pharmacist intervention.

Takeaway

Early pharmacist contact gave adherence work a measurable operating model.

By contacting patients soon after therapy start, HSSP pharmacists could identify side effects, arrange supportive care, answer questions, and involve providers before preventable discontinuations occurred.

Authors noted that further study is warranted to continue defining HSSP and pharmacist impact on oral oncolytic therapy outcomes.
