Building a Unique Specialty Pharmacy Rotation

A Twin Cities health system converted a patient-care elective into a structured specialty pharmacy APPE, giving students four focused weeks across infectious disease, oncology, and general specialty clinics with repeated clinical presentations and feedback.

Objective

Review implementation, structure, student feedback, and educational pearls from the 2020-2021 academic year.

The poster frames specialty pharmacy APPEs as a way to build foundational skills, foster interest in specialty pharmacy, and improve the quality of future specialty pharmacists.

COVID-19 compressed the design into a four-week experience, so the team emphasized deliberate onboarding, multiple practice sites, weekly deliverables, and repeated preceptor feedback.

Learner journey

From baseline goals to clinic immersion

Rotation structure

A four-week clinic path balanced breadth, repetition, and culminating presentation practice.

Infectious disease

HIV, HCV, HBV, latent TB infection, and antifungals. Deliverables: journal club and semiformal patient case.

Oncology

Oral oncolytics and hematology. Deliverables: journal club and semiformal patient case.

General specialty

Cardiology, MS, rheumatology, dermatology, GI, endocrinology, HAE, ALS, and other disease states.

General specialty

Continued broad specialty exposure. Deliverables culminated in a formal patient care presentation.

Onboarding first week SP definitions, business models, reimbursement, LDDs, payer access, accreditation, safety, and reporting.
Feedback Friday Weekly noon webinar presentations with preceptor questions and follow-up assignments.
Daily clinical tasks Clinical questions, chart review, patient status checks, lab data, communications, and therapy appropriateness.

Evidence flow

What the poster says made the rotation effective

1. Start with baseline goals

Students, except the first, completed a survey on specialty pharmacy background and personal learning goals.

2. Teach core SP context

Onboarding introduced specialty medications, payer access, business models, clinical programs, accreditation, and safety reporting.

3. Rotate across disease states

Students spent at least one-week intervals with different preceptors and specialty practice sites.

4. Repeat high-value work

Weekly journal clubs and case presentations created rhythm, feedback loops, and interview-ready formal presentation practice.

5. Keep careers central

Preceptors made career goals the focal point of clinical discussions and students reported growing interest in the field.

Educational pearls

Design choices reduced preceptor burden while increasing learner consistency.

1

Share oversight

Precepting pharmacists appreciated the balance of shared student oversight and cross-specialty clinical discussion.

2

Standardize articles

Using the same high-value journal club articles across rotations promoted consistent learning.

3

Structure discussion

Students valued structured discussions, which also eased time burden for preceptors.

Student comments repeatedly described the rotation as challenging, productive, and interest-building because it combined weekly presentations, multiple specialty areas, and enthusiastic preceptors.

Conclusion

A patient-care APPE was a strong fit for a health system specialty pharmacy setting.

The authors conclude that the experience was "incredibly successful," with overwhelmingly positive feedback despite most students entering with little specialty pharmacy experience.

They recommend similar programs for health systems with specialty pharmacy services to foster specialty pharmacy interest and sharpen student clinical acumen.
