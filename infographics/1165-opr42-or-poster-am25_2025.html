Transforming Rare Disease Management: Real-World Impact of an Integrated Health System Specialty Pharmacy Services

Yale New Haven Health reviewed 2024 prescriptions for mavacamten and Wilson disease therapies to show how an integrated health system specialty pharmacy supports access, turnaround time, financial assistance, and high-touch pharmacist services.

High-touch specialty pharmacy services Access work plus clinical monitoring formed the rare-disease support model.

Specialty pharmacy liaisons helped with prior authorizations, appeals, and prescription processing, while pharmacists provided longitudinal assessments and medication therapy interventions.

Methods

Prescription processing was split into clean fills versus fills requiring intervention

Population

Patients receiving mavacamten for cardiomyopathy or trientine and penicillamine for Wilson disease.

Classification

Dispenses were clean unless clinical or insurance issues occurred during prescription processing.

Primary outcome

Evaluate specialty clinical pharmacist services for rare disease management.

Secondary outcomes

Evaluate turnaround time and financial assistance.

Background

Rare disease medications require close monitoring, coordination, and sometimes REMS-related management.

Access pressure

Complex payor models, carve-outs, deductibles, and limited distribution drugs can hinder specialty medication access.

Financial assistance

Twenty-six patients received assistance, representing about 22% of the combined cohort.

Service signal

High-touch pharmacist assessments, interventions, and refill outreach aligned with very high therapy persistence.

Conclusion

The integrated HSSP high-touch model was described as suited for navigating rare disease patient management.

Limitations

Measurement caveats

Turnaround time definitions are not standardized across specialty pharmacies, and health systems may not have access to limited distribution rare disease medications.

Future direction

Access to limited distribution drugs

The poster argues that patients with rare diseases need easier treatment access and resources, and that integrated HSSP access to limited distribution medications may improve outcomes.
