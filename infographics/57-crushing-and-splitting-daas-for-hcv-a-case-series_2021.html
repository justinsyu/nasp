Crushing and Splitting DAAs for HCV Treatment: A Case Series

Three academic health systems reported real-world HCV treatment cases where direct-acting antiviral tablets were crushed, halved, or quartered for patients unable to swallow intact tablets.

Objective

Describe safety and effectiveness when HCV DAA tablets cannot be taken intact.

DAAs commonly produce SVR rates above 90%, but evidence is limited for patients who need tablets crushed, split, mixed with food, or administered through a PEG tube.

Methods

A focused chart review across three specialty pharmacy practice settings.

Clinical context

Reasons and comorbidity context shaped administration

Pattern Count Interpretive note
Head and neck cancer history 6 Most common driver of swallowing difficulty.

Evidence flow

From swallowing barrier to measured virologic response

1. Identify barrier

Patients needed altered administration because intact DAA tablets were not feasible.

2. Select regimen

GLE/PIB, LDV/SOF, SOF/VEL, or SOF/VEL + RBV were used according to clinical context.

3. Manipulate tablet

Tablets were crushed, halved, or quartered, then delivered by mouth, vehicle, or PEG tube.

4. Monitor response

All cases with available on-treatment and end-of-treatment labs were undetectable.

5. Confirm safety

No severe adverse effects were observed; unpleasant taste was reported.

Conclusion: this small real-world case series supports the safety and effectiveness of HCV DAA tablet manipulation when swallowing barriers require crushing, splitting, or tube administration, while highlighting the need to preserve follow-up through SVR12.
