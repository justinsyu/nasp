NASP 2020 | Poster-33

Implementation of an enhanced oral chemotherapy care model to prevent medication errors

A hospital-owned specialty pharmacy built a refill safeguard for high-risk cyclic oral chemotherapy: flag the patient, review labs before dispensing, intervene with prescribers when risk appears, and repeat the check before each cycle.

Evidence flow

A refill protocol that treats each cycle as a fresh safety gate

Flag high-risk OC starts

New common cyclic oral chemotherapy starts were marked "High-Risk" in the pharmacy profile.

Check labs before fill

Pharmacists reviewed available electronic medical record labs against reference values and missing records.

Intervene when risk appears

Concerns triggered physician interventions, prescriber messages, care-team email summaries, or refill timing changes.

Repeat before next cycle

A cyclic pre-screen encounter was set before subsequent dispensations, with outreach and education as needed.

Objective

Reduce oral chemotherapy medication errors

The primary objective was to reduce medication errors with oral chemotherapy by increasing safeguards around refills in a hospital-owned specialty pharmacy.

  • OC errors can occur during storage, dosing, prescribing, preparation, dispensing, or administration.
  • Patients self-administer therapy at home, so refill decisions need current clinical context.
  • Lab review, routine in infusion and inpatient chemotherapy, was brought into outpatient OC refill activity.

Methods

Workgroup response to a medication error

  • Convened a workgroup to eliminate risks tied to patients possessing chemotherapy when continuation was inappropriate.
  • Reviewed literature and national guidance to model best-practice OC care components.
  • Identified adult cancer patients on high-risk cyclic oral chemotherapy from Feb 17 to Jun 19, 2020.
  • Used Lexicomp, Micromedex, and pharmacokinetic information to identify drug interactions.

Conclusion

Electronic record access was central to pharmacy safety work

Integrating laboratory review before oral chemotherapy dispensing caught errors in one-fifth of cases and supported safer continuation decisions for self-administered oncology therapy.

Next iteration

Automated high-risk categorization

Further workflow enhancements, including automatic "High-Risk" categorization, were ongoing to improve continuity of care and strengthen pharmacist impact on patient safety. The authors noted that a larger sample is needed to better evaluate ongoing prescreen compliance.
