Specialty pharmacist engagement as a migraine outcomes engine

A Froedtert and Medical College of Wisconsin prospective cohort study asks whether structured pharmacist-patient contact during the first six months of CGRP monoclonal antibody therapy changes intervention volume, migraine burden, daily function, adherence, cost support, and urgent migraine-related care.

Objective

Measure whether added pharmacist contact translates into clinical signal

The primary objective is to analyze the impact of increased pharmacist-patient contact by counting interventions during the first six months after CGRP monoclonal antibody initiation.

The secondary objective is to evaluate changes in migraine frequency and missed school, work, or usual daily activities from baseline through six months.

Care model

Specialty pharmacy work sits between access, safety, coaching, and refills

Access and affordability

Prior authorizations, financial assistance, and coordination for high-cost CGRP monoclonal antibodies.

Medication management

Interaction review, administration counseling, and personalized support as part of the integrated care team.

Outcome capture

Structured calls document migraine days, missed activities, adherence, interventions, outreach, and care utilization.

Methods

Prospective single-center cohort: new CGRP starts followed through month six

Inclusion pattern

  • New to subcutaneous CGRP monoclonal antibody therapy: erenumab, fremanezumab, or galcanezumab.
  • Prescribed by a Froedtert Hospital Neurology Clinic provider.
  • Migraine ICD-10 code G43.XXX documented in the electronic medical record.
  • Adult patients, age 18 years or older.

Results framework

The poster defines six evidence streams for pharmacist impact

1

Interventions

Number and type of medication therapy problem interventions from initial evaluation to six-month call.

2

Migraine days

Average migraine days compared at enrollment, month 3, and month 6.

3

Function

Missed days of school, work, or usual daily activities over the prior month.

4

Adherence

Average adherence measured by proportion of days covered.

5

Access support

Cost assistance provided and patient outreach completed by pharmacists.

6

Acute care

Migraine-related clinic visits and emergency department visits after therapy initiation.

Conclusion

Clinical impact will be read through pharmacist activity plus patient-reported migraine burden

This poster positions the neurology specialty pharmacist as an outcomes touchpoint for patients initiating CGRP monoclonal antibodies, with a six-month framework to connect interventions, adherence, affordability support, migraine frequency, daily function, and acute-care utilization.

Future direction

Next refinement targets

  • Develop standardized pharmacist intervention protocols.
  • Integrate patient-reported outcome measures into routine specialty care.
  • Analyze cost-effectiveness of specialty pharmacy interventions.
  • Continue follow-up beyond six months to assess sustained benefit.
