Goal range: National Osteoporosis Foundation materials were used to define recommended daily calcium intake by age and gender, generally 1000-1200 mg/day.

Evidence flow

A specialty pharmacist and dietitian turned a phone assessment into a tailored calcium plan.

Identify patients

Targeted adults receiving osteoporosis therapy or medications associated with bone loss, including denosumab, elagolix, leuprorelin acetate, and chronic prednisone.

Estimate intake

Telephonic interviews combined dietary calcium estimates with supplement use using a validated dietary intake tool.

Screen risk

Medication history captured PPI, H2RA, levothyroxine, and supplement issues; FRAX was calculated for patients older than 40.

Mail guidance

Patients received a cover letter with progress, FRAX results, NOF calcium materials, supplement advice, and food coupons.

Follow up

At 3-6 months, staff assessed behavior change and whether patients reached their goal calcium intake.

Medication-related problems

The intervention identified supplement timing, formulation, and label-comprehension issues.

7/7 Levothyroxine patients using calcium separated doses by at least 1 hour.
3/6 Patients on calcium carbonate with PPI/H2RA changed to calcium citrate.
3 Patients misread supplement labels where 3 tablets equaled a serving.
1 Patient with poor intake was referred to the prescriber.

Objective

Increase the share of targeted specialty pharmacy patients reaching calcium RDA by 20%.

The primary outcome tested whether collaboration between specialty pharmacy and a registered dietitian-nutritionist in a grocery chain model could move patients to 1000-1200 mg/day.

Secondary outcomes included awareness of medication risk for bone mineral density loss, medication-related problem resolution, and coupon tracking.

Lessons learned

Patients moved toward goal, but prescriber counseling and nutrition infrastructure remained gaps.

  • Only 38% reported that prescribers discussed calcium and vitamin D intake.
  • Ten percent of at-risk patients reported less than 200 mg/day of dietary calcium.
  • Coupon redemption suggested patient interest in food-based calcium support.
  • The authors noted an opportunity for a calcium-focused grocery shelf-tag program.
