NASP 2019 | Poster 12

Development of a Pharmacy Collaborative Practice Agreement in a Renal Transplant Clinic

Vanderbilt's renal transplant clinic used a pharmacist-physician Collaborative Pharmacy Practice Agreement to move routine immunosuppressant refill authority closer to the specialty pharmacy team while tracking workload, growth, and safety.

Implementation pathway

From state authority to clinic execution

Jul 2014 Tennessee pharmacy and medical associations finalized CPPA legislation.
Jul 2015 Vanderbilt Renal Transplant Clinic and Transplant Pharmacy developed a CPPA.
Dec 2015 VUMC P&T Committee and Medical Board approved the proposed agreement.
Nov 2016 VUMC legal defined CPPA requirements and auditing needs.
Feb 2017 State Board of Pharmacy and Attorney General approved the CPPA.
Mar 2017 CPPA initiated inside the renal transplant clinic.

Objective and cohort

Test whether pharmacist prescribing authority improved clinic efficiency

Objective: Assess the impact of a pharmacist-physician CPPA on prescription volume, clinic workload, and safety outcomes.

Method: Three 7-month intervals compared immunosuppressant prescriptions authorized by pharmacists, nurses, and physicians before pharmacists, prior to CPPA, and after CPPA establishment.

Included

  • Adults 18 years and older
  • Post renal transplant follow-up
  • At least one immunosuppressant prescription in all intervals

Excluded

  • Failed renal transplant
  • Discharged from the transplant clinic
  • Re-transplanted during the study window

Prescription volume and staffing

Growth continued while pharmacy capacity expanded

Quarterly filled-prescription pattern is extracted from the poster's volume table; final team growth is summarized in the conclusion as 8 to 24 team members.

Need

Post-transplant medication regimens are complex and frequently adjusted to preserve graft and patient health.

Agreement

The clinic implemented a formal CPPA that defined pharmacist prescribing authority and audit expectations.

Shift

Once established, pharmacists authorized 85% of immunosuppressant refills, reducing physician and nurse burden.

Scale

Prescription volume and specialty pharmacy staffing increased alongside the streamlined refill process.

Safety

Seven quarterly Veritas safety reviews reported no safety concerns after CPPA implementation.

Measured signals

Conclusion

CPPA implementation concentrated refill work in pharmacy without a reported safety signal

The poster concluded that the agreement increased prescription volume, reduced physician and nurse burden, supported pharmacy growth from 8 to 24 team members, and produced no safety concerns across seven quarterly reviews.

The authors positioned the model as a streamlined path from prescribing to dispensing that allows closer post-transplant monitoring while freeing physicians and nurses to focus on patient care.
