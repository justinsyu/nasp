2023 poster 61 | Specialty pharmacy performance improvement

Achieving Quality Pharmacy Care for HIV Post-Exposure Prophylaxis Therapy

Price Chopper Specialty Pharmacy evaluated a centralized workflow designed to make HIV PEP prescriptions ready for local pickup inside the CDC-recommended 72-hour window after possible exposure.

Central question

Can a specialty pharmacy workflow protect the 72-hour PEP window?

72 hr CDC start window
4 missed goal cases

Objective and method

Performance improvement built around prescription readiness

  • 1 Identify PEP prescriptions as urgent. Intake staff reviewed product availability, insurance on file, and missing context from the prescription.
  • 2 Clarify clinical and access barriers. Specialty pharmacists checked regimens, pediatric dosing, days supplied at point of care, and prescriber notes.
  • 3 Coordinate pickup and counseling. Patient contact documented counseling, barriers, and care plan completion in the specialty pharmacy platform.
  • 4 Measure the 72-hour endpoint. The program documented whether the prescription was ready for local pharmacy pickup before the window closed.

Evidence flow

A centralized workflow connected specialty review to local access

Intake PCC

Enter and flag

Enter the prescription, check product availability and insurance information, and notify the pharmacist that the PEP prescription is urgent.

Intake SP RPh

Clinical review

Complete DUR and clarify regimen, days supply, pediatric weight or dose, and PEP indication when information is missing.

Insurance PCC

Remove payment friction

Review copay, financial assistance, and worker compensation coordination to prevent access delays.

Patient contact

Counsel and document

Complete outreach, review indication and point-of-care supply, counsel the patient or guardian, and complete the care plan.

Primary finding 36 / 40

Most patients met the medication-readiness goal, but the program exposed care barriers that simple fill metrics would miss.

Barriers surfaced by the PIP

The workflow found operational issues and communication risks

Missing prescription context

Thirty-seven prescriptions lacked nPEP/oPEP details, forcing staff to reconstruct exposure context under time pressure.

Clarifications and full bottles

Twenty-six pharmacist clarifications included 18 cases where medication required a 30-day full bottle and 3 pediatric dose checks.

Worker compensation delays

Four worker compensation billing delays created risk that therapy would start after 72 hours unless medication was supplied before claim approval.

Trauma-informed care needs

Staff reported difficulty discussing traumatic exposure events and a need for training, referral resources, and self-care support.

Conclusion and next steps

Readiness improved, but sustainable quality needs better upstream information

Improve prescriber notes

Add nPEP vs oPEP, exposure date and time, pediatric weight, and days supply given at point of care directly in e-prescribing notes.

Align discharge instructions

Ensure written plans explain multi-medication therapy and include both brand and generic names to reduce confusion at pickup.

Train the pharmacy team

Use multidisciplinary education to support trauma-informed conversations, adolescent privacy knowledge, referrals, and routine self-care.
