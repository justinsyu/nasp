NASP Annual Meeting 2023 / Poster 14

Impact of Integrating a Pharmacy Liaison-Managed Care Model within a Diabetes Population

Berkshire Health Systems and Shields Health Solutions evaluated a diabetes pharmacy liaison model designed to reduce access friction, coordinate refills, and support adherence for patients managed through adult endocrinology clinics.

Primary clinical signal

Average HbA1c moved from 8.7% baseline to an estimated 7.9% after enrollment.

The poster links the magnitude of HbA1c reduction to potential implications for total medical expenditures.

Cohort profile

The evaluated group was older, long-engaged, and heavily supported with CGM.

Methods

Adult endocrinology clinics embedded a liaison-managed medication workflow.

Oct 2018 Integrated diabetes medication management model was implemented in target adult endocrinology clinics at Berkshire Health Systems.
Apr 2022 Observed outcomes window began for the diabetes pharmacy model.
Mar 2023 Outcomes data were assessed through patients on service by March 2023.
6 months HbA1c change was measured from up to 60 days before onboarding through 6 months after enrollment.

Liaison care model

Access work happened before and after the prescription reached the pharmacy.

  • Investigate pharmacy and medical benefits, complete prior authorizations, and identify financial assistance.
  • Document approved prior authorizations and alert the prescriber of the preferred pharmacy.
  • Educate patients on pharmacy options, copays, and delivery.
  • Coordinate monthly medication and durable medical equipment refills.

Background need

Diabetes medication management creates practical barriers that a liaison can directly address.

The poster notes that patients with diabetes face medication affordability issues, adherence barriers, and disease-management complexity. Published systematic reviews cited by the authors report wide adherence ranges for insulin and oral antidiabetic drugs.

The liaison model was positioned as a health-system workflow for improving access and refill continuity around complex diabetes therapy.

Conclusion

The model was associated with positive access, adherence, affordability, and HbA1c signals.

  • High PDC, quick prior authorization turnaround, and lower medication copays suggested improved medication access and continuity.
  • The 0.8% HbA1c reduction in the study cohort may have implications for total medical expenditures.
  • The authors concluded that the model can be adapted to other health systems to simplify care and improve outcomes for patients with diabetes.
