Utilizing a Transdisciplinary Team to Enhance PEP Patient Care Readiness

Price Chopper Specialty Pharmacy and academic partners interviewed frontline pharmacy teammates to identify what delays, emotional demands, and workflow constraints stand between a time-sensitive PEP prescription and confident trauma-informed patient care.

Why readiness matters

PEP care is both urgent and emotionally complex.

The poster frames PEP as a compressed workflow: patients should start within 72 hours, continue therapy for 28 days, and may arrive at the pharmacy with incomplete prescription details, insurance friction, trauma histories, or a proxy speaking on their behalf.

Objective to method

A transdisciplinary team used interviews to convert frontline friction into training content.

Recruit

PCSP teammates received an information sheet, consent language, scheduling details, and optional gift card information.

Interview

Recorded Zoom interviews explored barriers, facilitators, background experience, and reactions to a PEP call flow.

Code

Two investigators piloted a coding framework and analyzed deidentified transcripts using bounded rationality.

Build

Interview themes informed a toolkit for sensitive PEP conversations, prescriber outreach, and patient support.

System interactions

Clinical and policy handoffs create ambiguity.

  • Emergency departments, urgent care, and primary care enter the path at different points.
  • Prescription notes may omit exposure timing, days supplied, or nPEP versus oPEP context.
  • Insurance, Medicaid, or workers' compensation issues can delay dispensing.
Personal constraints

Calls require trauma-informed judgment.

  • Patients may be distressed, delayed, or using a proxy to communicate.
  • Staff described stress from difficult calls, volume, and time-intensive insurance work.
  • Participants wanted training for sexual assault survivors and other specific populations.
Organizational constraints

Workflow details can break continuity.

  • Medication may not be in stock or available through the receiving pharmacy network.
  • Patients may not answer calls from a deidentified pharmacy phone number.
  • Passing patients between providers after trauma disclosure can undermine support.

Facilitators inform process

Participants pointed to five practical fixes that connect prescribers, pharmacy staff, and patients.

Better Rx notation Time of exposure, days supplied, PEP type, and counseling status on the prescription.
Real-time stock network Know where PEP medications are available before patients are redirected.
Public awareness Raise community awareness that PEP exists and is time sensitive.
After-hours plan Improve navigation for late Friday calls and other low-support windows.
Resource numbers Equip the pharmacy team with support contacts for patient needs beyond dispensing.

Toolkit content identified

Training needs clustered into three lanes.

Prescriber development

  • Patient experience obtaining a PEP prescription.
  • Pharmacy experience providing PEP.
  • Ways to improve prescription completeness and starter supply handoffs.

Pharmacy team development

  • Trauma-informed care and self-care.
  • Communication and privacy with adolescents and young adults.
  • Team training, prescriber outreach, and in-stock pharmacy networks.

Patient support

  • PEP information for varied indications.
  • PrEP education for prevention continuity.
  • SANE and other support materials and resources.

Conclusion

Interviews translated readiness concerns into toolkit requirements.

PCSP teammates identified barriers and facilitators that can be used to improve the PEP care process, with emphasis on prescriber outreach, clearer prescription information, pharmacy team training, and patient support resources.

Limitations

The poster frames the findings as local and exploratory.

The project used a small sample from one specialty pharmacy, and the authors noted that some underserved populations were not represented in participant discussions.

Bottom line: a PEP specialty pharmacy toolkit must solve for time pressure, trauma-sensitive communication, prescription completeness, and real-world access logistics in one workflow.
