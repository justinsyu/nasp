Predicting Time to Medication Access for Hematologic Malignancies

Vanderbilt compared oral anti-neoplastic access time across limited distribution drugs and integrated specialty pharmacy fills, then tested whether embedding a clinic pharmacist could shorten the LDD path from treatment decision to medication shipment.

Objective

Test whether pharmacist integration compressed the LDD access interval

The poster asked two linked questions: how LDD access time compared with non-LDD access time, and whether a clinical pharmacist embedded in the hematology clinic decreased time to LDD shipment.

Conclusion

Integration helped, but LDD network limits remained visible

Embedding a clinical pharmacist in the clinic significantly shortened time from treatment decision to shipment for LDD medications, partially overcoming access barriers.

Even after integration, access was slower for LDDs than for non-LDD medications because LDDs could not be fully incorporated into the clinic specialty pharmacy workflow.
