Appropriate anticoagulation rates were similar for IMiD-treated multiple myeloma patients across pharmacy settings.

Baptist Health Specialty Pharmacy retrospectively evaluated whether SAVED-score-guided prophylaxis differed for multiple myeloma patients receiving immunomodulatory drugs through a health-system specialty pharmacy versus non-HSSP channels.

Methods Flow

The evaluation translated guideline risk scoring into a pharmacy-channel comparison.

Define cohort

Adults with multiple myeloma on an IMiD for at least one month were included.

Exclude barriers

Patients with contraindications to anticoagulant therapy under NCCN guidance were excluded.

Pull data

Case management program and electronic health record reports supplied study variables.

Score risk

SAVED scores determined whether aspirin or stronger anticoagulant prophylaxis was appropriate.

Compare outcomes

Appropriateness, ER events, costs, and pharmacist interventions were compared by dispensing channel.

Decision Rule

SAVED score framed prophylaxis as aspirin versus anticoagulant therapy.

<2 Aspirin expected when risk score is below 2.
≥2 Enoxaparin, rivaroxaban, apixaban, fondaparinux, or warfarin expected when risk score is 2 or higher.

Risk context

Multiple myeloma patients have elevated VTE risk, and IMiD therapy increases the importance of prophylaxis review.

Guideline anchor

The poster cites NCCN incorporation of SAVED score use in 2019 for MM patients receiving IMiDs.

Measured gap

Inappropriate therapy appeared in 18 HSSP patients and 16 non-HSSP patients.

Outcome lens

Bleeding and thrombotic ER visits were also compared, with no statistically significant difference.

Practice signal

The authors recommend SAVED-score calculations during new IMiD clinical review and periodically during therapy.

Limitations

Risk timing and documentation quality could influence classification.

  • Patients were not separated by newly diagnosed versus previously diagnosed multiple myeloma.
  • VTE risk is highest in the first six months after diagnosis, so risk may have varied between groups.
  • SAVED-score variables such as recent surgery and VTE history depended on manual office-note documentation.

Recommendations

Embed SAVED-score review into pharmacist clinical workflows.

The poster recommends calculating SAVED scores during clinical review of new IMiD prescriptions before dispensing and periodically during therapy, with prospective research to assess ER visits related to anticoagulation.

  • Risk scoring should occur before IMiD dispensing.
  • Periodic reassessment is needed as therapy and risk factors change.
  • Prospective outcomes research should focus on anticoagulation-related ER visits.

Conclusion: the study found no statistically significant difference in inappropriate anticoagulation, thrombotic events, or bleeding-related events between health-system specialty pharmacy and non-HSSP patients receiving IMiDs.
