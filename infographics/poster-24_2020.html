NASP 2020 / Poster 24

Persistence on droxidopa remained difficult despite integrated specialty pharmacy monitoring.

Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy evaluated adult patients receiving droxidopa for symptomatic orthostatic hypotension and tracked persistence, adherence, adverse events, falls, and healthcare utilization within an integrated neurology specialty pharmacy model.

Interpretation

Monitoring did not eliminate non-persistence.

The poster defined non-persistence as the first coverage lapse greater than 60 days. At 6 months, 61 patients were persistent; by 12 months the probability of remaining persistent was 0.63, with 23 patients censored.

At the end of the study period, 55 patients, or 61.8%, were persistent. Median follow-up was 10.9 months.

Methods and evidence flow

A single-center retrospective cohort connected dispensing records with patient-reported and EHR outcomes.

Identify

Adults prescribed droxidopa and filled at least 3 times through the center's specialty pharmacy.

Track

Measure time to first non-persistent event, defined as a medication coverage lapse greater than 60 days.

Monitor

Capture adherence by PDC, patient-reported adverse events and falls, ED visits, and hospitalizations.

Interpret

Separate persistence challenges from adherence among patients who continued therapy.

Objective

Evaluate persistence in an integrated specialty pharmacy model.

The poster asked whether adult patients with symptomatic orthostatic hypotension remained persistent on droxidopa while receiving care supported by a specialty pharmacist embedded in the outpatient neurology clinic workflow.

Conclusion

High adherence did not solve the persistence problem.

The authors concluded that many patients were not persistent through 12 months despite close monitoring for adverse events and efficacy. Among patients who maintained therapy, adherence rates were high.

Future analysis was planned to examine reasons for non-persistence and relationships among adverse events, healthcare utilization, and persistence.
