Safety update: Tepotinib in METex14 skipping NSCLC

The VISION Phase II safety analysis followed 255 patients with advanced or metastatic MET exon 14 skipping NSCLC treated with tepotinib, emphasizing treatment-related adverse events, edema timing, and practical dose-modification guidance.

Eligibility

Advanced NSCLC, EGFR/ALK wild-type, MET alteration by liquid and/or tissue biopsy, and first- to third-line therapy.

Safety capture

TEAEs were graded by NCI-CTCAE v4.03 and treatment-related causality was investigator assessed.

Clinical-interest AEs

Edema, gastrointestinal AEs, and creatinine increase were analyzed for onset and resolution patterns.

Management

Edema was commonly handled with dose reduction, treatment interruption, and supportive measures.

Conclusion

Tepotinib was generally well tolerated in this large METex14 skipping NSCLC population.

Edema management recommendations

Monitor early, manage conservatively, and modify dose before severity escalates

Baseline

Measure weight and limbs

Proactive monitoring included weight and peripheral limb measurements to detect increases.

Support

Use conservative measures

Support stockings, limb elevation, increased physical activity, and kinesiotherapy were recommended.

Escalate

Consider lymphedema clinic

Cross-functional management could be considered for edema management.

Modify

Reduce or interrupt early

Dose reduction or temporary treatment interruption was recommended to mitigate severity.

Treatment modifications due to edema

Few edema events led to permanent discontinuation

18.8% Patients with at least one edema event leading to dose reduction.
23.1% Patients with at least one edema event leading to temporary interruption.
4.3% Patients with at least one edema event leading to permanent discontinuation.

General dose guidance: maintain grade 2 if tolerable; withhold and resume reduced dose for intolerable grade 2 or grade 3; permanently discontinue for grade 4.

Conclusion: in VISION, tepotinib was generally well tolerated for METex14 skipping NSCLC; peripheral edema was the key safety signal, usually mild or moderate, and was managed through monitoring, supportive care, dose reduction, or temporary interruption.
