Management of Prescription Cannabidiol Adverse Effects and Drug-Drug Interactions

Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy followed real-world prescription CBD use for one year, tracking drug-drug interactions, adverse drug events, liver function tests, and the pharmacist actions used to keep therapy moving safely.

Objective

Describe first-year CBD safety signals and how specialty pharmacists managed them.

The poster focused on adverse drug events and drug-drug interactions experienced during the first year of prescription CBD use in a real-world neurology clinic setting.

Design Single-center retrospective cohort study.
Inclusion Patients prescribed CBD through the center's neurology clinic from January 2019 through April 2020.
Outcomes Medication use, adverse effects, DDIs, liver function tests, and management actions.

Patient mix

Pediatric patients made up roughly two-thirds of the cohort.

Liver monitoring

One in four monitored patients had an elevated AST.

Drug interaction pattern

Clobazam drove the DDI story.

Among 65 documented DDIs, 89% were pharmacokinetic and 89% involved clobazam, making dose adjustment of the interacting antiepileptic drug a recurring management pathway.

89% Pharmacokinetic interactions; changes in absorption, distribution, metabolism, or elimination.
11% Pharmacodynamic interactions; cumulative adverse-effect risk.
58 Clobazam interactions, equal to 89% of DDIs.
3 Valproic acid interactions; phenobarbital and other drugs were less frequent.

DDI management Clobazam interactions most often led to other-medication dose changes.
ADE management Drowsiness or sedation was commonly addressed through CBD or interacting-drug dose changes.
Continuity goal Monitoring and management were framed around helping patients safely continue therapy.

Need

Post-approval real-world data were needed to describe longitudinal CBD safety management.

Integrated review

Specialty pharmacists evaluated safety and appropriateness before and during treatment.

Interaction signal

Clobazam accounted for 58 of 65 interactions, most classified as pharmacokinetic.

Event signal

Drowsiness or sedation accounted for 44 of 138 reported adverse drug events.

Action

Pharmacists used DDI checks, counseling, chart review, and provider-facing recommendations.

Conclusion

Specialty pharmacists were positioned as safety managers for first-year CBD therapy.

The authors concluded that clobazam was the most common DDI, drowsiness and sedation were common adverse events, and both were frequently managed through CBD or interacting medication dose changes.

The poster frames pharmacist monitoring as a practical safeguard for mitigating ADEs and DDIs so patients can continue prescription cannabidiol therapy when appropriate.
