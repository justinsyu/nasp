Clinical and quality outcomes of a bleeding disorder program in a health system specialty pharmacy

A retrospective review of patients served by an integrated specialty pharmacy and Hemophilia Treatment Center found low bleed-related burden, high emergency factor readiness, and dose dispensing variance far below the common +/- 10% ordering standard.

Evidence flow

How the poster connects pharmacy operations to patient outcomes

Rare, complex therapy

Hemophilia and von Willebrand disease require complex management and high-cost factor products.

Integrated care model

The HSSP works with the HTC, prescribers, patients, clinicians, and billing pathways.

Home factor readiness

Most patients had emergency factor on hand, supporting faster home treatment of bleeds.

Waste control

Mean variance was 2%, well below the poster's cited +/- 10% standard-ordering benchmark.

Clinical signal

Low pain, limited absenteeism, and minimal acute-care use suggest favorable patient burden.

Conclusion

Specialty pharmacy coordination may reduce delays, complications, and waste

The authors conclude that the health system specialty pharmacy plays a central role in maintaining adequate home factor supply. In this cohort, emergency readiness and tight dose variance aligned with low acute-care utilization, low pain scores, and limited absenteeism.

Source references in the poster include Chai-Adisaksopha et al. on hemophilia bleeding outcome definitions and National Hemophilia Foundation MASAC recommendations on home clotting factor concentrate dosing.
