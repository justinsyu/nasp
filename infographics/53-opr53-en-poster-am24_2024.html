Burden of illness in nonrelapsing secondary progressive multiple sclerosis

A matched-cohort analysis of linked US EHR and claims data found that people with nrSPMS had higher comorbidity signals, more physician visits, and substantially higher annual costs than demographically matched MS-free controls.

Why it matters

nrSPMS represents progressive disability without recent relapse, and no DMTs were approved for this population in the US.

Objective

Understand real-world clinical and economic burden among people with nrSPMS in the United States.

Data window

Integrated administrative database with linked EHR and claims records from Jan 1, 2012 to Dec 31, 2021.

Design

Two-year baseline to classify nonrelapsing status, followed by one year of HCRU and cost measurement.

Signal

Clinical burden clustered around fatigue, depression, gait abnormalities, infections, and elevated outpatient pharmacy costs.

Methods

Matched claims-and-EHR study with a two-year lookback

01 Identify SPMS phenotype through EHR records and a validated claims-based nrSPMS algorithm.
02 Require continuous medical and pharmacy enrollment across baseline and follow-up periods.
03 Exclude relapse evidence during the two-year baseline and exclude selected neurologic disorders.
04 Match each nrSPMS patient to an MS-free control by demographics, region, and insurance.

Study constraints

Interpretation guardrails

  • Cross-sectional burden estimates may not capture changing disease management over time.
  • Claims miscoding could affect patient identification and comorbidity reporting.
  • Findings may not generalize to populations outside the covered insurance mix.

Conclusion

nrSPMS carried additional clinical and economic burden in a population without approved therapies

Compared with matched MS-free controls, people with nrSPMS showed higher prevalence of several MS-related comorbidities, greater physician-visit intensity, and markedly higher healthcare costs, led primarily by outpatient pharmacy spending.
