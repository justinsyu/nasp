Assessment of the Implementation of Collaborative Pharmacy Practice Agreements in an Integrated Health System Specialty Pharmacy

Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy evaluated newly implemented collaborative practice agreements across integrated specialty clinics. In the first six months after implementation, pharmacists documented patient encounters, placed medication and lab orders, and maintained at least 95% medication list and allergy review compliance.

Objective

Measure outcomes after CPA implementation

The study assessed whether collaborative practice agreements in an integrated health-system specialty pharmacy enabled pharmacists to contribute measurable clinical activity after implementation.

  1. Single-center retrospective cohort using electronic medical record data.
  2. Included clinics with newly implemented CPAs and six months of post-implementation follow-up.
  3. Measured patient encounters, prescription orders, laboratory orders, and quarterly audit safety concerns.

Quarterly audits

Safety-process adherence stayed high

Post-implementation medication list and allergy review compliance was at or above 95% in every clinic, indicating that the added pharmacist responsibilities did not erode this audited review process.

Interpretation

CPAs shifted work into pharmacist-managed specialty workflows

Across the evaluated clinics, pharmacists contributed documented patient contact and placed both medication and laboratory orders where the agreement scope permitted it.

The highest total ordering activity appeared in multiple sclerosis, cystic fibrosis, movement disorders, and hemostasis clinics.

Conclusion

Expanded responsibility without lowering review compliance

The authors concluded that pharmacists practicing under CPPAs in an IHSSP can increase patient-care capacity, add medication and laboratory order activity, and maintain high medication and allergy list review compliance.
