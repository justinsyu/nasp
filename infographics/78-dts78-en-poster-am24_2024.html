Impact of automated financial assistance enrollment at a large mail-order specialty pharmacy on patient outcomes

A pre/post Optum Specialty Pharmacy analysis tested whether Optum Savings IQ automated matching to copay cards, grants, and financial resources reduced patient out-of-pocket cost without slowing access to specialty therapy.

Core result: OSIQ engagement was associated with lower median copay and shorter PAD hold time, while turnaround time was not statistically different.

Objective

Test whether automated assistance changes cost and access friction

The study compared treatment-naive specialty patients before OSIQ implementation with patients after implementation across oncology, multiple sclerosis, and autoimmune disease states.

  • Primary endpoints were average out-of-pocket copay and turnaround time.
  • The secondary endpoint was patient assistance department hold time.
  • Patients with copay below $5, IVR refill processing, existing treatment, or plan restrictions on copay cards were excluded.

Evidence flow

From prescription processing data to outcome comparison

Define index periods

Compare January 1 to March 31, 2023 before OSIQ with January 1 to March 31, 2024 after implementation.

Select new starts

Include treatment-naive patients identified by a new prescription within the prior 365 days.

Apply exclusions

Remove low-copay, IVR refill, existing, and plan-restricted copay-card cases.

Balance cohorts

Use propensity score matching to create equal control and participant groups.

Compare outcomes

Apply descriptive statistics, Wilcoxon signed-rank, and chi-square testing with alpha set at 0.05.

Interpretation

Automation shifted the financial-assistance bottleneck

  • OSIQ utilization was linked to a statistically significant reduction in copay per fill.
  • Turnaround time was similar despite automated assistance enrollment adding financial matching activity.
  • Shorter PAD hold time suggests more efficient financial assistance processing after implementation.

Study boundaries

What the poster could and could not show

The authors described a single-center, mail-order specialty pharmacy study with a short three-month observation window.

  • Findings may not generalize to other pharmacy settings or broader disease states.
  • Oncology, MS, and autoimmune therapies were the focus.
  • Longer follow-up was proposed to assess adherence, persistence, and total cost of care.

Conclusion: automated OSIQ financial-assistance enrollment substantially reduced patient out-of-pocket cost and PAD hold time in matched specialty pharmacy cohorts, without a statistically significant change in turnaround time.
