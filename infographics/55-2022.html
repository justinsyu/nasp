Objective and Methods

Map early adverse events, treatment modifications, and adherence after starting combination therapy.

The poster reports a single-center retrospective cohort analysis of patients with metastatic or unresectable melanoma initiating encorafenib and binimetinib through an integrated specialty pharmacy program.

Clinical question

How often do early adverse effects translate into dose changes or holds after combination therapy starts?

Cohort lens

Eighteen patients with BRAF-mutated metastatic or unresectable melanoma were followed retrospectively.

Safety signal

The poster counted 126 adverse events, with fatigue and nausea most common.

Management signal

Forty-four percent required at least one hold, and 39% required at least one dose reduction.

Outcome signal

Median PDC stayed high at 89%, increasing to 96% after adjustment.

Conclusion and future research

Early adverse effects were common, yet adherence remained high enough to make pharmacist-led mitigation the next question.

The authors concluded that treatment modifications due to adverse effects are common in the first 90 days after encorafenib and binimetinib initiation, while medication adherence remains high despite adverse events.

  • Early monitoring should expect holds and dose reductions, especially during the first 60 days.
  • Adherence data suggest patients can remain covered even when therapy is interrupted or adjusted.
  • Future work should test how integrated health-system pharmacists can prevent and address AEs while preserving optimal dosing.
