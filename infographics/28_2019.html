NASP 2019 poster 28_2019

A targeted approach to reducing rates of discontinuation and dose reduction in patients receiving sorafenib or regorafenib

Vanderbilt Specialty Pharmacy tested whether a structured pharmacist monitoring program could detect adverse reactions earlier and help patients stay on oral multikinase inhibitor therapy through the first 90 days.

Objective

Could proactive pharmacist touchpoints reduce treatment disruption?

The poster assessed the impact of pharmacist intervention on rates of sorafenib and regorafenib treatment discontinuation and dose reduction due to adverse reactions.

Control group CG n=17

Initial counseling plus refill-oriented phone calls at days 30, 60, and 90.

Intervention group IG n=16

Welcome kit plus additional early clinical calls tailored by therapy.

Rationale

Adverse events with sorafenib and regorafenib commonly lead to discontinuation or dose reduction.

Mechanism

Pharmacists monitored side effects, blood pressure, missed doses, supply, interactions, and care-team communication.

Workload

The intervention group generated 80 calls and 41 documented interventions among 13 patients.

Main action

Adverse-effect mitigation accounted for 26 interventions, or 63% of all pharmacist actions.

Conclusion

Tailored clinical monitoring helped detect and mitigate adverse reactions so patients remained on therapy longer.

Interpretation

A small, targeted monitoring layer produced a large descriptive signal

The poster concluded that tailored clinical monitoring of oral multikinase inhibitors was effective in detecting and mitigating adverse reactions.

The strongest visual signal was the difference in any therapy interruption or dose reduction within 90 days: 65% in the control group versus 13% in the intervention group.
