DAA treatment failure and retreatment after NAT+ HCV solid organ transplantation

A four-center retrospective case series pooled HCV-negative solid organ transplant recipients who received HCV NAT+ organs, failed initial direct-acting antiviral therapy, and then underwent DAA retreatment.

Evidence flow

From NAT+ organ transplant to retreatment strategy

1

HCV-negative recipient

Adult patients received HCV NAT+ solid organs at Vanderbilt, MUSC, Ochsner, or Rochester.

2

Initial DAA course

Initial regimens were SOF/VEL, GLE/PIB, or LDV/SOF, typically for 12 weeks.

3

Failure identified

Failures included positive viral load at end of treatment, on treatment, or within weeks after EOT.

4

Retreatment selected

SOF/VEL/VOX-containing regimens were the dominant retreatment strategy, with RBV or longer duration in selected cases.

5

SVR assessed

The case series measured sustained virologic response after retreatment as the primary outcome.

Objective

Describe HCV NAT+ SOT recipients who failed initial DAA therapy, retreatment strategies, and subsequent SVR rates.

Methods

Adult HCV-negative NAT+ organ recipients with initial DAA failure were reviewed across four tertiary medical centers.

Initial DAA

SOF/VEL x12 represented 46% of initial regimens; GLE/PIB x12 represented 38%; LDV/SOF x12 represented 15%.

Signal

All patients ultimately achieved SVR after one or two DAA retreatment courses.

Conclusion

Retreatment regimens varied, but SOF/VEL/VOX-containing regimens were used most often, commonly for 12 weeks.

Clinical interpretation

What the case series adds

Because initial DAA failure is uncommon in this population, the pooled multisite series gives practical detail on how retreatment was selected and monitored.

Question Poster signal
Who failed? Mostly kidney recipients; ages ranged from 31 to 73 years.
What was used? SOF/VEL/VOX-based retreatment was prescribed most frequently.
What happened? SVR was high after retreatment, and universal after an additional course for one patient.
