A review of biosimilar adoption after originator product removal

Northwestern Medicine Specialty Pharmacy described how clinical specialty pharmacists guided patients, specialists, and insurance-driven biosimilar selection after adalimumab originator removal from the health-system medication list.

Originator removal project

4-month Pharmacists operationalized policy, prescriber outreach, patient notification, and counseling toward insurance-preferred biosimilars.

Objective

Describe initiatives, challenges, and lessons learned

The poster focuses on what happened when a health-system specialty pharmacy removed originator adalimumab: how many patients transitioned, what happened to spend and purchasing, and what operational barriers emerged.

  • Primary objective: count patients transitioned to a biosimilar.
  • Secondary objectives: originator continuation, adalimumab drug spend, and purchase volume.
  • Policy allowed pharmacist-directed substitution when biosimilar approval and state biological product requirements were met.

Methods translated into workflow

Specialty pharmacists turned a formulary decision into a patient-facing transition pathway

1

Remove originator

Adalimumab originator was removed from the health-system specialty pharmacy serviced medication list.

2

Align policy

Pharmacists used pharmacy and therapeutics biosimilar substitution guidance to support product selection.

3

Notify specialists and patients

Prescribing specialists received information and patients were notified of the originator product removal.

4

Counsel to preferred biosimilars

Patients were counseled on recommended changes to insurance-preferred adalimumab biosimilar products.

Conclusion

Adoption depended on trust, policy, and operational readiness

  • Successful adoption was attributed to specialist acceptance, medication-list removal, and established pharmacy-patient relationships.
  • Barriers included insurance formulary preferences, strength and formulation availability, starter kit availability, shortages, and private-label manufacturing.
  • The authors present the project as a framework for pharmacies considering originator product removal as more biosimilars enter the market.
