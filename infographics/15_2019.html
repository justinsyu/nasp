Clinical impact of pharmacist interventions on immune globulin therapy in the home

Nufactor tracked adverse drug reactions reported during or after home immune globulin infusions, then followed whether pharmacist recommendations were accepted and whether the same ADR was reduced or prevented in the next infusion cycle.

Objective

Turn home-reported ADRs into repeatable prevention steps

The poster asked how often IG-associated ADRs occurred, how severe they were, and whether pharmacist recommendations reduced recurrence after the next infusion.

The narrative also reports that 0.35% were categorized as serious during the 2-year period; those serious events were consistent with FDA boxed warnings for IG products and did not lead to IG discontinuation in half of these patients.

Home risk context

Home IG patients may have fewer immediate intervention options when ADRs occur.

Pharmacist touchpoint

Regular patient communication positioned pharmacists to identify and manage ADR patterns.

Practical conclusion

The poster frames proactive pharmacist intervention as a way to reduce recurrence.

Conclusion

Pharmacists can have a significant impact on preventing or reducing ADRs associated with IG therapy, with high prescriber acceptance and a positive effect on preventing recurrence.
