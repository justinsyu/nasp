Costs and lost time for employees whose spouses have dementia and/or Alzheimer's disease

A linked employer-claims analysis connects patients with Alzheimer disease or dementia to employee spouses, showing how direct medical costs and workplace absence move together across the household.

Objective & method

Claims plus absence data frame caregiving as a workplace issue

1

Identify spouse-patient pairs

Patients were found with ICD-10 codes for Alzheimer disease, dementia, or both, then linked to employee spouses.

2

Anchor on the index year

Pairs needed at least one year of continuous data after the patient's initial diagnosis.

3

Measure cost and work loss

Patient and care-partner medical/Rx costs were paired with FML and discretionary incidental time-off days.

Conclusion signal

2+ weeks

FML absences translated into more than two weeks of worker productivity loss in 2021 and 2022, while DITO days showed a less linear upward pressure.

1. Need

Millions of people live with Alzheimer disease or dementia, and unpaid caregiving often reaches working spouses.

2. Linkage

The RRDb links employees to dependents and carries claims, demographics, leave, disability, and workers' compensation fields.

3. Outcomes

Costs were tracked for patient and care partner; absence was tracked through FML claims and DITO days.

4. Finding

Patients accounted for most medical costs, but employee spouses still showed measurable health-cost and productivity impacts.

5. Implication

Employers may need benefit designs that recognize spouse caregiving as both a household and workforce burden.
