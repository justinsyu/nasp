Breaking barriers: patient navigation in specialty pharmacy for chronic hepatitis C treatment access

Cleveland Clinic Specialty Pharmacy describes a quality improvement effort using patient navigation, a collaborative practice agreement, and a Pharmacy HCV Ambulatory Clinic to move viremic patients from positive labs into treatment.

Service model

Daily viral-load surveillance becomes a navigation queue

1

Daily report

Specialty pharmacists receive all new HCV viral loads across the enterprise, regardless of ordering location.

2

Chart review

Manual review identifies treatment eligibility, contact information, and recent health-system engagement.

3

Registry capture

Patients with HCV viremia are added to Compass Rose for reminders, tracking, and follow-up.

4

Care triage

Simplified-treatment eligibility routes patients to primary care, specialty pharmacy under CPA, or a specialist.

5

Treatment access

Patient navigators coordinate referrals, insurance support, assistance programs, reminders, and telemedicine when possible.

Stakeholder network

Navigation expands the referral surface

  • OB/GYN screens in pregnancy and queues post-pregnancy follow-up.
  • MAT providers screen and rescreen high-risk patients.
  • HIV providers refer overlapping-risk HCV-positive patients.
  • Emergency departments screen after overdose admissions and trigger callbacks.
  • ID specialists and primary care help route viremic patients into treatment.

Objectives

What the project set out to change

  • Quantify the health-system specialty pharmacy impact on HCV treatment rates and clinical outcomes.
  • Engage stakeholders across a large enterprise in patient navigation that reduces treatment barriers.
  • Increase provider acceptance of pharmacist recommendations.
  • Build a pharmacy HCV ambulatory clinic and collaborative practice agreement to reduce wait time for treatment.

Barrier removal design

Patient and system barriers were paired with practical navigation actions

Comorbid conditions

Education, counseling, referrals for MOUD or syringe services, co-localized care, and delayed revisit after pregnancy.

Loss to follow-up

Case managers, registry tracking, proactive scheduling, reminders, secure messaging, and lab-result communication.

Adherence and adverse effects

Specialty pharmacist counseling, directly observed therapy when appropriate, incentives, and transportation help when available.

Access and finances

Insurance education, patient assistance and copay program liaison work, telemedicine, and primary-specialist collaboration.

Practitioner expertise

Updated screening awareness, primary-care treatment support, eConsult collaboration, and the OH-HEP education program.

Interpretation

Early implementation caveats explain why navigation capacity matters

  • Go-live delays from staff training and specialized CPA training created a backlog of HCV-positive patients needing chart review and triage.
  • Some providers were reluctant to create referrals to the HCV Ambulatory Clinic, described as a patient-ownership barrier.
  • Specialist referrals from gastroenterology, hepatology, infectious disease, and transplant surgery suggested pharmacist treatment capacity could support both simplified and complex cases.

Conclusion

Navigation converts a positive lab into a managed pathway

The project frames specialty pharmacists as patient navigators who can coordinate barrier removal, triage patients to the right treatment setting, and use registry infrastructure to retain patients in care.

Success is measured as higher treatment volume during July 1, 2024 to June 30, 2025 while maintaining the high cure and completion rates reported from 2023.
