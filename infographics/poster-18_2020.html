Integrated specialty pharmacy access compressed dalfampridine approval time from weeks to 1 day.

Evidence flow

The intervention was an operating-model shift, not a drug change.

Pre-VSP prescription path external fulfillment

  1. Start form and prescription submitted to manufacturer.
  2. Manufacturer hub processed enrollment.
  3. Clinic nurses managed prior authorization as needed.
  4. Insurance approved or denied the prior authorization.
  5. External pharmacy dispensed the drug.

Post-VSP prescription path integrated HSSP

  1. Start form and prescription submitted to manufacturer.
  2. VSP pharmacists proactively managed prior authorization, counseling, and care coordination.
  3. Insurance approved or denied the prior authorization.
  4. VSP dispensed and mailed the drug.

Barrier

Limited distribution networks can exclude health-system specialty pharmacies, separating clinic teams from dispensing work.

Population

Adults with multiple sclerosis starting or restarting dalfampridine from a VUMC provider were included.

Integrated role

After LDN access, VSP pharmacists coordinated authorization, counseling, dispensing, and shipment in one model.

Access signal

The post-VSP cohort had 100% insurance approval, 100% therapy initiation, and a 1-day median approval time.

Implication

Network restrictions may delay or inhibit access when integrated specialty pharmacy teams are kept outside the channel.

Sample profile

The cohort was predominantly ambulatory MS patients.

Conclusion

Once VSP could dispense dalfampridine, all post-period patients initiated therapy.

The poster concluded that VSP admission to the limited distribution network reduced time to therapy access and enabled successful therapy initiation for all post-VSP patients.

It also cautioned that LDNs may delay or inhibit access to specialty medications for patients served by integrated health-system specialty pharmacies.
