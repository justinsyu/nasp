NASP 2023 poster 78 | Inflammatory bowel disease medication access

Impact of Multi-Step Order Transmittal in an Inflammatory Bowel Disease Clinic

Atrium Health Wake Forest Baptist evaluated whether routing targeted IBD specialty prescriptions into an EHR work queue could shift medication access from a reactive handoff to a prospective, pharmacy-led process.

Access speed before and after MSOT

The largest shift was the front-end handoff into prior authorization.

MSOT moved targeted prescriptions into a monitored EHR queue, reducing both PA submission time and PA determination time while preserving the embedded pharmacist and medication access specialist model.

MSOT workflow

One EHR rule created a prospective pharmacy work queue.

Provider sends Rx

GI provider enters a targeted medication prescription in the EHR.

Algorithm routes

Generic name and prescribing department logic sends the Rx to the MSOT queue.

Access team acts

Embedded pharmacist and technician complete benefits investigation and PA work.

Rx released

Pharmacist or MAS releases the prescription to the preferred specialty pharmacy.

Clinic notified

The clinic receives determination details through an EHR note.

Internal specialty pharmacy dispense

Dispense time improved, including in the delayed induction subgroup.

Ustekinumab and risankizumab require IV induction before subcutaneous maintenance therapy. That ordering pattern created a known lag, but dispense time still decreased after MSOT.

Objective, method, conclusion

What the poster tested.

Objective: Evaluate the impact of MSOT implementation on medication access in an IBD clinic.

Methods: IRB-approved retrospective cohort study comparing new PA prescriptions from May 1 to November 1, 2021 with May 1 to November 1, 2022.

  • Included prescriptions for targeted medications requiring a new prior authorization.
  • Excluded uninsured patients and prescriptions requiring renewal prior authorizations.
  • Conclusion: MSOT improved PA turnaround time and supported workflow enhancements that can improve care quality.

Prescription capture

Internal capture moved from 43% to 48%.

The poster notes this increase was not significant, but frames internal capture as relevant to both patient care and specialty pharmacy revenue.

Post-MSOT48%

Operational interpretation

MSOT changed the access posture.

The discussion describes a communication-intensive reactive process becoming a real-time, prospective process without added pharmacy staff. Anecdotally, the workflow also improved pharmacists' ability to make proactive clinical interventions, though the poster calls for more information to quantify that effect.
