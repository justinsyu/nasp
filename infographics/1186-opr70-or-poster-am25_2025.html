Health Literacy Tool For Multiple Sclerosis Patients On Specialty Medications

A UCSF Health specialty pharmacy team tested whether the Brief Health Literacy Screen could help pharmacists identify patients with multiple sclerosis who need tailored medication education.

Objective

Find patients who need education beyond standard counseling

The project aimed to develop a health literacy screening approach focused on patients' understanding of their condition, rather than only comprehension or arithmetic.

  • Low health literacy can limit understanding of MS and specialty medication instructions.
  • Cognitive symptoms in MS can affect memory, attention, processing, visual-spatial function, and verbal fluency.
  • The operational goal was to identify who may benefit from additional tailored medication education.

Screening thresholds

BHLS creates three action bands

3-9
Limited health literacy

Patient requires additional tailored education or low-literacy medication materials.

10-12
Marginal health literacy

Patient may benefit from additional medication education.

13-15
Adequate health literacy

Patient is considered proficient and does not need additional education.

Methods

Three-part workflow: baseline practice, BHLS screening, post-use feedback

1

Pre-screening survey

Asked pharmacists whether they assess health literacy, what screening tools they use, and whether they review the APeX Social Drivers of Health item.

2

Brief Health Literacy Screen

Used three Likert-style questions on medical forms, help reading hospital materials, and difficulty learning from written information.

3

Post-screening survey

Assessed perceived helpfulness, implementation experience, and likelihood of continuing the tool in everyday practice.

Central implementation gap

Helpful is not enough The poster reports that most pharmacists found BHLS helpful, but continuation was limited because the next-step workflow and patient resources were not standardized.

Background

Health literacy is a social determinant of health, and MS-related cognitive decline can make disease and medication education harder to absorb.

Need

A standardized way to identify patients who need additional education was lacking in the specialty pharmacy workflow.

Tool

BHLS offered a fast three-question screen that mapped patients into limited, marginal, or adequate literacy bands.

Barrier

Pharmacists wanted clearer guidance on what to do after a patient screened low.

Conclusion

Future work should pair screening with standardized handouts, medication education materials, and follow-up procedures.

Qualitative themes

Post-screen feedback centered on the missing support layer

Lack of resources

Respondents cited the need for multilingual handouts, pictorial instructions, and a standardized follow-up plan.

Incomplete workflow

Leaving intervention steps to individual judgment made the project harder to scale across providers.

Standardized follow-up

A basic framework or toolkit was recommended for patients identified with limited health literacy.
