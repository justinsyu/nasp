Compassionate care in a specialty pharmacy

Yale New Haven Health Outpatient Pharmacy Services surveyed specialty pharmacy staff to compare compassionate-care knowledge, confidence, grief exposure, and perceived training gaps for conversations with patients who have poor prognoses.

Objective

Measure readiness for emotionally difficult specialty pharmacy conversations.

OPS provides disease management for more than 40 disease states. Staff often support patients with chronic progressive diseases, poor prognoses, or end-of-life needs, but the poster notes variation in counseling expertise.

  • Question: What do staff know about compassion, empathy, and active listening?
  • Perception lens: How confident and prepared do staff feel?
  • Use case: Guide an education program for all staff.

Methods

A 14-item anonymous electronic survey separated knowledge from perception.

3 Knowledge items on empathy, compassion, and active listening.
8 Perception items about confidence, grief, education needs, barriers, and skills.
3 Demographic items; survey links and QR codes were distributed by email and in person.
Core knowledge gap 10%

Only one in ten respondents correctly identified five elements of active listening, while compassion definition accuracy was even lower.

Conclusion

Formal training was positioned as the bridge between compassionate intent and consistent practice.

The survey found knowledge gaps in compassion, empathy, and active listening, plus role-specific education gaps in therapeutic communication and personal coping. The poster concludes these results should guide an education program offered to all staff.

  • Define compassion and empathy using shared language.
  • Practice active listening and poor-prognosis communication.
  • Include coping strategies for grief after patient death.
