Clinical and Economic Burden in People with Relapsing-Remitting Multiple Sclerosis in the United States

A matched-cohort claims and electronic health record study compared adults with RRMS against MS-free controls to quantify clinical events, healthcare resource use, and 2021-adjusted healthcare costs during one year of follow-up.

Evidence flow

How the poster moved from database records to burden estimates

Identify RRMS

People were identified through EHR phenotype keywords or a validated claims-based RRMS algorithm during 2012-2020.

Require coverage

Continuous medical and pharmacy enrollment for one year after index was required unless the patient died within the year.

Apply exclusions

Claims rules excluded evidence suggesting progressive disease, worsening EDSS, or exacerbations in the post-index period.

Match controls

Each RRMS patient was paired with an MS-free control with matching demographic and insurance characteristics.

Compare outcomes

Comorbidities, HCRU, and healthcare costs were compared descriptively with two-sided tests and P<0.05 significance.

Matched demographics were identical by design: mean age 52.9 years, 79.1% female, and 84.0% Caucasian in each cohort.

Objective

Real-world burden in US RRMS

The poster asked how much clinical and economic burden remains among people with relapsing-remitting MS in the United States despite available disease-modifying therapies.

  • HCRU included inpatient admissions, emergency department visits, outpatient services, pharmacy use, and infection-related costs.
  • All costs were reported in US dollars and adjusted to year 2021.

Limitations

Claims-based interpretation caveats

The authors noted possible miscoding in claims data, which could affect patient identification and reported comorbidities.

  • Results may not generalize to populations outside the covered commercial-insurance database.
  • EHR-based identification was used when patients were identified by both EHR and claims sources.

Conclusion: compared with matched MS-free controls, people with RRMS had more infections and comorbidities, higher healthcare resource utilization, and substantially higher healthcare costs, indicating persistent clinical and economic burden.
