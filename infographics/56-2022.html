NASP 2022 / Poster 56-2022 / Specialty pharmacy interventions

Cost impact analysis of clinical interventions collected via specialty pharmacy reporting tool

Optum Specialty Pharmacy quantified cost savings and cost avoidance from clinician-documented interventions, translating a large reporting-system sample into a reviewed set of 2,868 interventions with an estimated combined financial impact of $9.66 million.

Objective and methods

Convert intervention documentation into an estimated financial impact model.

The poster assessed average and total estimated cost savings or avoidance across clinical intervention categories captured in Optum Specialty Pharmacy's internal reporting system.

Eligible records Completed clinical pharmacy interventions from Aug. 1, 2020 through Jul. 31, 2021 in categories identified as potentially cost saving or cost avoiding.
Cost savings calculations Based on clinician-reported changes, prescription data, and drug average wholesale pricing.
Cost avoidance calculations Based on drug AWP where applicable, management program data, and primary literature.
Analysis Descriptive statistics only, with categories reviewed for trends and applicability before further analysis.

Results pattern

The financial estimate split 58% savings and 42% avoidance.

Review of 2,868 interventions produced $5,611,370 in estimated cost savings and $4,050,242 in estimated cost avoidance. The poster positions the internal reporting system as a way to surface intervention categories that can be optimized for future tracking and research.

Conclusion

Documented clinical interventions can be converted into a focused value story, but cleaner capture is the next frontier.

The authors concluded that the project quantified cost savings or avoidance for 2,868 clinical interventions and identified trends for future system optimization and research. They also noted limitations from manual documentation, possible duplication or omission, and cost-avoidance estimates based on literature rather than actual medical claims.

  • The largest dollar driver was quantity decrease, despite ADE management being the highest-volume category.
  • Cost avoidance depended heavily on published cost assumptions for ADEs, hospitalization, and interaction outcomes.
  • Future work should improve tracking fidelity and link interventions to true frequency and financial outcomes.
