Real-world migraine treatment moved care away from acute settings and toward specialty providers.

PurpleLab and Parexel analyzed US representative claims from 2018 to 2024 to understand how migraine treatment patterns varied across diagnosis, drug therapy, acupuncture, and other procedure cohorts, with attention to provider specialty and reported race.

Objective and methods

Four migraine cohorts made treatment variation visible across the care pathway.

Define migraine

ICD-9-CM, ICD-10-CM, and MS-DRG codes identified migraine and migraine subtypes.

Add treatment lenses

Cohorts included diagnosis only, diagnosis plus drug treatment, acupuncture, and other procedures.

Map providers

HCP specialties were assigned using NPI taxonomy and patient volume in each cohort.

Compare shifts

Specialty distributions and race patterns were compared as treatment intensity changed.

Main result

Treatment cohorts shifted toward neurology, headache, pain, and complementary care.

Care moved away from emergency medicine as treatment became more specific.

Emergency medicine represented 11.4% of the diagnosis cohort but only 1.8% in the drug treatment cohort and 0.64% in the acupuncture cohort.

The authors interpret this as a movement from acute and general care toward migraine- specific specialty management.

White patients represented 85% to 90% of cohorts, Black/African American patients 7% to 12%, and Asian patients 2% to 3%. The authors note that Asian patients had a higher relative percentage seeking CAM including acupuncture.

Interpretation

The poster frames specialization as a potential marker of preference-aligned care.

As patients sought migraine-specific drugs, procedures, acupuncture, or combinations of therapy, care concentrated in provider specialties expected to manage migraine more directly: neurology, headache disorders, pain medicine, and CAM.

The race analyses were descriptive, but the poster highlights possible relationships between racially diverse patient groups, provider specialty, and alternative medicine use that warrant further study.

Conclusion: more specific migraine treatment was associated with lower acute-care and ED provider involvement.

The authors concluded that treatment variation and HCP specialty shifting may align with patient preference for certain migraine treatments, with care moving from acute settings toward specialty providers as management becomes more migraine-specific.
