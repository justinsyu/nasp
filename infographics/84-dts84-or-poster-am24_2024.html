Optimizing Use of Electronic Patient-Reported Outcomes (ePROs) to Assess and Improve Disease Management

A targeted literature review and multidisciplinary focus group used rheumatoid arthritis treat-to-target care as a working model for making ePROs more usable, actionable, and sustainable in specialty practice.

Objective

Characterize barriers and practical solutions for ePRO implementation

  • Use rheumatoid arthritis as an example of treat-to-target care that depends on shared decision-making.
  • Identify why PRO/ePRO collection is difficult to sustain across real outpatient workflows.
  • Translate literature themes and focus-group discussion into implementable next steps for clinicians, patients, and specialty pharmacies.

Evidence flow

Two-part qualitative approach converted published evidence into workflow recommendations

1

Search literature

PubMed and EBSCO/CINAHL were searched in November and December 2022 for U.S. outpatient RA T2T articles.

2

Extract themes

Twenty relevant articles were reviewed for concepts around PRO/ePRO collection, shared decisions, and sustainability.

3

Convene panel

A virtual March 2023 focus group brought together rheumatology, informatics, infusion, population health, and clinic operations perspectives.

4

Map solutions

Participants expanded the barriers list and proposed EHR, education, workflow, and patient-engagement solutions.

Decision support concept

Pair RAPID3 with CDAI to separate concordance from discordance

RAPID3: patient-reported lens

Quick point-of-care measure covering physical ability, pain, and overall health that can be completed in office or before a visit.

CDAI: clinical disease activity lens

Clinician-facing measure incorporating tender and swollen joint counts plus patient and clinician global assessments.

Incentive gap

Challenge: Clinicians may not see how PRO data changes decisions.

Solution: Embed PROs in decision trees, protocols, training, and value communication.

Measure uncertainty

Challenge: Teams need comprehensive assessment without extra workload.

Solution: Build consensus around disease-impact PROs such as RAPID3 and patient-priority outcomes.

Staff bandwidth

Challenge: More patient information can exceed clinic capacity.

Solution: Use portals, check-in touchscreens, and remote monitoring between visits.

IT integration

Challenge: Manual collection and documentation make ePRO use inconsistent.

Solution: Integrate ePROs directly into the EHR and reduce documentation burden.

Patient understanding

Challenge: Patients may not know why questionnaires matter.

Solution: Explain how the data will be used and have office staff champion completion.

Conclusion

ePROs are most useful when they are integrated into decisions, not simply collected

  • The main barriers were clinician time restrictions, IT integration issues, and lack of patient understanding.
  • Recommended next steps include clinician and patient education, professional-organization partnerships, and easier digital capture tools.
  • The framework extends beyond rheumatoid arthritis and creates a practical role for specialty pharmacies in patient-centric workflows.
