Specialty pharmacist integration shortened access time for pimavanserin

Vanderbilt University Medical Center evaluated whether embedding specialty pharmacy work into the neurology clinic changed the path from treatment decision to insurance approval for Parkinson's disease-related psychosis therapy.

  • Single-center retrospective cohort
  • Pimavanserin
  • Parkinson's disease psychosis
  • May 2016-Jul 2018

Access pattern

Monthly access times compressed after the pharmacist became the access hub

The poster reported a 21-day average decrease and a median shift from 24.5 to 3 days from treatment decision to access.

Evidence flow

Integration reduced handoffs and moved authorization work to a specialty pharmacist

Before integration 9-step path

1Prescriber decides to treat
2Nurse completes and faxes treatment form
3Medication hub returns benefits investigation
4Clinic office completes prior authorization and appeal work
5Hub routes prescription to dispensing pharmacy for shipment

After integration 5-step path

1Prescriber decides to treat
2Pharmacist receives message
3Pharmacist completes benefits investigation, prior authorization, and appeal work
4Prescriber sends medication order
5Medication ships to the patient

Objective

Determine the impact of specialty pharmacist integration on time to access for pimavanserin.

Design

Single-center retrospective cohort of neurology clinic patients prescribed pimavanserin from May 2016 through July 2018.

Primary endpoint

Medication access time, defined as days between the treatment decision and insurance approval.

Access signal

Before integration, patients had a 23-fold increase in odds of longer time to access compared with the integrated model.

Conclusion

Integrated clinical pharmacy can expedite access and initiation while preserving monitoring for safety and efficacy.

Interpretation

The model turned specialty pharmacy from a downstream dispenser into an access-and-monitoring partner

The access gains aligned with the workflow change: benefits investigation, authorization, appeal support, financial assistance, counseling, care coordination, and monitoring were concentrated with the specialty pharmacist.

Further research was recommended to determine whether faster access translated into improved clinical outcomes.
