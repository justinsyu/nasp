Care delivery shift

IV center to clinic injection Embedded specialty pharmacy turns a capacity problem into a protocolized specialty-care pathway.

Objective

Reduce burden while preserving access, safety, and reimbursement discipline

The project aimed to improve patient access, experience, and clinical outcomes by creating a clinic-administered subcutaneous alternative to traditional IV infusion for eligible patients with multiple sclerosis.

Traditional IV pathway Infusion center chair time, longer nursing coverage, travel burden, facility charges, extended monitoring.
>
Clinic SC pathway 10-minute injection, shorter observation, paired provider visit, pharmacy-led access and documentation.

Methods

Implementation built as a seven-part operating model

1

Define vision

Identify demand for a convenient, capacity-sparing administration option.

2

Build protocol

Specify screening, counseling, injection, observation, and emergency response.

3

Align team

Coordinate neurology, nurses, pharmacists, technicians, access, and operations.

4

Test feasibility

Assess space, staffing, storage, scheduling, and clinic-day design.

5

Map workflow

Create SOPs, order sets, documentation templates, and staff drills.

6

Navigate access

Verify benefits, prior authorizations, billing route, and supply logistics.

7

Use partners

Leverage clinical education, reimbursement resources, and patient materials.

Conclusion

Pharmacist integration was the operating engine

  • A multidisciplinary model was necessary to launch a new clinic process safely and consistently.
  • Clinic-embedded specialty pharmacists led workflow design, coordination, access planning, and implementation.
  • Ocrelizumab/hyaluronidase SC injection emerged as a valued option with high patient satisfaction and preference.
