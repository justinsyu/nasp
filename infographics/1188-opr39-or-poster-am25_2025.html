NASP 2025 | OPR39-OR | Poster 1188

Real-world insights on biosimilar conversion following adalimumab originator formulary exclusion

A single-center retrospective cohort at Optum Specialty Pharmacy tracked treatment-experienced patients affected by adalimumab originator product formulary exclusion across April 2024 to February 2025.

Objective

Measure conversion after removing originator adalimumab from formulary access.

The primary endpoint evaluated the rate of biosimilar conversion associated with formulary exclusion of the adalimumab originator product.

The secondary endpoint stratified non-biosimilar conversions by diagnosis and drug class to identify actionable trends.

Methods lens

Patients were followed for up to 9 months after their first therapy conversion.

Patients previously receiving originator adalimumab were included if active insurance was maintained throughout follow-up. Dispensing records from a national specialty pharmacy prescription database supplied the analysis data.

Result signal

Formulary exclusion produced high biosimilar conversion with low observed reversion.

In the primary conversion stage, 85% of patients converted from originator adalimumab to a biosimilar, while 9% moved to a non-biosimilar alternative and 6% did not switch.

Across later conversion stages, biosimilars continued to account for the large majority of observed therapy pathways: 86% in secondary conversion and 94% in tertiary conversion.

1. Formulary trigger

Originator adalimumab was excluded from formulary access for affected patients.

2. Eligible cohort

Patients were required to maintain active insurance through the follow-up period.

3. Dispense evidence

Prescription database records identified conversion status from April 2024 to February 2025.

4. Conversion tracking

Patients were categorized as biosimilar, originator, non-biosimilar alternative, or no switch.

5. Policy implication

Results support formulary strategies aimed at increasing biosimilar uptake and reducing specialty drug costs.

Conclusion Formulary management can drive broad adalimumab biosimilar uptake while preserving a pathway for clinically appropriate alternatives.

The poster authors conclude that the findings support continued use of formulary management strategies to optimize biosimilar adoption and inform broader managed care and policy decisions.

Limitations

Results came from a single large national specialty pharmacy, relied on dispensing records, and did not assess disease activity, adverse events, or longer-term discontinuation.

Future consideration

Further research is planned to assess longer-term clinical outcomes and sustained cost savings.
