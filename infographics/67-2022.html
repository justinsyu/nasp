Insurance approval delay for biologic dose escalation was linked to worse IBD inflammatory activity

In a single-center retrospective cohort of adults with inflammatory bowel disease, longer time to insurance approval after a provider's dose-escalation decision was associated with reduced likelihood of C-reactive protein improvement.

Approval pathway

The friction point was not only whether PA was needed, but how long approval took.

Among patients requiring prior authorization, most were ultimately approved, often after appeals. The clinical signal emerged when the authorization clock stretched beyond the 14-day delay threshold.

Biomarker result

Follow-up CRP rose with longer approval time.

The regression figure described higher follow-up CRP as time from treatment decision to insurance approval increased, while the delayed subgroup had higher follow-up CRP than patients without delay.

Clinical interpretation

CRP moved with the insurance clock; HBI did not.

Regression signal p=.019

Longer time to insurance approval significantly decreased the likelihood of CRP improvement.

  • Objective: Evaluate the impact of time to dose-escalation insurance approval on disease activity in IBD.
  • Result: Patients delayed more than 14 days had higher follow-up CRP than patients without delay.
  • Counterpoint: Time to approval did not have a significant impact on HBI outcomes.

Dose escalation patterns

Two regimens accounted for most escalations.

Ustekinumab every 6 weeks and adalimumab weekly were the most common alternate dosing regimens in the cohort.

Clinical need

Patients with IBD may need alternate biologic dosing when standard dosing is insufficient.

Access barrier

Non-FDA-approved dose escalation often triggers complex prior authorization and appeals.

Operational measure

The team measured days from provider decision to insurance approval.

Clinical measure

Follow-up CRP was captured at least 45 days after the escalation decision.

Practice implication

An integrated specialty pharmacy team may reduce delay in PA and appeal completion.

Conclusion

Delayed approval translated into worse inflammatory biomarker outcomes.

The poster concludes that longer time to secure insurance approval for dose-escalated biologic therapy was associated with worse CRP outcomes, highlighting how complex authorization processes can affect clinical outcomes and why integrated specialty pharmacy support may be beneficial.
