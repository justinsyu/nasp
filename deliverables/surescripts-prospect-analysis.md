# Surescripts: NASP Outreach Prospect Analysis

Prepared for internal use by an HEOR consulting firm screening NASP (National Association of Specialty Pharmacy) outreach targets. The customer of record for any proposed work is Surescripts; the audience it would need to convince is the specialty-pharmacy community (health-system specialty pharmacies, independent specialty pharmacies, EHR and pharmacy-technology partners) that attends and presents at NASP.

## 1. NASP presence

Surescripts has a confirmed, multi-channel relationship with NASP, though it is not a corporate member.

- **Exhibitor (2022): confirmed.** Surescripts appears on the NASP exhibitors and sponsors list as a 2022 exhibitor ([NASP exhibitors/sponsors page](https://naspnet.org/annual-meeting/exhibitors-sponsors)). The page also lists sponsorship tiers (Diamond, Ruby, Sapphire, Topaz, Onyx), but the sponsor names render as table placeholders rather than text, so whether Surescripts held a paid sponsorship tier could not be confirmed from this source [unverified: Surescripts sponsorship tier].
- **Volunteer (2025): confirmed.** The NASP 2025 annual meeting volunteers page lists Cecelia Byers, PharmD, with the title "Specialty Pharmacy Clinical Product Manager | Surescripts" ([NASP volunteers page](https://naspnet.org/annual-meeting-expo-volunteers)). This indicates an individual staff member is engaged with NASP committee or meeting activity, and that Surescripts maintains a dedicated specialty-pharmacy product role.
- **Corporate member: not present.** Surescripts does not appear anywhere on the NASP corporate member directory ([NASP corporate member directory](https://naspnet.org/about/corporate-member-directory)), which lists members across Palladium, Platinum, Gold, Silver, Bronze, Nickel, and Copper tiers. Surescripts is absent from all tiers.
- **Presenter: not confirmed.** No evidence was found that Surescripts has presented a session or authored a poster at NASP (see Section 2).

Net: the two premised claims are verified. Surescripts is on the 2022 exhibitor list and a Surescripts employee is on the 2025 volunteer list. Surescripts is not a NASP corporate member.

## 2. Poster and published-evidence footprint

**At NASP, Surescripts appears only as cited infrastructure, never as a poster author or sponsor.** A keyword search of the firm's local archive of NASP posters from 2019 to 2025 (489 markdown files) returned exactly two matches, both attributing Surescripts as a data source or software vendor inside another organization's work, not as an author:

- `2123-OPR12-EN-Poster-AM25_2025.md` (2025): a retrospective cohort study by another organization that states "Fill data obtained from HSSP software and Surescripts (Non-HSSP patients)." Surescripts is the source of fill data for non-health-system-specialty-pharmacy patients.
- `79_Creating-a-Digital-Refill-Program-Using-Readily-Available-Software_2023.md` (2023): a workflow poster that uses "the Patient Communications Solution by SureScripts in EnterpriseRx." Surescripts is the named software tool, not the author.

This is the profile the firm is screening for: an organization that appears as plumbing inside the evidence base (a data source and a tool) but has produced no outcomes evidence of its own at NASP. The local archive is a strong proxy for NASP poster authorship, so the conclusion is that Surescripts' NASP poster-authorship footprint is effectively zero.

**Beyond NASP, the published-evidence picture splits into two distinct categories:**

1. **Self-published marketing and operational metrics.** Surescripts publishes an annual report (formerly the "National Progress Report," now the "Annual Impact Report") with network-volume statistics: 27.2 billion transactions exchanged in 2024, 2.6 billion e-prescriptions, touchless prior authorization approvals averaging 34 seconds, and 889,907 prescribers using Real-Time Prescription Benefit (a 16.1% year-over-year increase), with an average reported saving of $82 per prescription when a lower-cost alternative was found ([Surescripts 2024 Annual Impact Report summary](https://surescripts.com/insights/surescripts-2024-annual-impact-report); [Surescripts press release, 2024 report](https://surescripts.com/press-releases/surescripts-2024-annual-impact-report-reveals-double-digit-increase-health-intelligence-sharing-measurably-improving-care-quality-efficiency-access-and-affordability)). These are descriptive operational counts and modeled savings, not controlled outcomes studies.

2. **Peer-reviewed outcomes evidence about Surescripts tools, authored by others.** The most rigorous outcomes study identified about a Surescripts product, a Real-Time Prescription Benefit (RTPB) analysis, was authored by researchers at UPMC, the Evernorth Research Institute, and the University of Pittsburgh, and published in the Journal of Managed Care and Specialty Pharmacy. According to PubMed, this retrospective matched intervention-control study of 1,302 matched pairs of commercial members new to diabetes therapy found proportion of days covered of 71.4% for RTPB members versus 68.7% for controls, generic dispensing rates of 60.1% versus 44.9%, and a plan cost per member per month that was 32.3% higher in the non-RTPB group (a difference of $81.69) (PMID 40884225; [DOI](https://doi.org/10.18553/jmcp.2025.31.9.862)). Surescripts is the vendor of the studied tool but is not listed among the authors or author affiliations. This is the central observation for research fit: when methodologically rigorous, peer-reviewed evidence about Surescripts tools exists, it is being generated by health systems and PBMs, not by Surescripts.

Characterization: **whitespace.** No outcomes-evidence authorship at NASP; infrastructure-only citations in the archive; self-published metrics that are operational rather than inferential; and the one strong peer-reviewed study credited to external authors.

## 3. Business model and data assets

Surescripts operates one of the largest health-information networks in the United States, positioned as a neutral intermediary connecting prescribers, pharmacies, EHR vendors, pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs), health plans, and life-sciences companies ([Surescripts network page](https://surescripts.com/why-surescripts/network); [Surescripts homepage](https://surescripts.com/)). The company was formed by the 2008 merger of RxHub (linking major PBMs to EHRs) and Surescripts (linking EHRs to chain and independent pharmacies), historically holding very high shares of the e-prescription routing and eligibility markets ([Waiting Room: Surescripts Explained](https://waitingroom.substack.com/p/surescripts-explained)).

**Customers and counterparties:** EHR vendors, health systems, retail and specialty pharmacies, PBMs, health plans, life-sciences companies, long-term and post-acute care organizations, and patient-access vendors ([Surescripts network page](https://surescripts.com/why-surescripts/network)). These are network participants who integrate Surescripts transactions into their workflows rather than end consumers.

**Data generated (scale and type):** 27.2 billion network transactions in 2024 spanning e-prescriptions, prescription-benefit optimization, prior authorization, medication history, and clinical messaging; 2.6 billion e-prescriptions; near-universal electronic prescribing for controlled substances (84.4% of e-prescribers, 98.3% of pharmacies enabled) ([Surescripts 2024 Annual Impact Report summary](https://surescripts.com/insights/surescripts-2024-annual-impact-report)). Core data assets are: e-prescription routing records, real-time prescription benefit and price-transparency queries, electronic prior authorization transactions (with reported average touchless approval of 34 seconds), and medication-history records.

**Specialty-pharmacy-specific assets:** Surescripts operates a Specialty Medications Gateway and Specialty Patient Enrollment service that automate gathering of clinical information (height, weight, conditions, labs, medications, allergies) from prescriber EHRs for specialty pharmacies under the Carequality framework. As of 2022, more than 250,000 providers supplied patient information through the Specialty Medications Gateway, and Gateway transaction volume in the first five months of 2022 was 421% higher than the same period in 2021 ([Surescripts press release on specialty services](https://surescripts.com/press-releases/surescripts-further-streamlines-specialty-prescribing-process-new-services-specialty-pharmacies); [Surescripts: Serving Specialty Pharmacies](https://surescripts.com/who-we-serve/specialty-pharmacies)). This is the data slice most relevant to the NASP audience: enrollment turnaround and clinical-data completeness for specialty referrals.

## 4. Research fit: can their data produce evidence that helps them?

**Technically, yes; the relevant question is venue and framing, not feasibility.** Surescripts' transaction-level network data could support outcomes studies on the metrics that matter to specialty pharmacy: prior-authorization turnaround time, time-to-therapy (referral to first fill), prescription abandonment at first fill, real-time-benefit impact on out-of-pocket cost and adherence, and clinical-data completeness in specialty enrollment. The diabetes RTPB study demonstrates that the underlying transaction data can sustain a matched, multivariable, peer-reviewed design (PMID 40884225; [DOI](https://doi.org/10.18553/jmcp.2025.31.9.862)). Surescripts also markets a First-Fill Abandonment solution, indicating it already structures abandonment data ([Surescripts First-Fill Abandonment press release](https://surescripts.com/press-releases/new-surescripts-first-fill-abandonment-solution-empowers-providers-insights-help-improve-patient-care-increase-medication-adherence-and-advance-value-based-care-performance)).

**The gap is the distinction between marketing metrics and rigorous outcomes studies.** Surescripts' self-published outputs are descriptive operational counts and modeled per-prescription savings. They are useful for sales but are not designed for inferential claims (no comparison group, no covariate adjustment, no peer review). The rigorous evidence that does exist about Surescripts tools is authored by external parties. A consulting engagement that converts Surescripts' operational data into methodologically defensible, conference-grade studies (controlled or matched designs, adjusted estimates, transparent limitations) would fill a genuine gap between what Surescripts publishes and what an outcomes-literate audience finds persuasive.

**Honest venue caveat.** Surescripts is a network vendor, not a specialty pharmacy. NASP's poster sessions are dominated by health-system and specialty-pharmacy operational and clinical studies, and Surescripts' general-network metrics (total e-prescription volume, all-cause RTPB savings) are not a natural fit for that audience. The credible angle is narrow: the specialty slice (Specialty Medications Gateway and Specialty Patient Enrollment), where Surescripts data speaks directly to problems NASP attendees own, namely enrollment turnaround, time-to-therapy, and clinical-data completeness for specialty referrals. Framed this way, NASP is a defensible venue; framed as a general-network value story, it is not. A secondary venue (AMCP, where the JMCP RTPB study was published) may be a better fit for benefit and adherence work, and that should be acknowledged in any pitch.

## 5. Verdict and recommended outreach

**Verdict: moderate.**

Rationale: Surescripts is a clean example of the screening target the firm wants. It is embedded in the NASP evidence base as infrastructure (a 2022 exhibitor, a 2025 volunteer, a cited data source and named tool in posters) with effectively zero outcomes-evidence authorship of its own. It owns large-scale, study-grade transaction data and a specialty-specific product line, and there is a demonstrated gap between its marketing-grade self-published metrics and the peer-reviewed evidence that others produce about its tools. The verdict is moderate rather than strong because Surescripts is a network vendor rather than a specialty pharmacy, so only the specialty slice of its data is a natural fit for the NASP audience, and a portion of its strongest evidence opportunities point toward managed-care venues rather than NASP.

Outreach angle: Position the firm as the partner that turns Surescripts' specialty-network transaction data into peer-reviewed, conference-grade outcomes evidence aimed specifically at the specialty-pharmacy decision-makers Surescripts sells to. Lead with the observation that the most rigorous published evidence about a Surescripts tool was authored by a health system and a PBM, not by Surescripts, and that owning that evidence (with NASP and AMCP placements) strengthens the commercial story with an outcomes-literate buyer. Anchor on the Specialty Medications Gateway and Specialty Patient Enrollment data, which map directly to NASP attendees' priorities.

Candidate study ideas:

1. **Time-to-therapy for specialty referrals via Specialty Medications Gateway.** Matched comparison of referral-to-first-fill interval for specialty enrollments completed through the Gateway versus manual (phone/fax) clinical-data gathering. Audience: health-system and independent specialty pharmacies evaluating enrollment-automation tools.
2. **Clinical-data completeness and rework in specialty enrollment.** Quantify the proportion of specialty enrollments arriving with complete clinical data (labs, weight, diagnosis) via the Gateway versus standard intake, and associated rework or delay. Audience: specialty-pharmacy operations and intake leaders.
3. **Electronic prior-authorization turnaround for specialty medications.** Adjusted analysis of touchless versus manual prior-authorization turnaround for specialty drug classes, with downstream time-to-therapy. Audience: specialty pharmacies, prescriber practices, and PBM partners.
4. **Real-time-benefit impact on specialty prescription abandonment and adherence.** Matched intervention-control study of first-fill abandonment and proportion of days covered for specialty initiations where the prescriber used Real-Time Prescription Benefit, modeled on the published diabetes RTPB design. Audience: payers and specialty pharmacies (strongest fit for AMCP, with a specialty-focused cut suitable for NASP).
