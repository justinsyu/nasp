# HSSP Outcomes Research: Cross-Organization Opportunity Synthesis

Prepared for internal use by an HEOR consulting firm serving health-system specialty pharmacy (HSSP) organizations. This synthesis sits above four per-organization opportunity briefs (Shields Health Solutions, CPS Solutions, Clearway Health, VytlOne/Maxor) and consolidates the patterns that recur across all four prospects. Customer of record for every proposal is the **health system**, not payers, PBMs, plan sponsors, or pharmaceutical manufacturers. The objective is to identify where the firm can enhance the methodological quality of each organization's outcomes research and make that research more relevant to the health-system decision-makers who are these organizations' actual customers.

## Scope and method

Each prospect's public evidence base was read at the asset level: the distilled prior analysis was used as a map and then verified against the raw corpus (PDFs, posters, white papers, outcome-study pages, case studies, survey pages, and platform pages). Every opportunity in the four briefs is anchored to a specifically named asset or a documented gap. The four briefs are the primary deliverables; this document reports the cross-cutting findings and a recommended sequence.

A single observation organizes everything below: the same seven gaps appear in all four portfolios. The organizations differ in evidence maturity and in their one distinctive asset, but the underlying methodological and relevance deficits are shared. That shared structure is the central commercial finding, because it means the firm can build one set of reusable methods and protocols and deploy them across the entire prospect set rather than custom-building for each.

## The seven recurring gaps (the repeatable opportunity map)

The table maps each recurring gap to the organizations where it was observed, the standard methodological upgrade, and the health-system decision the upgrade serves. "All four" indicates the gap was observed in every prospect's corpus.

## What the firm uniquely brings

The four prospects are already content-rich. Two of them (Shields, CPS) publish more HSSP outcomes content than almost any organization in the field. The firm should therefore not compete on volume. The recurring gaps point to five capabilities that are absent across all four portfolios and that are difficult for these organizations to staff internally:

1. **Causal inference on observational data.** Matching, difference-in-differences, and interrupted time series are absent from every portfolio, yet they are the designs health-system finance and quality committees expect for capital and accreditation decisions. Every prospect has the data; none applies these methods.

2. **Counterfactual-anchored economics.** Three of the four headline economic claims rest on either an internal unit-cost algorithm, a borrowed literature unit cost, or a blended multi-year figure. A net-impact model with an itemized cost stack and probabilistic sensitivity analysis is a recognized standard none has adopted, and it is the difference between a number that survives CFO reconciliation and one that does not.

3. **Re-pointing the cost object and audience to the health system.** The strongest economic assets in the set are computed from a payer or plan-spend perspective. Re-specifying outcomes to the system's own cost object covers several measures: avoidable utilization at its facilities, prescription capture and leakage, clinician capacity, and equity in its population. This reframing is one none of the payer-oriented analyses provides, and it is what makes the work relevant to the customer.

4. **Standardization and benchmark governance.** Each organization has inconsistent or ungoverned measures. A measure dictionary and case-mix risk adjustment make cross-site comparison defensible. For the organizations with cross-site data (CPS Therigy; the VytlOne/Maxor survey; the Shields network), this converts a scale claim into a usable benchmark.

5. **Independence.** A third party validating a claim carries credibility the vendor cannot self-assert. This matters most for the cross-site benchmarks and for the VytlOne/Maxor VytlAIQ platform, which is marketed on outcomes the vendor has not published and which a CIO and compliance officer will not accept from the vendor alone.

## Two priority cross-organization studies and the reusable assets behind them

Because gaps 2 and 3 appear in all four portfolios and map to the two decisions health-system decision-makers weigh most heavily (the build, expand, or outsource economics and the recovery of clinician capacity), two study designs are worth building once as reusable protocols and proposing to every prospect:

**Priority study A: prescription capture and retention.** Measure the share of system-originated eligible specialty prescriptions the HSSP retains, with a staggered-adoption difference-in-differences where service lines or sites onboard at different times, and retained net contribution as a co-primary measure. This is the most direct economic argument for the build, expand, or outsource decision and is currently unquantified against eligible volume in all four corpora. The protocol transfers across clients with only data-source changes.

**Priority study B: clinician time-and-capacity.** Measure clinician administrative minutes on prior authorization, financial assistance, and refill coordination, and the net patient-facing capacity recovered, in matched clinics before and after HSSP integration. This addresses the workforce concern health-system executives rank first and is something almost no HSSP competitor measures directly, so it is both relevant and differentiating.

Alongside these, three reusable templates recur in every brief and should be standardized into a repeatable service: a **net-economic-impact model** with an itemized cost stack and sensitivity analysis; an **equity-stratification template**; and the **HSSP measure dictionary** described under "A standardized HSSP outcomes measurement framework for the field" below. Building these once and deploying them across clients is efficient and is the basis for a repeatable set of services rather than four custom-built engagements.

## Per-organization summary and recommended starting engagement

The four prospects sit across a maturity range. The engagement approach differs by level, but each has a low-commitment entry point that requires little or no new data and demonstrates the firm's rigor on the organization's own material.

**Clearway Health (least structured; build a research foundation).** The portfolio is a small set of executive case studies with no study design, denominators, comparators, or methods beneath them. Initial engagement: a case-study-to-evidence conversion that re-expresses each case as a structured study without losing executive readability, starting from the Boston Medical Center IBD 96%-versus-88% PDC contrast (the one near-comparative result in the entire prospect set). Most accessible asset: OU Health is already running a patient-reported-outcomes pilot and has publicly stated its intent to study whether the model reduces total cost of care, so a named partner has already committed to the exact question. Clearway is the lowest-risk near-term entry: the initial engagement needs no new data and a prospective study is already on the table.

**VytlOne/Maxor (operationally strong, methodologically thin; upgrade and link).** The case studies are endpoint-to-endpoint before/after narratives and the flagship county figure blends gross revenue, net margin, avoided cost, and one-time recoveries into a single 13-year number. Initial engagement: a net-economic-impact model with an explicit value framework on the county case, plus an interrupted-time-series capture estimate. Differentiated option: independent validation of the VytlAIQ platform (precision/recall of the continuous 340B audit, false-positive and human-review burden, pre/post operational outcomes, model governance), which is timely because the platform is sold on outcomes it has not published, and the vendor's own transcript describes its decision-maker audience as skeptical of AI.

**CPS Solutions (high volume, gated, ungoverned benchmarks; rigor and governance).** CPS produces the most content and holds the one cross-site data platform in the set (Therigy, stated at over 100,000,000 patient responses), but roughly two-thirds of the study pages are gated, the readable studies are descriptive or unadjusted, and the cross-site benchmarking states no risk adjustment. Initial engagement: a landing-page-to-protocol conversion that specifies methods on CPS's own highest-traffic studies, starting from the already-published diabetes risk-stratification study. Greatest potential: a multi-site, risk-adjusted comparative-effectiveness study of HSSP care-model variants (embedded pharmacist versus liaison-supported versus standard) using Therigy data, which answers a question no single health system can answer alone, paired with the benchmark-governance framework that makes the Therigy advantage defensible.

**Shields Health Solutions (most mature; upgrade and re-point).** Shields operates a genuine evidence engine with a peer-reviewed lineage and a multi-system actuarial cost analysis. Proposing more descriptive output would add little. Initial engagement and the highest-value single asset: a matched, quasi-experimental re-analysis of the Optum total-cost-of-care study. That study currently rests on an unmatched 197-versus-8,824 comparison with single-divisor risk adjustment and t-tests. Re-estimating with matching and difference-in-differences on the existing baseline and follow-up data converts Shields' most-cited claim from an association into a defensible causal estimate. Shields is the most demanding decision-maker and the highest-credibility reference if earned.

## The differentiated single-organization opportunities

Three opportunities have no equivalent elsewhere in the set and are where each organization can hold distinctive ground:

- **CPS, multi-site comparative effectiveness of care models** using Therigy. Only CPS can field a risk-adjusted comparison of HSSP staffing models across many health systems. This is the highest-differentiation study in the entire set.
- **Clearway, cell and gene therapy readiness evidence.** The Children's National operating model (payer negotiation, funds-flow planning, procurement pathway, and a completed ZYNTEGLO administration) is unique among the prospects but is reported as a single-patient narrative. A standardized multiple-case readiness series is a specific and tailored research gap and a long-horizon standard-setting position for the field.
- **VytlOne/Maxor, independent AI validation.** VytlAIQ is the only AI analytics platform in the set marketed on outcomes; independent pre/post evaluation with governance documentation is exactly the evidence a CIO and 340B compliance officer require and the vendor cannot credibly self-produce.

## A standardized HSSP outcomes measurement framework for the field

Gap 7 appears in all four portfolios and points to a repeatable service that is larger than any single engagement. No organization in the set publishes a standardized HSSP outcome dictionary spanning the domains health systems actually decide on: prescription capture and retention, time to therapy and access, adherence and persistence, clinician administrative burden and clinic capacity, avoidable utilization and total cost of care to the system, patient experience, equity, and accreditation and quality. A firm that defines this dictionary, with numerator and denominator logic, minimum sample-size thresholds, and case-mix risk adjustment, supplies the common reference that every individual study feeds into and positions itself as the field's measurement standard rather than a study-by-study vendor. Every per-organization engagement above can be specified to populate this common framework, which strengthens the firm's position with each client.

## Recommended sequence

1. **Lead every prospect conversation with a no-new-data initial engagement on an existing priority asset** (Clearway case-study conversion; CPS landing-page-to-protocol; VytlOne/Maxor net-economic model; Shields matched re-analysis). Each is fast, low-cost, demonstrates rigor on the organization's own material, and builds the trust required for the larger studies.

2. **Build the two priority cross-organization protocols once** (prescription capture and retention; clinician time-and-capacity) and the three reusable templates (net-economic-impact model, equity-stratification template, measure dictionary). These transfer across all four clients and are the basis for a repeatable set of services.

3. **Start with Clearway as the first prospect.** It has the lowest-risk initial engagement and a partner-requested prospective study already on the table at OU Health, which together make it the most accessible near-term entry. Develop the reusable prescription capture and clinician-capacity protocols during that engagement so they are ready to propose to CPS, VytlOne/Maxor, and Shields.

4. **Pursue the differentiated single-organization opportunities once a relationship exists**, since each (Therigy comparative effectiveness, Clearway cell and gene therapy series, VytlAIQ validation) depends on data access or platform cooperation that follows from an initial engagement rather than preceding it.

The methods proposed here are portable from the firm's current health-outcomes research work and require no payer or pharmaceutical positioning; every proposal is health-system-facing and attaches to a specific asset each organization has already produced.

## Glossary

**Health-system specialty pharmacy (HSSP):** a specialty pharmacy owned and operated by a health system to serve its own patients.

**Prescription capture (capture rate):** the share of specialty prescriptions written by a health system's providers that are filled by that system's own specialty pharmacy rather than an external pharmacy.

**Leakage:** specialty prescriptions written within a health system but filled at an outside pharmacy, so the system loses the associated dispensing revenue and visibility into the patient's care.

**Prescription retention:** keeping eligible specialty prescriptions within the system's own specialty pharmacy, that is, reducing leakage.

**340B:** a federal program that lets eligible safety-net health systems purchase outpatient drugs at reduced prices.

**Limited distribution drug (LDD):** a specialty drug a manufacturer makes available through only a small number of approved pharmacies.

**Proportion of days covered (PDC):** a standard adherence measure equal to the share of days in a period for which a patient had the medication on hand.

**Time to therapy (time to first fill):** the elapsed time from when a prescription is written to when the patient receives the medication.

**Persistence:** continuing to refill and remain on a therapy over time.

**Patient-reported outcome (PRO) / patient-reported outcome measure (PROM):** an outcome reported directly by the patient (for example symptom burden or quality of life), usually via a validated questionnaire.

**Social determinants of health (SDOH) / health-related social needs (HRSN):** non-medical factors (for example housing, transportation, food, income) that affect health and access to care.

**Total cost of care:** total spending on a patient's care across all settings over a defined period.

**Avoidable utilization:** emergency department visits, hospital admissions, or readmissions that appropriate care could have prevented.

**Value-based care (VBC):** payment and care models that tie reimbursement to outcomes and total cost rather than to volume of services.

**Cell and gene therapy:** one-time, high-cost treatments that modify a patient's cells or genes, requiring specialized handling, financing, and coordination.

**Specialty pharmacy accreditation (URAC, ACHC):** quality certifications from independent bodies that many payers and manufacturers require for network and drug access.

**Per member per month (PMPM):** a standardized cost or utilization figure expressed as the average amount per enrolled health-plan member per month.

**Medicare Advantage (MA):** privately administered Medicare health plans, an alternative to traditional fee-for-service Medicare.

**Cost object:** in cost accounting, the entity a cost is measured against (for example a payer, a health system, or a facility); re-pointing the cost object means recalculating the same outcome from the health system's standpoint instead of a payer's.

**Cost stack:** the itemized layers of cost (for example labor, drug acquisition, dispensing, technology, overhead) that make up a total, as opposed to a single gross figure.

**Net Promoter Score (NPS):** a satisfaction measure based on how likely respondents are to recommend a service.

**Full-time equivalent (FTE):** a unit expressing staffing in terms of full-time positions.

**Comparison group (comparator):** a group that did not receive the intervention, used as a baseline to judge the intervention's effect.

**Quasi-experimental design:** a study that estimates cause and effect without randomly assigning participants, by constructing comparison groups or using repeated measurements over time.

**Propensity-score matching:** a method that pairs intervention and comparison participants who have similar characteristics so the two groups are comparable.

**Risk adjustment (case-mix adjustment):** statistically accounting for differences in participants' health and complexity so comparisons are fair.

**Difference-in-differences:** a method that compares the before-and-after change in a group that received an intervention with the change over the same period in a group that did not, to isolate the intervention's effect.

**Interrupted time series:** a method that measures an outcome at many points before and after an intervention to separate the intervention's effect from pre-existing trends.

**Counterfactual:** an estimate of what would have happened without the intervention.

**Comparative effectiveness:** research that directly compares two or more care approaches to determine which produces better outcomes.

**Sensitivity analysis:** re-running an analysis under different assumptions to test whether the conclusions hold.

**Entropy balancing:** a reweighting method that makes a comparison group statistically similar to the intervention group on chosen characteristics without one-to-one pairing.

**Funnel plot:** a statistical display that shows each site's result against the range of variation expected by chance, used to flag true outliers in benchmarking.

**Co-primary measure:** one of two primary outcomes of equal importance in a study.
