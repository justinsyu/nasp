Glossary

Clinical and operational terms

Clinical, operational, and research-method terms used across this brief. 34 terms

Health-system specialty pharmacy (HSSP)
a specialty pharmacy owned and operated by a health system to serve its own patients, as opposed to an external or contract pharmacy (sometimes abbreviated HSSP).
Prescription capture (capture rate)
the share of specialty prescriptions written by a health system's providers that are filled by that system's own specialty pharmacy rather than an external pharmacy.
Leakage
specialty prescriptions written within a health system but filled at an outside pharmacy, so the system loses the associated dispensing revenue and visibility into the patient's care.
Prescription retention
keeping eligible specialty prescriptions within the system's own specialty pharmacy, that is, reducing leakage.
340B
a federal program that lets eligible safety-net health systems purchase outpatient drugs at reduced prices.
Contract pharmacy
an outside pharmacy a 340B health system contracts with to dispense its drugs; displacing it with an owned pharmacy keeps the dispensing in-house.
Limited distribution drug (LDD)
a specialty drug a manufacturer makes available through only a small number of approved pharmacies.
Proportion of days covered (PDC)
a standard adherence measure equal to the share of days in a period for which a patient had the medication on hand.
Time to therapy (time to first fill)
the elapsed time from when a prescription is written to when the patient receives the medication.
Persistence
continuing to refill and remain on a therapy over time.
Sustained virologic response at 12 weeks (SVR12)
an undetectable hepatitis C viral level 12 weeks after treatment, used as the marker of cure.
Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD)
chronic inflammatory conditions of the gut, including Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis.
Harvey Bradshaw Index
a validated index of Crohn's disease activity; lower scores indicate less active disease.
Chronic myeloid leukemia (CML)
a type of blood cancer.
Major molecular response (MMR)
a defined reduction in measurable leukemia burden used to gauge response to chronic myeloid leukemia therapy.
Hepatitis C virus (HCV)
a viral liver infection that specialty antiviral therapy can cure.
Viral suppression
an HIV viral level reduced below a detectable threshold, indicating effective treatment.
Ryan White Program (HRSA)
a federal program administered by the Health Resources and Services Administration that funds and reports on HIV care for low-income patients.
Patient-reported outcome (PRO)
an outcome reported directly by the patient (for example symptom burden or quality of life), usually via a validated questionnaire.
Linkage to care
the steps by which a patient identified with a condition is connected to and started on appropriate treatment.
Cell and gene therapy
one-time, high-cost treatments that modify a patient's cells or genes, requiring specialized handling, financing, and coordination.
Total cost of care
total spending on a patient's care across all settings over a defined period.
Net Promoter Score (NPS)
a satisfaction measure based on how likely respondents are to recommend a service.
HCAHPS
a standardized national survey of patients' experience of hospital care.
Full-time equivalent (FTE)
a unit expressing staffing in terms of full-time positions.
Third-party administrator (TPA)
an organization that processes claims or administers benefits on behalf of a payer or plan.
Specialty pharmacy accreditation (URAC, ACHC)
quality certifications from independent bodies that many payers and manufacturers require for network and drug access.
Comparison group (comparator)
a group that did not receive the intervention, used as a baseline to judge the intervention's effect.
Retrospective cohort study
a study that follows a defined group of patients using existing records to assess outcomes after an intervention or exposure.
Interrupted time series
a method that measures an outcome at many points before and after an intervention to separate the intervention's effect from pre-existing trends.
Time-and-motion study
direct observation and timing of tasks to measure how staff time is spent.
Attribution
determining how much of an observed result is caused by the intervention rather than by other concurrent changes.
Confidence interval
a range around an estimate that expresses how much uncertainty it carries.
Sensitivity analysis
re-running an analysis under different assumptions to test whether the conclusions hold.

Approach snapshot

Research approach snapshot

Clearway's public evidence base consists almost entirely of executive-facing case studies built around named health-system partners (Boston Medical Center Health System, Children's National Hospital, Signature Healthcare, OU Health, and The Brooklyn Hospital Center). These assets are effective at executive persuasion. They pair financial results with named clinical and operational metrics and direct quotations from chief pharmacy officers, chief financial officers, and ambulatory leaders.

The financial results are substantial. BMC specialty pharmacy revenue grew from $20 million to $600 million from 2012 to 2022; Signature contributed $31.7 million since August 2022, $13 million above budget; OU Health reported more than $288 million in two years; and Children's National reported $57 million from October 2023 through September 2024, $20 million over forecast. The genuine strengths are the named-partner proof points, the safety-net/academic/pediatric/community-hospital mix that matches the target decision-maker, and a consistent operating-model narrative (embedded liaison model, 340B sustainability, contract-pharmacy displacement, accreditation).

The genuine limitation is methodological. Across the portfolio, results are reported without study design, denominators, baseline periods, comparator construction, attribution logic, or statistical methods. Clearway has the least structured outcomes research of the four prospects under review, which makes the central opportunity the construction of a research foundation rather than the addition of more content.

Quality

Quality-enhancement opportunities

Each item names the asset, the observed weakness, the specific HEOR upgrade, and why the upgrade strengthens the case for a health-system decision-maker.

  • 2.1 QualityThe BMC inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) enrolled-versus-non-enrolled contrast is the only near-comparative result and is reported without design
    AssetBoston Medical Center case study PDF (66fb7bc11e664f364d47ba17_..._Boston_Medical_Center_Health_System_Case_Study.pdf), Results pages.
    Observed weaknessThe report states 96% proportion of days covered (PDC) for enrolled IBD participants versus 88% for non-enrolled, plus a "more drastic change in average Harvey Bradshaw Index over 12 months," and 76% major molecular response (MMR) at year two for enrolled chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) participants. No denominators, cohort definitions, enrollment criteria, follow-up windows, baseline equivalence, or adjustment for confounding are given. The 95% PDC and the 89% HIV viral suppression and 90% sustained virologic response at 12 weeks (SVR12) are single-arm point estimates with no comparator and no industry-source citation beyond a stated 60% PDC norm.
    HEOR upgradeConvert the existing enrolled-versus-non-enrolled comparison into a structured retrospective cohort study with explicit inclusion/exclusion criteria, a defined index date, a fixed measurement window, and adjustment for measurable confounders (age, payer, baseline severity, regimen). Report the PDC difference with a confidence interval and a pre-specified adherence definition. Add denominators to the SVR12, viral suppression, and major molecular response estimates and benchmark each against published disease-state literature.
    Why it strengthens the caseA pharmacy director or service-line chief deciding whether to enroll a disease cohort needs to know the size of the enrolled-versus-non-enrolled effect and whether it survives adjustment, not a single percentage. A defensible comparison is what distinguishes an outcomes claim from a testimonial when the decision-maker's quality committee reviews it.
  • 2.2 QualityThe Brooklyn Hospital Center HIV result is a strong adherence signal reported as an unqualified absolute
    Assethospital-executives-how-specialty-pharmacy-can-be-a-lever-for-health-system-mission-centric-change.html (Becker's webinar summary).
    Observed weaknessThe page reports "100% viral suppression" and "zero missed doses in well over a year" among approximately 190 participants on injectable HIV therapy, and a hepatitis C virus (HCV) "test to treat" dashboard that changed testing workflow. The denominator (approximately 190) is stated, but the observation period ("well over a year"), the definition of a missed dose, the suppression measurement protocol, and the pre-program baseline are not. There is no comparator period.
    HEOR upgradeReframe as a pre/post interrupted-time-series or single-arm longitudinal study with a defined cohort, a stated baseline missed-dose rate and suppression rate before the embedded program, explicit measurement intervals, and a denominator-anchored persistence curve. For the HCV test-to-treat claim, define the linkage-to-care cascade (antibody-positive, confirmed, linked, treated, SVR12) with counts at each step.
    Why it strengthens the caseInjectable HIV adherence and HCV linkage-to-care are quality and population-health metrics that infectious disease service lines and quality leaders already report. A cascade with denominators is directly comparable to the system's own internal benchmarks and to Ryan White Program (Health Resources and Services Administration, HRSA) reporting, which raises its credibility for a clinical audience.
  • 2.3 QualityFinancial claims are gross and lack net-economic framing
    AssetsBMC PDF ($20M to $600M; more than $580M over 10 years); Signature PDF ($31.7M since opening, $13M over budget; 100% average month-over-month revenue growth; the monthly contract-versus-onsite revenue and margin chart on page 6); OU Health PDF and HTML (more than $288M in two years); Children's National PDF ($57M, $20M over forecast; 39% additional previously ineligible claims month over month); the Children's National "Impact of In-house Specialty Pharmacy Services for 340B Eligible Health Systems" illustration (+34%, +37%, +27% gross margin across three example drugs).
    Observed weaknessRevenue and budget-overperformance figures are reported gross. None of the assets nets out implementation cost, pharmacy build cost (Children's National opened a new pharmacy with five new staff in November 2024), staffing cost (Signature: seven liaisons, one clinical pharmacist, three pharmacists, four technicians; Children's National: comparable staffing), technology cost (the Charter platform), inventory carrying cost and working capital, bad debt, or 340B program expense. The +34%/+37%/+27% margin example is a generic per-drug illustration ("revenue minus 340B expense, administration and dispensing fees"), not a measured system-level net result, and the three drugs are unnamed.
    HEOR upgradeBuild a net-economic-impact-to-the-system model for the hospital-owned specialty pharmacy, with a transparent margin definition, an itemized cost stack (labor, technology, inventory, build/amortization, patient-assistance outlay, avoided contract-pharmacy dispensing fees), a stated time horizon, and one- and two-way sensitivity analyses on the largest drivers (payer mix, capture rate, 340B eligibility share, drug-mix margin). Re-present the gross revenue figures as net contribution with the cost stack shown.
    Why it strengthens the caseA CFO or system-finance leader evaluating build-versus-outsource needs net contribution and a break-even timeline, not gross revenue. A sensitivity analysis also pre-empts the most common executive objection, that the headline number is not repeatable in a different payer or 340B environment.
  • 2.4 QualityMulti-component results are reported without attribution logic
    AssetsChildren's National PDF (938% patient growth in two years; prior authorization (PA) turnaround from up to 42 days to 1.2 days or less; 39% additional eligible claims; $57M revenue), with the same period also featuring a new pharmacy build, ACHC and URAC accreditation, EHR/claims/third-party administrator (TPA) data analysis, and 340B eligibility expansion; OU Health PDF (1,291 first-year to 2,742 patients; 9,578 PAs at 91% approval; less than 1.8 days versus a 17-day industry reference).
    Observed weaknessEach headline outcome co-occurs with several simultaneous changes (staffing, contracting, EHR workflow, payer-mix shifts, accreditation, 340B-rule changes, market conditions). The reports attribute outcomes to the partnership as a whole without separating the contribution of individual components.
    HEOR upgradeApply a structured attribution framework (a driver decomposition or, where data permit, a segmented interrupted-time-series keyed to discrete implementation milestones such as liaison embedding, pharmacy opening, and accreditation dates). State explicitly which results are plausibly attributable to the operating model versus concurrent system or market changes, and label the remainder as associative.
    Why it strengthens the caseA decision-maker replicating the model in its own environment needs to know which components drive which results so it can budget and sequence them. Honest attribution also raises the credibility of the claims that do hold.
  • 2.5 QualityClinical outcomes are uneven across the portfolio and not standardized
    AssetsBMC PDF (PDC, SVR12, viral suppression, Harvey Bradshaw Index, major molecular response); OU Health PDF (PA approval, enrollment, fertility cost savings, a stated pilot of patient-reported outcomes (PRO) in HIV, HCV, rheumatoid arthritis, migraine, and multiple sclerosis); Signature PDF (PA volume and turnaround, prescription volume, enrollment percentage, but no longitudinal clinical endpoints); Children's National PDF (PA turnaround, growth, Net Promoter Score (NPS), but no disease-state clinical endpoints).
    Observed weaknessDisease-state clinical outcomes are reported richly for BMC, partially for OU Health, and largely not for Signature or Children's National. The same disease (for example HCV, present at BMC, Brooklyn, OU Health, and Signature) is measured differently or not at all across sites, preventing cross-site comparison.
    HEOR upgradeDefine a standardized disease-state outcome set (for example, for HCV: SVR12 with a defined cascade; for IBD: PDC plus a validated severity index; for HIV: viral suppression and persistence) with common denominators, windows, and definitions, then apply it across partner sites to produce a multi-site outcomes series.
    Why it strengthens the caseA prospective decision-maker wants to see that the model produces consistent clinical results across hospital types, not a single strong site. Standardization is also the precondition for any future pooled or multi-site analysis.
  • 2.6 QualityProvider and clinician burden reduction is claimed repeatedly but rarely quantified
    AssetsChildren's National PDF (provider/clinician NPS of 100 with three survey dimensions: "Saves time," "Improves patient adherence," "Provides a better care experience"; provider free-text testimonials); Signature PDF (provider NPS of 100, captured twice yearly; testimonials); OU Health PDF/HTML (provider NPS of 91; a statement that the monthly dashboard tracks the clinic-embedded team's impact on Hospital Consumer Assessment of Healthcare Providers and Systems (HCAHPS) questions); Brooklyn page (providers relieved of prior-authorization work); the GLP-1 and MTM articles (liaison time-saving vignettes).
    Observed weaknessBurden reduction is asserted through satisfaction scores and anecdotes. None of the assets quantifies clinician hours saved, prior-authorization tasks shifted out of the clinic, appeals handled, calls avoided, rework prevented, or clinic visit capacity recovered. The NPS instrument, sample size, and response rate are not described.
    HEOR upgradeRun a quantified clinician-burden study using time-and-motion or task-volume methods: count prior-authorization tasks and appeals absorbed by the pharmacy team per clinic per month, estimate clinician and nurse minutes returned, and convert to recovered visit capacity. Standardize the satisfaction instrument and report sample size and response rate alongside the NPS.
    Why it strengthens the caseClinic capacity and clinician administrative burden are operational and financial currency for ambulatory operations leaders and service-line chiefs. A figure such as "X prior-authorization tasks and Y clinician hours per clinic per month returned to direct care" supports both a staffing business case and a clinician-retention argument, which testimonials cannot.
  • 2.7 QualityEquity and access claims are central but not stratified
    Assetsall five case studies emphasize safety-net, pediatric, rural, low-income, and Medicaid populations; the MTM article cites low health literacy, language, and transportation barriers; the GLP-1 article describes a single diabetes patient's six-month coverage delay resolved by a liaison.
    Observed weaknessAccess, affordability, patient-assistance, and adherence outcomes are reported in aggregate. They are not stratified by payer type, language, geography (urban versus rural), or social-risk proxy, so the equity claim is narrative rather than measured.
    HEOR upgradeStratify the existing access and adherence outcomes (time-to-therapy, PA approval, patient-assistance dollars secured, PDC) by payer, language, and geography where the data exist, and report disparities closed or persisting.
    Why it strengthens the caseMission and community-benefit leaders, and accreditation/quality leaders, must document equity for their own reporting. Stratified outcomes let the system use Clearway's data directly in its community-benefit and quality narratives.

Relevance

Relevance-enhancement opportunities

Each item names the gap or unaddressed question, the proposed study or reframing, the design, the primary outcome domain, and the health-system decision and audience served.

  • 3.1 RelevanceBuild-versus-outsource decision is implied but never modeled at the system level
    GapThe Signature and Children's National cases are fundamentally build-versus-outsource stories (displacing contract pharmacies with an owned pharmacy), yet neither presents a net build-versus-outsource model the decision-maker can apply.
    Proposed workA net-economic-impact and capture-and-retention model that quantifies contribution from prescription capture and retention, avoided contract-pharmacy dispensing fees, and 340B margin, against the full cost of building and operating an owned pharmacy.
    DesignDeterministic financial model with probabilistic sensitivity analysis, parameterized from one or more partner sites (Signature's monthly contract-versus-onsite revenue and margin series is suitable raw material for the capture-shift curve).
    Primary outcome domainTotal economic contribution to the system and prescription capture and retention.
    Decision and audienceBuild, expand, or outsource the specialty pharmacy; system finance, C-suite, and pharmacy leadership.
  • 3.2 RelevancePrior-authorization speed is reported but not connected to time-to-therapy or downstream utilization
    GapPA turnaround is the most consistently reported metric (BMC 2 days; Signature 1.4 business days; Children's National 1.2 days or less from up to 42; OU Health less than 1.8 days versus a 17-day reference). It is presented as an operational statistic, not connected to time-to-first-fill, treatment initiation rate, or avoidable utilization.
    Proposed workLink PA turnaround to a patient-level time-to-therapy endpoint and, where feasible, to treatment-initiation and early-discontinuation rates; frame faster initiation as access and quality for the system's own patients.
    DesignRetrospective cohort with time-to-event (time from prescription to first fill) analysis; optional pre/post around liaison embedding.
    Primary outcome domainPatient access and time-to-therapy.
    Decision and audienceWhether embedded liaisons justify their cost through faster, more reliable therapy starts; ambulatory operations, service-line chiefs, and quality leaders.
  • 3.3 RelevanceClinician burden and clinic capacity
    GapBurden reduction is the most repeated qualitative claim and the least quantified (see 2.6).
    Proposed workA quantified clinician-burden and clinic-capacity study (PA tasks and appeals absorbed, clinician/nurse minutes returned, recovered visit capacity).
    DesignTime-and-motion or task-volume study with a pre/post or embedded-versus-non-embedded clinic comparison.
    Primary outcome domainClinician administrative burden and clinic capacity.
    Decision and audienceStaffing and full-time equivalent (FTE) justification, clinician retention; ambulatory operations, service-line chiefs, pharmacy leadership.
  • 3.4 RelevanceCell and gene therapy operating-model readiness
    GapThe Children's National case describes a cell and gene therapy operating model (payer negotiation, legal and risk management, procurement pathway, revenue-integrity coordination, funds-flow planning, and execution of a ZYNTEGLO treatment for transfusion-dependent beta-thalassemia) but presents it as a narrative, with no readiness framework, no time-to-treat or cost-recovery metrics, and a single patient.
    Proposed workA structured cell and gene therapy readiness and operating-model evidence study: a documented framework (decision points, stakeholders, funds-flow scenarios) plus measured outcomes per case (time from referral to administration, payer-coverage outcome, institutional financial exposure and recovery).
    DesignMultiple-case study series with a standardized data-capture template across cases as volume accrues.
    Primary outcome domainOperating-model readiness, access to one-time high-cost therapies, and institutional financial integrity.
    Decision and audienceWhether the system can safely take on cell and gene therapy in-house; C-suite, pharmacy leadership, system finance, and pediatric or specialty service-line chiefs. This is the most differentiated content in the portfolio and has no equivalent among the comparison prospects.
  • 3.5 RelevancePatient-reported outcomes research is already underway at OU Health and is not yet used
    GapThe OU Health case study states the program is "currently piloting patient-reported outcomes research (data collection and reporting)" in HIV, HCV, rheumatoid arthritis, migraine, and multiple sclerosis, and the fertility article states OU Health's stated next step is "to research whether these initiatives reduce overall healthcare spending by improving patient outcomes." This is an active research collaboration that is not yet structured or published.
    Proposed workCo-design the PRO study protocol (instrument selection, sampling, analysis plan) and the linked utilization/total-cost-of-care analysis the system has already said it wants, converting an internal pilot into a defensible multi-condition outcomes study.
    DesignProspective PRO cohort with a linked retrospective utilization and total-cost-of-care-to-the-system analysis.
    Primary outcome domainPatient-reported outcomes plus total cost of care to the system and avoidable utilization.
    Decision and audienceWhether the embedded model improves outcomes and lowers system spend; quality leaders, system finance, and pharmacy leadership. This is the single most credible initial engagement because the partner has already committed to the question.
  • 3.6 RelevanceQuality and accreditation framed as access and care quality, not payer economics
    GapACHC and URAC accreditation (Children's National February and June 2025; Signature June 2024) and the HCAHPS-tracking statement (OU Health) are presented as gateways to payer and limited-distribution-drug access. For a health-system audience the more relevant framing is internal quality, accreditation readiness, and the patient-experience contribution of the embedded team.
    Proposed workDocument the accreditation pathway as a quality-infrastructure case and, separately, quantify the embedded pharmacy team's measured contribution to HCAHPS and patient-experience items (building on OU Health's existing dashboard).
    DesignProcess case study plus a pre/post or correlational HCAHPS analysis.
    Primary outcome domainQuality, accreditation readiness, and patient experience.
    Decision and audienceQuality and accreditation leaders, ambulatory operations.
  • 3.7 RelevanceEquity-stratified outcomes for safety-net populations
    Gapsee 2.7; equity is asserted system-wide but never stratified.
    Proposed workAn equity-stratified analysis of access, affordability, patient-assistance, and adherence outcomes.
    DesignRetrospective cohort with stratification by payer, language, and geography.
    Primary outcome domainEquity in access, affordability, and adherence for the system's population.
    Decision and audienceMission/community-benefit leaders, quality leaders, C-suite.

Differentiation

Differentiation note

These opportunities are credible and specific and tailored for three reasons. First, every one is anchored to a specific named asset and a specific documented gap, not to generic HEOR offerings. For example, the BMC IBD 96%-versus-88% PDC contrast is the seed for a structured cohort; OU Health's already-running PRO pilot is the seed for a multi-condition outcomes study; and Signature's monthly revenue/margin chart is the input to a net-economic model. Second, the proposed designs are matched to the data Clearway already holds (retrospective cohorts, interrupted time series, time-and-motion, and case series). As a result, the work is feasible without new primary data collection in most cases. Third, all outcome domains are health-system-facing (total cost of care to the system, prescription capture and retention, clinic capacity, accreditation and quality, equity for the system's own population, and cell and gene therapy readiness). None is a payer, PBM, or manufacturer deliverable.

The strategic point is that Clearway currently has the least structured research of the four prospects under review. That is the opportunity, not a disqualifier. The portfolio is a small set of high-quality persuasion assets with no research foundation beneath them. The right first engagement is not to produce more case studies but to convert the existing ones into structured evidence and to stand up the one prospective study a partner (OU Health) has already asked for.

Positioning. A case-study-to-evidence conversion program plus the OU Health PRO/total-cost study would give Clearway a defensible research foundation that its current case-study-only approach lacks, and would let its strongest assets withstand scrutiny from the quality and finance reviewers who sit inside the health systems it is selling to.
