Glossary

Glossary

Clinical, operational, and method terms used across this brief. 28 terms

Health-system specialty pharmacy (HSSP)
A specialty pharmacy owned and operated by a health system to serve its own patients.
Prescription capture (capture rate)
The share of specialty prescriptions written by a health system's providers that are filled by that system's own specialty pharmacy rather than an external pharmacy.
Leakage
Specialty prescriptions written within a health system but filled at an outside pharmacy, so the system loses the associated dispensing revenue and visibility into the patient's care.
Prescription retention
Keeping eligible specialty prescriptions within the system's own specialty pharmacy, that is, reducing leakage.
Time to therapy (time to first fill)
The elapsed time from when a prescription is written to when the patient receives the medication.
Persistence
Continuing to refill and remain on a therapy over time.
Abandonment
A prescription that is approved or ready but never picked up or filled.
Patient-reported outcome (PRO) / patient-reported outcome measure (PROM)
An outcome reported directly by the patient (for example symptom burden or quality of life), usually via a validated questionnaire.
Minimal clinically important difference (MCID)
The smallest change in an outcome measure that patients or clinicians would consider meaningful.
Hemoglobin A1c (A1C)
A blood marker of average blood glucose over roughly three months, used to gauge diabetes control.
Dermatology Life Quality Index (DLQI)
A validated patient questionnaire measuring the effect of skin disease on quality of life.
Patient-reported outcome instruments (AIRQ, PozQoL, PROMIS-10)
The Asthma Impairment and Risk Questionnaire (AIRQ) is a validated questionnaire assessing asthma control; PozQoL is a validated questionnaire measuring quality of life for people living with HIV; PROMIS-10 is a short, validated general measure of physical and mental health-related quality of life.
Health-related quality of life (HRQoL)
A measure of how health affects a person's overall well-being and daily functioning.
Social determinants of health (SDOH) / health-related social needs (HRSN)
Non-medical factors (for example housing, transportation, food, income) that affect health and access to care.
Total cost of care
Total spending on a patient's care across all settings over a defined period.
Specialty pharmacy accreditation (URAC, ACHC)
Quality certifications from independent bodies that many payers and manufacturers require for network and drug access.
Comparison group (comparator)
A group that did not receive the intervention, used as a baseline to judge the intervention's effect.
Propensity-score matching
A method that pairs intervention and comparison participants who have similar characteristics so the two groups are comparable.
Entropy balancing
A weighting method that makes a comparison group statistically similar to the intervention group on chosen characteristics.
Risk adjustment (case-mix adjustment)
Statistically accounting for differences in participants' health and complexity so comparisons are fair.
Difference-in-differences
A method that compares the before-and-after change in a group that received an intervention with the change over the same period in a group that did not, to isolate the intervention's effect.
Interrupted time series
A method that measures an outcome at many points before and after an intervention to separate the intervention's effect from pre-existing trends.
Funnel plot
A statistical display that shows each site's result against the range of variation expected by chance, used to flag true outliers in benchmarking.
Mixed-effects model
A regression method that accounts for data grouped within sites or clusters when estimating an effect.
Counterfactual
An estimate of what would have happened without the intervention.
Confidence interval
A range around an estimate that expresses how much uncertainty it carries.
Sensitivity analysis
Re-running an analysis under different assumptions to test whether the conclusions hold.
Comparative effectiveness
Research that directly compares two or more care approaches to determine which produces better outcomes.

Approach snapshot

Research approach snapshot

CPS operates a high-volume HSSP evidence content program built around four recurring formats: an annual Patient Impact Report (now in its fifth edition, 2026-patient-impact-report.html), a large catalog of single-study landing pages tied to conference posters (ISPOR, AMCP, ASHP Midyear, NASP), case studies, and Therigy data/dashboard assets.

Genuine strengths that should not be re-proposed as if absent:

  • A centralized outcomes-research team that produces a steady poster pipeline across at least a dozen disease states, with a recurring author core including Casey Fitzpatrick, Jessica Mourani, Carly Giavatto, and Ana Lopez-Medina.
  • At least one peer-reviewed publication: the diabetes risk-stratification study in the Journal of Pharmacy Practice.
  • Growing methodological ambition, including a machine-learning and regression analysis of rheumatoid arthritis medication persistence recognized as an ISPOR 2026 Top 5% finalist (cps-hssp-research-top-5-ispor-2026.html).
  • A cross-site data platform (Therigy) that is a structurally distinctive asset for multi-site research.

The principal limitations are consistent and addressable:

  • Almost all study detail sits behind lead-capture forms, so methods are not publicly verifiable.
  • The readable studies are descriptive, single-site, or pre-post without a controlled or risk-adjusted comparator.
  • Cross-site benchmarking (the 2023 Data & Analytics Report and the dashboard network studies) is asserted without any stated risk adjustment or site-mix adjustment.
  • Clinical results are rarely connected to the system-level economic or operational decision they are meant to inform.

The opportunity is not more volume. It is converting a large descriptive catalog into a smaller number of fully specified, comparator-anchored, risk-adjusted studies, and using the Therigy multi-site asset to answer questions no single site can answer alone.

Quality enhancement

Quality-enhancement opportunities

Each card pairs a CPS asset with its observed methodological weakness, a specific HEOR upgrade, and why the upgrade strengthens the health-system case.

  • Asset (file)Risk_Strat_Poster_AMCP_Final.pdf and its JPP publication (outcomes-study-risk-stratification-jpp-published.html); the most complete CPS study available Quality
    Observed methodological weaknessRetrospective descriptive single-site study (SOMC). The Not Enrolled group (n=100) is a non-equivalent, self-selected comparison. It comprises patients who did not fill with the HSSP, so enrollment is confounded with engagement, access, and severity. Within-group hemoglobin A1c (A1C) change is tested with a paired t-test (p<2.2e-16), but the between-group difference versus Not Enrolled is not adjusted for baseline A1C or covariates. The cost figure ($736 per 1% A1C reduction, applied to derive $170,368) is a literature-borrowed unit cost, not a measured cost, and it ignores baseline imbalance (high-risk baseline A1C 11.47 vs Not Enrolled 7.99).
    Specific HEOR upgradeRe-estimate the enrolled-versus-Not-Enrolled contrast with propensity-score or entropy-balancing matching on baseline A1C, age, comorbidity, and payer. Report a covariate balance table (standardized mean differences) and the adjusted difference-in-differences (DiD) in A1C with confidence intervals. Replace the applied-unit-cost savings with a measured or, at minimum, probabilistic estimate carrying a range.
    Why it strengthens the health-system caseA pharmacy director or chief financial officer (CFO) deciding whether to staff embedded chronic-disease-management pharmacists will discount an unmatched comparison where the comparator started two A1C points lower. A matched, adjusted estimate is the standard those audiences apply to other staffing decisions.

Differentiation

Differentiation note

CPS already produces more HSSP outcomes content than almost any organization in this set: an annual benchmark report in its fifth edition, a continuous conference-poster pipeline, peer-reviewed and award-recognized work, and a cross-site data platform at 100,000,000+ responses. The firm should therefore not compete on volume. What CPS does not currently do, on the public evidence, is set out below, and it is precisely what we add.

  • Rigor and verifiability: The readable studies are descriptive, single-site, or unadjusted pre-post, and most detail is gated. Our contribution is comparator-anchored, risk-adjusted, fully specified protocols (propensity-score matching, interrupted time series, staggered-adoption difference-in-differences) applied to CPS's own material rather than new marketing.
  • Benchmark governance: CPS markets cross-site comparison but states no risk adjustment, no measure dictionary, and no sample-size thresholds, so the heterogeneity of health-system populations is an unmanaged threat to every benchmark. We supply the governance that makes the Therigy advantage defensible rather than merely large.
  • Multi-site comparative question: CPS's distinctive asset is the only one in this prospect set that can compare care-model variants across many health systems with risk adjustment, and CPS has not yet deployed it that way. That is a differentiated, specific, and tailored study only CPS can credibly field.
  • Decision translation: CPS reports clinical and PROM endpoints and one borrowed-unit-cost economic figure, but does not connect outcomes to the system-level decisions (build, expand, retain, optimize) that its health-system customers actually make. We add the prescription-retention, clinician-capacity, avoidable-utilization, and net-contribution layer that turns clinical proof into a case the system's board can act on.

In each instance the addition is rigor and comparability, not more content.
