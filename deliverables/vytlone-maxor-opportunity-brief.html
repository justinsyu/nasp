Glossary

Terms

Clinical, operational, and research-method terms used across this brief. 33 terms

Health-system specialty pharmacy (HSSP)
A specialty pharmacy owned and operated by a health system to serve its own patients.
Electronic health record (EHR)
The digital record of a patient's clinical information across a health system.
Protected health information (PHI)
Individually identifiable health data subject to privacy and security rules.
Prescription capture (capture rate)
The share of specialty prescriptions written by a health system's providers that are filled by that system's own specialty pharmacy rather than an external pharmacy.
Leakage
Specialty prescriptions written within a health system but filled at an outside pharmacy, so the system loses the associated dispensing revenue and visibility into the patient's care.
Prescription retention
Keeping eligible specialty prescriptions within the system's own specialty pharmacy, that is, reducing leakage.
340B
A federal program that lets eligible safety-net health systems purchase outpatient drugs at reduced prices.
Limited distribution drug (LDD)
A specialty drug a manufacturer makes available through only a small number of approved pharmacies.
Wholesale acquisition cost (WAC)
A drug's manufacturer-set list price to wholesalers, before discounts.
Route of administration codes (SC, IV, IN, PO)
Subcutaneous (under the skin), intravenous (into a vein), intranasal (into the nose), and oral (by mouth).
Prior authorization (PA)
An insurer requirement that a prescription be approved before it is covered, a common cause of delay in starting therapy.
Prescription access liaison (PAL)
A staff role that helps patients clear access barriers such as prior authorization and copay assistance.
Time to therapy (time to first fill)
The elapsed time from when a prescription is written to when the patient receives the medication.
Persistence
Continuing to refill and remain on a therapy over time.
Abandonment
A prescription that is approved or ready but never picked up or filled.
Proportion of days covered (PDC)
A standard adherence measure equal to the share of days in a period for which a patient had the medication on hand.
Total cost of care
Total spending on a patient's care across all settings over a defined period.
Pharmacy desert
An area with limited or no convenient access to a pharmacy.
REMS (Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy)
An FDA-required safety program for certain drugs that adds dispensing, monitoring, or enrollment requirements.
Cold chain
Temperature-controlled handling and storage required for certain specialty medications.
Specialty pharmacy accreditation (URAC, ACHC)
Quality certifications from independent bodies that many payers and manufacturers require for network and drug access.
Per member per month (PMPM)
A standardized cost or utilization figure expressed as the average amount per enrolled health-plan member per month (a payer-side measure, out of scope for this brief).
Comparison group (comparator)
A group that did not receive the intervention, used as a baseline to judge the intervention's effect.
Retrospective cohort study
A study that follows a defined group of patients using existing records to assess outcomes after an intervention or exposure.
Quasi-experimental design
A study that estimates cause and effect without randomly assigning participants, by constructing comparison groups or using repeated measurements over time.
Difference-in-differences
A method that compares the before-and-after change in a group that received an intervention with the change over the same period in a group that did not, to isolate the intervention's effect.
Interrupted time series
A method that measures an outcome at many points before and after an intervention to separate the intervention's effect from pre-existing trends.
Segmented regression
The statistical technique used in an interrupted time series to estimate the change in level and trend at the point of intervention.
Stepped-wedge rollout
A study design in which an intervention is introduced to sites in a staggered sequence, so each site serves as its own control before it receives the intervention.
Precision and recall
Measures of how accurate an automated flag is; precision is the share of flags that are correct, recall is the share of true cases the system catches.
Counterfactual
An estimate of what would have happened without the intervention.
Confidence interval
A range around an estimate that expresses how much uncertainty it carries.
Sensitivity analysis
Re-running an analysis under different assumptions to test whether the conclusions hold.

Prepared for internal use by a health economics and outcomes research (HEOR) consulting firm serving health-system specialty pharmacy (HSSP) organizations. Customer of record for every proposal below is the health system, not payers, pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs), plan sponsors, or pharmaceutical manufacturers. VytlOne/Maxor operates several lines that are payer-facing or plan-sponsor-facing (PMPM (per member per month) trend, lowest-net-cost, that is, the lowest total cost after rebates and discounts, benefit design, plan drug spend, biosimilar plan adoption). PMPM and lowest-net-cost are payer-side measures, and those deliverables are out of scope for this brief. Where a VytlOne/Maxor asset is built for a plan-spend audience (for example the insights reports and the quarterly pipeline reports), it is referenced only to propose a reframing toward a health-system specialty pharmacy decision.

Research approach

Research approach snapshot

VytlOne/Maxor publishes for HSSP audiences through four formats: operational case studies (county health-system financial turnaround; prescription access liaison access metrics; rare-disease adherence), a recurring health-system leader survey conducted with Sage Growth Partners (second annual, N=100, with trend comparison to the 2024 N=80 wave), strategic intelligence (annual insights reports and quarterly pipeline reports), and an AI analytics platform (VytlAIQ) marketed specifically for health-system pharmacy 340B compliance, revenue capture, and liaison optimization. Genuine strengths that should not be re-proposed as if absent: concrete operational proof points stated as discrete before/after deltas (turnaround 180 to 51 minutes; prior authorization 30 days to 5.6 days; specialty capture 31% in 2021 to 64% in 2024; refill interval 38 to 29 days), a longitudinal county case spanning a 13-year partnership, a repeatable annual survey instrument with year-over-year trend data, and a structurally distinctive analytics asset (VytlAIQ) built on consolidated 340B, claims, electronic health record (EHR), and pharmacy-management data. The principal limitations are consistent across the four formats:

  • The case studies are single-site before/after narratives without denominators, baseline periods, eligibility, comparators, or statistical testing.
  • The financial claims mix gross revenue, net margin, avoided cost, and corrected billing without a consistent value framework.
  • The survey is reported without sampling frame, response rate, weighting, or stratification.
  • The VytlAIQ outcome claims are asserted without validation methods, model governance, false-positive/review burden, or pre/post measurement.

The opportunity is not more content. It is raising the causal and accounting rigor of the existing operational assets and linking operational metrics to the clinical, utilization, and capacity outcomes a health system uses to decide whether to build, expand, retain, or optimize an HSSP. Throughout, prescription capture and leakage are treated as the central economic metrics.

Add nonresponse weighting to the satisfaction measure.

A denominator-anchored, trend-controlled estimate with a staffing ROI converts a testimonial into a defensible business case for funding the role across additional clinics.

Outcome is a process proxy (refill interval) not linked to persistence, discontinuation, or downstream utilization. The case is explicitly framed for manufacturer-sponsored therapy programs, so the HSSP relevance must be made explicit.

A comparator-anchored estimate makes the high-touch model defensible as a quality intervention the system can adopt.

Year-over-year trend (N=80 to N=100) is reported without testing whether the two samples are comparable. It converts a marketing claim into an adoption-grade evidence package.

Relevance-enhancement opportunities

  • Gap / unaddressed questionOperational metrics are never linked to downstream clinical or utilization outcomes. Turnaround time, capture rate, PA turnaround, and refill interval are reported as endpoints; none is connected to time to therapy, persistence, abandonment, or avoidable ED/hospitalization for the system's own patients (Maxor-Case-Study-County-Health-System.pdf, VytlOne_Prescription_Access_Liaison_Case_Study.pdf, VytlOne_Improving_Adherence_Through_High_Touch_Interventions.pdf). Relevance
    Proposed study or reframingAn outcomes-linkage study that connects the access/operational levers to patient-level clinical and utilization endpoints the system bears: time to first fill, first-fill abandonment, persistence, and avoidable utilization at the system's own facilities.
    DesignRetrospective cohort on EHR plus pharmacy data; time-to-event methods for time to therapy; pre/post or comparator-based estimates for abandonment, persistence, and avoidable utilization, with risk adjustment.
    Primary outcome domainClinical/adherence outcomes; total cost of care to the system and avoidable utilization.
    Health-system decision / audience servedService-line chiefs, quality/accreditation leaders, and population-health/finance leaders deciding whether the HSSP model improves outcomes the system is accountable for, not only throughput.
  • Gap / unaddressed questionPrescription capture and retention is asserted but never measured against eligible volume. Capture rates (67% average; 31% to 64%) are reported without the denominator of system-originated eligible specialty prescriptions or the continuity consequence of leaked scripts. Relevance
    Proposed study or reframingA prescription capture and retention study quantifying the share of system-originated specialty prescriptions retained within the HSSP and the net revenue and care-continuity consequence of reduced leakage.
    DesignRetrospective pre/post (ideally staggered-adoption difference-in-differences across clinics or service lines) on EHR plus pharmacy claims; capture rate and retained net revenue as co-primary measures; continuity and downstream utilization as secondary.

  • Gap / unaddressed questionClinician administrative burden and clinic capacity are unmeasured. The PAL and county cases describe liaisons absorbing prior authorizations and refill coordination, but no asset measures clinician time removed or clinic capacity freed. The survey reports demand-readiness gaps (18% confident they can meet expansion demand) without quantifying the staffing capacity the HSSP frees up. Relevance
    Proposed study or reframingA clinician time-and-capacity study measuring administrative time (PA, copay, refill coordination) before and after liaison/HSSP integration in matched clinics, including reallocated visit slots or panel growth.
    DesignTime-log or EHR time-stamp measurement with matched comparison clinics; pre/post difference-in-differences; observed clinician minutes and net patient-facing capacity as primary measures.
    Primary outcome domainClinician administrative burden and clinic capacity.
    Health-system decision / audience servedService-line chiefs, ambulatory operations, and chief medical officers (CMOs) deciding whether HSSP integration relieves workforce strain; the survey shows leaders rank capacity-to-deliver as a top concern.
  • Gap / unaddressed questionNo equity-stratified access outcomes exist, despite a corpus centered on access, county/safety-net settings, HIV, rare disease, and pharmacy deserts (pharmacy-deserts.html, VytlOne_Prescription_Access_Liaison_Case_Study.pdf).     Proposed study or reframingAn equity-stratified access analysis reporting time to therapy, copay-assistance capture, capture rate, and avoidable utilization stratified by payer type, race/ethnicity, language, and rurality, and testing whether the HSSP model narrows access disparities for the system's own population.
    DesignRetrospective cohort with prespecified subgroup and interaction testing; where copay-assistance and access data exist, pre/post change among high-barrier subgroups vs others.
    Primary outcome domainPatient access / equity for the system's own population.
    Health-system decision / audience servedSystem boards, community-benefit/mission leaders, and population-health teams; answers whether the access gains are distributed equitably, which aggregate metrics cannot.
  • Gap / unaddressed questionNo standardized HSSP outcomes definitions across the case-study portfolio. Each case reports its own ad hoc metrics (turnaround in minutes; PA turnaround in days; capture as a ratio; refill interval in days) with different windows and no shared dictionary, preventing cross-site comparison or benchmarking. Relevance
    Proposed study or reframingA standardized HSSP outcomes dictionary and analytic protocol (cohort entry, index date, denominators, follow-up windows, comparators) applied uniformly across capture, time to therapy, PA turnaround, adherence/persistence, clinician burden, and patient experience.
    DesignMethods/specification engagement producing one outcome dictionary and statistical analysis plan, retro-applied to the existing case sites to make them comparable and benchmarkable.
    Primary outcome domainCross-domain (operational, access, clinical) standardization.
    Health-system decision / audience servedPharmacy leadership and quality leaders who need to compare sites and benchmark against peers; also raises the credibility of every individual case.
  • Gap / unaddressed questionPipeline and insights reports are framed for plan spend, not health-system operational readiness. VytlOne_Pipeline_Q2_2026.pdf organizes around drug approvals, wholesale acquisition cost (WAC)/annual cost, and indication expansion, yet it already carries the operationally relevant variables for HSSP readiness: route of administration (subcutaneous (SC), intravenous (IV), intranasal (IN), oral (PO)), in-office vs infusion-center delivery, infusion-time differences, limited-distribution status, and high-cost rare-disease therapies. Relevance
    Proposed study or reframingReframe the quarterly pipeline (for the HSSP audience only) into a local readiness analysis: for upcoming specialty/rare-disease therapies, estimate local eligible patient volume, monitoring burden, staffing needs, site-of-care and infusion-capacity implications, cold-chain/REMS handling, and accreditation/limited-distribution access requirements. Exclude plan-spend forecasting.
    DesignTranslational analysis layered on the existing pipeline data: map each pipeline therapy to operational readiness dimensions; where a partner system shares its case mix, produce a site-specific volume and capacity estimate.
    Primary outcome domainService-line growth; operational readiness (staffing, site of care, accreditation, cold-chain/REMS).
    Health-system decision / audience servedPharmacy leadership, service-line chiefs, and ambulatory operations planning capacity for incoming specialty therapies; converts a plan-spend artifact into an HSSP operations tool.

Differentiation

Differentiation note

VytlOne/Maxor can already generate operational proof points, an annual survey, quarterly intelligence, and an analytics platform, and does so competently; proposing more of that volume would add little. The credible, specific and tailored value is in methods their assets do not currently apply and that are difficult to staff internally without appearing self-interested:

  • Causal inference on their own operational series. The case studies are endpoint-to-endpoint before/after narratives (180 to 51 minutes; 31% to 64% capture; 38 to 29 days). Interrupted time series, segmented regression, and staggered-adoption difference-in-differences on the monthly series VytlOne/Maxor already collects separate intervention effect from secular trend; these are the designs absent across the portfolio and the ones health-system finance and quality committees expect for capital and accreditation decisions.
  • A consistent value framework and net-economic-impact attribution. The flagship financial story blends gross revenue, net margin, avoided cost, one-time billing recoveries, and operating cost into a single multi-year figure. Separating those objects and attributing them with stated assumptions and ranges is a recognized standard the assets do not adopt and the difference between a number that survives CFO reconciliation and one that does not.
  • Linking operational throughput to system-relevant outcomes. Capture, turnaround, and refill interval are reported as ends in themselves. Linking them to time to therapy, abandonment, persistence, clinician capacity, equity, and avoidable utilization at the system's own facilities re-points the evidence from "the pharmacy is busy and profitable" to "the pharmacy improves the outcomes the system is accountable for," which is what service-line, quality, and population-health leaders decide on.
  • Independent validation of VytlAIQ. The platform is marketed on outcomes ("assess us on the outcomes") with no published outcome, governance, or review-burden data, against a decision-maker audience the transcript acknowledges is skeptical of AI. A pre/post evaluation with precision/recall, false-positive and human-review burden, and model-governance documentation is independent evidence the vendor cannot credibly self-assert, and it is the form of proof a CIO and compliance officer require before adopting a tool that touches 340B and PHI.
  • Survey rigor and stratification for self-benchmarking. The recurring survey is a structurally distinctive asset, but without a disclosed sampling frame, response rate, weighting, or stratification it functions as demand-signaling rather than a benchmark. Adding methodological disclosure and peer stratification (by size, 340B status, HSSP ownership/maturity, geography) lets a health system locate itself against comparable peers, which is the difference between content and a decision tool.

Engagement approach. Because VytlOne/Maxor's evidence is operationally strong but methodologically thin, the engagement approach is upgrade and link, not build-from-scratch: each proposal attaches to a specific existing asset and raises its causal/accounting rigor or links its operational metric to a named health-system outcome and decision-maker, rather than duplicating evidence VytlOne/Maxor already produces.
