# CPS Solutions: HEOR Opportunity Brief

Prepared for internal use by a health economics and outcomes research (HEOR) consulting firm serving health-system specialty pharmacy (HSSP) organizations. The customer of record for every proposal below is the **health system**, not payers, pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs), or pharmaceutical manufacturers. Some CPS assets are framed for a payer, PBM, or manufacturer audience. One example is `the-importance-of-transparency-and-proving-your-specialty-pharmacys-outcomes.html`, which lists payers, PBMs, and manufacturers as the scrutinizing parties. Those framings are noted only to be re-pointed toward health-system decisions.

Grounding corpus: 6 PDFs and 379 HTML pages in `C:\Users\Justin\Desktop\nasp\hssp_research_downloads\CPS`. The one PDF with full methods and results is `Risk_Strat_Poster_AMCP_Final.pdf` (the SOMC diabetes risk-stratification poster), which was read in full. Approximately forty HTML pages were read in full across five clusters: the disease-state outcome studies (HCV linkage, HIV PEP, migraine, MS, pharmacist interventions, IPF, dermatology ADE, psoriasis Dermatology Life Quality Index (DLQI), diabetes), the Therigy data and dashboard assets (2023 Data & Analytics Report, 100,000,000-response milestone, TherigyInsights dashboards, TherigyVista launch, AJHP dashboard study), the annual Patient Impact Reports (2023-2026 plus spotlights), the SDOH/HRSN and PRO/PROM studies, and the business/build case studies and conference-announcement pages (ISPOR 2024, ISPOR 2026 "Top 5%," AMCP 2024, ASHP Midyear, NASP 2024). Distinctive CPS asset confirmed: the Therigy platform (TherigySTM, TherigyInsights, TherigyVista) with cross-site scale stated at 100,000,000+ patient responses, 790 clinical activities/assessments across 24+ therapeutic categories, and (as of the 2018 milestone) 120 pharmacies and 800,000+ patients managed.

## 1. Research approach snapshot

CPS operates a high-volume HSSP evidence content program built around four recurring formats: an annual Patient Impact Report (now in its fifth edition, `2026-patient-impact-report.html`), a large catalog of single-study landing pages tied to conference posters (ISPOR, AMCP, ASHP Midyear, NASP), case studies, and Therigy data/dashboard assets.

Genuine strengths that should not be re-proposed as if absent:
- A centralized outcomes-research team that produces a steady poster pipeline across at least a dozen disease states, with a recurring author core including Casey Fitzpatrick, Jessica Mourani, Carly Giavatto, and Ana Lopez-Medina.
- At least one peer-reviewed publication: the diabetes risk-stratification study in the Journal of Pharmacy Practice.
- Growing methodological ambition, including a machine-learning and regression analysis of rheumatoid arthritis medication persistence recognized as an ISPOR 2026 Top 5% finalist (`cps-hssp-research-top-5-ispor-2026.html`).
- A cross-site data platform (Therigy) that is a structurally distinctive asset for multi-site research.

The principal limitations are consistent and addressable:
- Almost all study detail sits behind lead-capture forms, so methods are not publicly verifiable.
- The readable studies are descriptive, single-site, or pre-post without a controlled or risk-adjusted comparator.
- Cross-site benchmarking (the 2023 Data & Analytics Report and the dashboard network studies) is asserted without any stated risk adjustment or site-mix adjustment.
- Clinical results are rarely connected to the system-level economic or operational decision they are meant to inform.

The opportunity is not more volume. It is converting a large descriptive catalog into a smaller number of fully specified, comparator-anchored, risk-adjusted studies, and using the Therigy multi-site asset to answer questions no single site can answer alone.

## 4. Top priority engagements

Ranked by value combined with feasibility and credibility. Each builds on an existing CPS asset.

### Priority 1: Landing-page-to-protocol conversion program (recommended starting engagement)
- **Research question:** For CPS's highest-traffic gated study pages, what is the fully specified protocol and what do the results show under transparent methods?
- **Study design:** A repeatable conversion template (population, index date, exposure, comparator, follow-up, analysis plan, missing-data handling) applied first to the diabetes risk-stratification study (already published, lowest risk) and then to the AFP, fill-time, and dashboard studies; each output is a structured methods box and a peer-reviewable abstract.
- **Primary outcome domain:** Methodological transparency across clinical, access, and utilization endpoints.
- **Target health-system audience:** Pharmacy leadership and quality committees performing due diligence.
- **Builds on:** `Risk_Strat_Poster_AMCP_Final.pdf`, `outcomes-study-risk-stratification-jpp-published.html`, and the gated study catalog. This is the recommended starting engagement because it requires no new data collection, demonstrates the firm's rigor on CPS's own material, and is low-cost and fast.

### Priority 2: Therigy benchmark governance and risk adjustment
- **Research question:** What measure definitions, denominators, sample-size thresholds, and risk-adjustment model make Therigy cross-site benchmarks defensible for a heterogeneous health-system network?
- **Study design:** A benchmark governance framework: measure dictionary with numerator/denominator logic, minimum-cell suppression, a case-mix risk-adjustment/stratification model (severity, payer mix, service-line maturity, staffing), refresh cadence, and risk-adjusted funnel-plot reporting.
- **Primary outcome domain:** Benchmark validity across clinical, access, and operational measures.
- **Target health-system audience:** Pharmacy informatics, quality leadership, and any system comparing itself to a Therigy peer benchmark.
- **Builds on:** `2023-therigystm-data-and-analytics-report.html`, `cps-software-management-enables-unprecedented-opportunities-for-outcomes-research.html`, and the dashboard studies. Differentiated because it operationalizes CPS's distinctive multi-site asset; high feasibility because the data infrastructure already exists.

### Priority 3: Multi-site risk-adjusted comparative effectiveness of HSSP care models
- **Research question:** Across CPS sites, which HSSP care-model variant (embedded pharmacist, liaison-supported, standard) produces the best persistence/adherence and lowest avoidable utilization, and for which patients?
- **Study design:** Multi-site retrospective cohort using Therigy data with mixed-effects models for site clustering and propensity-score or entropy-balancing case-mix adjustment; begin with rheumatoid arthritis (extending the ISPOR 2026 persistence work) or diabetes (extending SOMC).
- **Primary outcome domain:** Clinical/adherence outcomes and avoidable utilization.
- **Target health-system audience:** Service-line chiefs and pharmacy leadership choosing a care-model design.
- **Builds on:** `cps-hssp-research-top-5-ispor-2026.html` (machine-learning RA persistence), `Risk_Strat_Poster_AMCP_Final.pdf`, and the Therigy scale assets. Highest differentiation; answers a question no single site can answer; moderate feasibility given multi-site data governance.

### Priority 4: Prescription capture and retention and net contribution study
- **Research question:** How much system-originated specialty volume does the HSSP retain that would otherwise leak externally, and what is the continuity and net-contribution consequence?
- **Study design:** Retrospective pre-post (staggered-adoption difference-in-differences where applicable) using TherigyVista capture and leakage data plus EHR, with capture rate and retained net contribution as co-primary measures and downstream utilization as a linked endpoint.
- **Primary outcome domain:** Prescription capture and retention and total cost of care to the system.
- **Target health-system audience:** System finance and pharmacy leadership making the build/expand decision.
- **Builds on:** `cps-launches-therigyvista-pharmacy-analytics-software.html` and the build case study. High value because it speaks to the central build-or-outsource decision; feasibility depends on EHR linkage.

### Priority 5: Clinical-to-economic translation layer for the flagship clinical portfolio
- **Research question:** What is the system-level avoidable-utilization and capacity consequence of CPS's documented clinical effects (diabetes A1C, migraine days, IPF and dermatology cost avoidance, IV-to-self-injection transition)?
- **Study design:** Decision-analytic modeling anchored to measured effect sizes with probabilistic sensitivity analysis, validated against facility utilization where available; replaces borrowed unit costs with counterfactual-anchored estimates where data allow.
- **Primary outcome domain:** Total cost of care to the system, avoidable utilization, and clinic capacity (for example infusion-suite demand released by IV-to-self-injection transitions).
- **Target health-system audience:** CFOs and service-line chiefs.
- **Builds on:** `Risk_Strat_Poster_AMCP_Final.pdf`, the IPF and dermatology cost-avoidance posters, and the ISPOR 2024 IV-to-self-injection poster. Converts existing clinical credibility into the economic argument that CFOs and service-line chiefs require.

## 5. Differentiation note

CPS already produces more HSSP outcomes content than almost any organization in this set: an annual benchmark report in its fifth edition, a continuous conference-poster pipeline, peer-reviewed and award-recognized work, and a cross-site data platform at 100,000,000+ responses. The firm should therefore not compete on volume. What CPS does not currently do, on the public evidence, is set out below, and it is precisely what we add.

- **Rigor and verifiability:** The readable studies are descriptive, single-site, or unadjusted pre-post, and most detail is gated. Our contribution is comparator-anchored, risk-adjusted, fully specified protocols (propensity-score matching, interrupted time series, staggered-adoption difference-in-differences) applied to CPS's own material rather than new marketing.
- **Benchmark governance:** CPS markets cross-site comparison but states no risk adjustment, no measure dictionary, and no sample-size thresholds, so the heterogeneity of health-system populations is an unmanaged threat to every benchmark. We supply the governance that makes the Therigy advantage defensible rather than merely large.
- **Multi-site comparative question:** CPS's distinctive asset is the only one in this prospect set that can compare care-model variants across many health systems with risk adjustment, and CPS has not yet deployed it that way. That is a differentiated, specific, and tailored study only CPS can credibly field.
- **Decision translation:** CPS reports clinical and PROM endpoints and one borrowed-unit-cost economic figure, but does not connect outcomes to the system-level decisions (build, expand, retain, optimize) that its health-system customers actually make. We add the prescription-retention, clinician-capacity, avoidable-utilization, and net-contribution layer that turns clinical proof into a case the system's board can act on.

In each instance the addition is rigor and comparability, not more content.

## Glossary

**Health-system specialty pharmacy (HSSP):** a specialty pharmacy owned and operated by a health system to serve its own patients.

**Prescription capture (capture rate):** the share of specialty prescriptions written by a health system's providers that are filled by that system's own specialty pharmacy rather than an external pharmacy.

**Leakage:** specialty prescriptions written within a health system but filled at an outside pharmacy, so the system loses the associated dispensing revenue and visibility into the patient's care.

**Prescription retention:** keeping eligible specialty prescriptions within the system's own specialty pharmacy, that is, reducing leakage.

**Time to therapy (time to first fill):** the elapsed time from when a prescription is written to when the patient receives the medication.

**Persistence:** continuing to refill and remain on a therapy over time.

**Abandonment:** a prescription that is approved or ready but never picked up or filled.

**Patient-reported outcome (PRO) / patient-reported outcome measure (PROM):** an outcome reported directly by the patient (for example symptom burden or quality of life), usually via a validated questionnaire.

**Minimal clinically important difference (MCID):** the smallest change in an outcome measure that patients or clinicians would consider meaningful.

**Hemoglobin A1c (A1C):** a blood marker of average blood glucose over roughly three months, used to gauge diabetes control.

**Dermatology Life Quality Index (DLQI):** a validated patient questionnaire measuring the effect of skin disease on quality of life.

**Patient-reported outcome instruments (AIRQ, PozQoL, PROMIS-10):** the **Asthma Impairment and Risk Questionnaire (AIRQ)** is a validated questionnaire assessing asthma control; **PozQoL** is a validated questionnaire measuring quality of life for people living with HIV; **PROMIS-10** is a short, validated general measure of physical and mental health-related quality of life.

**Health-related quality of life (HRQoL):** a measure of how health affects a person's overall well-being and daily functioning.

**Social determinants of health (SDOH) / health-related social needs (HRSN):** non-medical factors (for example housing, transportation, food, income) that affect health and access to care.

**Total cost of care:** total spending on a patient's care across all settings over a defined period.

**Specialty pharmacy accreditation (URAC, ACHC):** quality certifications from independent bodies that many payers and manufacturers require for network and drug access.

**Comparison group (comparator):** a group that did not receive the intervention, used as a baseline to judge the intervention's effect.

**Propensity-score matching:** a method that pairs intervention and comparison participants who have similar characteristics so the two groups are comparable.

**Entropy balancing:** a weighting method that makes a comparison group statistically similar to the intervention group on chosen characteristics.

**Risk adjustment (case-mix adjustment):** statistically accounting for differences in participants' health and complexity so comparisons are fair.

**Difference-in-differences:** a method that compares the before-and-after change in a group that received an intervention with the change over the same period in a group that did not, to isolate the intervention's effect.

**Interrupted time series:** a method that measures an outcome at many points before and after an intervention to separate the intervention's effect from pre-existing trends.

**Funnel plot:** a statistical display that shows each site's result against the range of variation expected by chance, used to flag true outliers in benchmarking.

**Mixed-effects model:** a regression method that accounts for data grouped within sites or clusters when estimating an effect.

**Counterfactual:** an estimate of what would have happened without the intervention.

**Confidence interval:** a range around an estimate that expresses how much uncertainty it carries.

**Sensitivity analysis:** re-running an analysis under different assumptions to test whether the conclusions hold.

**Comparative effectiveness:** research that directly compares two or more care approaches to determine which produces better outcomes.
