# Optimizing Specialty Medication Access Through Multi-Step Order Transmittal (MSOT) Workflows: A Single Health System Evaluation

UVAHealth logo

Diana Le, PharmD, MBA │ Nathan Hart, PharmD, MBA │ Joshua Weber, PharmD, MBA-HCM, CSP, BCMTMS, 340B ACE

## BACKGROUND

* Specialty pharmacy workflows face growing challenges from payer lockouts, Limited Distribution Drug (LDD) restrictions, and complex access pathways.<sup>1</sup>

* Multi-Step Order Transmittal (MSOT) within an electronic health record (EHR) centralizes and streamlines prescription access across ambulatory and specialty clinics.<sup>1</sup>

* Unlike conventional provider initiated prior authorization processes, MSOT automatically routes complex prescriptions via embedded EHR logic to a centralized team, ensuring earlier and more coordinated access support without additional provider burden.<sup>1</sup>

* This approach aims to improve prescription capture rates, reduce turnaround time, and increase visibility into access barriers.

## OBJECTIVES

To evaluate the impact of MSOT implementation across Neurology, Rheumatology, and Cardiology clinics on:

1) Prescription turnaround time (TAT)

2) Prescription capture rate

3) LDD and payer lockout identification

4) Provider satisfaction via Net Promoter Score (NPS)

## METHODS

* Retrospective, pre/post descriptive cohort analysis conducted from March 2024 – April 2025 at a single academic medical center.

* Evaluated outcomes before and after centralized pharmacy intervention through MSOT implementation.

* Descriptive analysis only.

Key interventions: Benefits Investigation (BI), Prior Authorization (PA) completion, and prescription fulfillment.

Data sources: Epic, MSOT queue, pharmacy dispense records, and Qualtrics survey. Limitations: Manual baseline tracking, incomplete PA capture, lower MSOT volume during phased rollout.

Pre-MSOT data (March 2024 – September 2024):

* TAT from intervention need to dispense (CoverMyMeds (CMM) and Epic).

* LDD and payer lockout tracked manually in Excel.

* Capture rate: % of prescriptions filled by UVA.

Post-MSOT data (October 2024 – April 2025):

* TAT from MSOT entry to adjudication or intervention through dispense.

* LDD and payer lockout from MSOT order questionnaires.

* Capture rate: % of MSOT prescriptions filled by UVA.

* NPS: % change in provider satisfaction from baseline.

## DISCUSSION

* The observed 53% reduction in turnaround time not only streamlines provider workflows but supports earlier therapy initiation which is critical in managing chronic and progressive conditions.

* Higher prescription capture within the health system also ensures closer care coordination and potential cost savings through avoidance of fragmented dispensing.

* The significant increase in prescription capture rate from 33% to 52% demonstrates better retention of prescriptions within the health system.

* LDD and payer lockout identification increased by 40%, enabling earlier access intervention.

* Provider NPS improved from +10% (96%) post-MSOT, reflecting enhanced trust and satisfaction with pharmacy services amid reduced administrative burden.

* Manual tracking and phased rollout limited data completeness, highlighting opportunities for broader automation.

## CONCLUSIONS

* MSOT offers scalable and meaningful benefits for health system specialty pharmacy programs.

* At UVA Health, MSOT implementation led to faster patient access, higher prescription capture, and proved provider satisfaction.

* The workflow also enabled strategic insight into access barriers such as payer and manufacturer restrictions.

* Expanding MSOT beyond pilot clinics could extend these benefits across more therapeutic areas.

* Integrating real-time analytics may further enhance performance and uncover additional value in complex medication pathways.<sup>1-3</sup>
