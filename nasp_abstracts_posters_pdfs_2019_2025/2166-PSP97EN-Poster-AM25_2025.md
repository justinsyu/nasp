# The Economic Benefit Of Biosimilars In North America: A Targeted Literature Review

Steven Ren PharmD<sup>1</sup>, Kunal Shastri MSc<sup>2</sup>, Kerise Clarke MSc<sup>3</sup>, Margaret H. Ainslie-Garcia MSc<sup>3</sup> | <sup>1</sup>Fresenius Kabi USA, LLC, <sup>2</sup>Fresenius Kabi SwissBioSim GmbH, Eysins, Switzerland, <sup>3</sup>EVERSANA, Burlington, Canada

## Introduction & Objective

Biosimilars are defined as “a biologic drug that is highly similar to a biologic drug that was already authorized for sale” by Health Canada.<sup>1</sup>

To be approved by regulatory agencies, biosimilars must provide similar efficacy and safety as their reference products.

A key **advantage** of biosimilars is their economic benefit compared to reference products due to their more **affordable** price, which can provide considerable savings to payers depending on utilization rates.<sup>2</sup>

OBJECTIVE: To assess the current and expected savings from biosimilar use in North America.

## Methods

A targeted literature review was conducted using MEDLINE and grey literature from January 2020 to January 2025.

The following keywords were searched with “biosimilar”: “projection*”, “economic”, “ex ante analysis”, “budget impact model”.

Peer reviewed publications and economic reports from industry, accredited associations, and government agencies were included within the literature search.

Only studies published in English were eligible for inclusion.

## Results

A total of 27 peer-reviewed publications and four reports were deemed to be relevant to the search question.

Analyses were conducted for Canada (n=11) and the US (n=20).

An economic benefit was identified or forecasted by 93% (n=29) of studies, all of which reported substantial savings for both Canadian and US payers.

The remaining two studies reported economic benefit was site-specific (eg, office setting) or stakeholder-specific (eg, patient).*

## CANADA

Icon showing $731M Non-medical switching policies have been implemented in Canada to drive biosimilar uptake. The pilot adopter British Columbia reported $731M in savings from the use of nine biosimilars<sup>†</sup> between 2019–2024.<sup>3</sup>

Icon showing savings Analyses indicate that adoption of even **<u>one</u>** biosimilar can result in meaningful savings: projections estimate implementation of **pro-active switching policies** could provide an estimated $140M savings for a ranibizumab biosimilar alone.<sup>4</sup>

## Conclusion

A growing body of literature validates the substantial economic benefits associated with the introduction or increased use of biosimilars for the US and Canada.

Health economic decision-makers should consider policies that favor biosimilars use as a tool to control costs.
