# Improving Adherence in Patients Taking Cycle-directed Oral Anti-cancer Medications

MU Health Care logo

Olivia Howard, PharmD, CSP; Amanda Stiers, CPhT-Adv
University of Missouri Health Care, Columbia, MO

## Background

* Adherence is the degree or extent to which a patient acts in accordance with prescribed intervals and doses of a medication regimen

* Benefits of patient-administered oral anti-cancer medications (OAMs) are balanced by challenges in maintaining adherence:

    - o Complex dosing regimens, toxicity/adverse effects, drug interactions, monitoring

* Consequences of non-adherence include lower drug efficacy leading to potentially worse outcomes such as decreased survival

* Studies have shown adherence aids such as treatment calendars, one-on-one education, and pill boxes improve adherence in patients taking OAMs

    - o Curry et al. found an increase in adherence from 30% to 85% over 2 years

* Mizzou Specialty Pharmacy recognized non-adherence in cycle-directed OAMs and wanted to conduct a quality improvement project to improve patient care

* Mizzou Specialty Pharmacy pharmacists and technicians collaborate to provide refill assessments, one-on-one treatment counseling, and follow up assessments, allowing for easy education and monitoring of cycle-directed OAMs

* Additionally, due to the limited study of cycle-directed OAM treatment calendars in oncology patients, further research is needed

## Purpose

To improve adherence in patients taking cycle-directed OAMs at Mizzou Specialty Pharmacy

## Methods

**Design & Plan**

* Single center, observational study

* Patients provided a treatment calendar with each refill including start/stop dates & check boxes for dose reminders

* Treatment calendars scanned into the electronic medical record for provider reference

* Adherence assessed via patient recollection, cycle date confirmation, and number of pills left at time of assessment

* At each adherence check, patients gave feedback on opinion & use of calendars

* Data collected from September 2023 to September 2024

**Primary Outcome**

* Number of patients who remained adherent on cycle-directed therapy after receiving a treatment calendar with each refill

**Secondary Outcome**

* Reasons for non-adherence

## Results

**Results (N = 34)**

74% (n= 25) of patients were adherent to their therapy regimen after receiving treatment calendars

* Of the 25 adherent patients, 4 were adherent but had delays to their treatment due to poor labs (provider-directed hold)

Of the 26% (n= 9) of non-adherent patients, the most common reason for non-adherence was fear of adverse effects or forgetting/not using calendar

Average number of cycles included for each patient = 6.67 cycles

97% (n=33) patients stated the calendars were helpful for adherence

## Conclusion

**Strengths**

* This study’s results had similar results to literature in terms of predicted outcomes

**Limitations**

* Fewer number of patients due to implementation for only six medications

* Lack of ability to compare pre/post data

**Conclusions**

* Adherence was achieved in majority of patients who used treatment calendars

* Fear of side effects and forgetting were the top reasons for non-adherence

* Patients and providers were satisfied with adherence aids provided

**Next Steps**

* Continuing to expand the project by providing treatment calendars for all cycle-directed therapies provided at Mizzou Specialty Pharmacy

* Expand pharmacist counseling to include in-depth review of the calendars upon receipt

* Expand provider & clinic education to review treatment calendar location in the EMR to improve scheduling errors
