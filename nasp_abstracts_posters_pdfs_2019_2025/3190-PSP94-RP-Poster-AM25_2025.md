# Development and implementation of a novel, pharmacist-led, long-acting HIV pre-exposure prophylaxis administration service in a health system outpatient pharmacy

<sup>1</sup>UCSF Health; <sup>2</sup>University of California San Francisco School of Pharmacy

## Background

<u>**Uptake of HIV Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis (PrEP)**</u>

* US far from goal of ending the HIV epidemic.

* PrEP lowers a person’s risk of acquiring HIV.

* 2012-2022: States with highest PrEP had 38% *decrease* in new HIV diagnoses. States with lowest PrEP had 27% *increase*

CDC has estimated that approximately 1.2 million people could benefit from PrEP, and a recently published study suggests that it could be double that.

<u>**Advances in HIV PrEP**</u>

* Highly effective PrEP available in long-acting (LA) injectables administered q 2-6 months.

    - Cabotegravir 600 mg IM q2 months (after load)

    - Lenacapavir 927 mg subcutaneously q6 mos (after load)

* LA-PrEP must be administered by healthcare provider

* Typically reimbursed through medical billing (CA Medicaid also covers through pharmacy benefit)

## Context

* Primary care clinics may not have adequate staff, space, familiarity, or budget for LA-PrEP.

* SB 159 (2020) allows pharmacists to furnish HIV PEP and 60-day supply of HIV PrEP.

* SB 339 (2023) allows pharmacists to furnish ongoing PrEP if clinical conditions met & to receive payment for services through Medicaid.

* Both 159/339 generally reference “FDA-approved PrEP”

## Program Description

UCSF Health provides LA-PrEP in the HIV Clinic. Providers at other UCSF locations expressed interest in providing LA-PrEP for their patients but did not have resources to establish a new service. The Ambulatory Pharmacy is establishing a long-acting injectable administration program to meet these needs.

* **Exploration (Oct 2024 - Jan 2025)**
    - **Outer context**: Rationale for program development, determining needs/alternatives, identifying potential program champions, reviewing B&P codes and CA SB 159/339, discussing with legal
    - **Inner context**: Understanding UCSF priorities, exploring revenue streams, determining internal team & champions, operating as clinic

* **Preparation (Jan-May 2025)**
    - **Communication/goals**: Weekly meetings to review progress/goals
    - **Enhancing pharmacist support**: SOPs, detailed workflow, training
    - **Enhancing Health IT infrastructure**: referrals, pharmacist privileges, visit type (INJ vs. IMM), Epic smartphrases, scheduling
    - **Purchasing materials**: Bed for consult room, other supplies
    - **Securing organizational approvals**: EMB, pharmacy leadership

* **Implementation (June 2025 - )**
    - **Obtaining product**: CAM workflow different than pharmacy acquisition
    - **Securing patient care space**: Shift to partnership with urgent care
    - **Separating screening visit & INJ appointment**: 340B/facility billing
    - **First patient 6/27/2025**: Onsite coaching with SME, debrief after

* **Sustainment (June 2025 - )**
    - **Ongoing training**: enhancing pharmacist confidence/experience
    - **Cycle for ordering supplies**: establish list/system/dates
    - **Following revenue cycle**: Pharmacist billing and drug reimbursement
    - **Discussing volume/capacity**: Interest growing (!), new medicines

## Challenges During Pilot

* Drug procurement processes different for clinic & pharmacy

* 2-inch needles needed for larger BMI (>30)

* Staff pharmacists hold different privileges in EMR than ambulatory care pharmacists (e.g. facility fee billing)

* An existing drug order for the established (maintenance dose) patient – required new (pharmacist-led) order to operate fully under SB159/339

* Laboratory does not provide supplies for 3-site STI testing.

* Post administration billing required clarification

Lightbulb icon

## Lessons Learned To Date

* Creation of a pharmacy clinic within a community pharmacy is potential pathway for clinic-administered meds & medical billing

* Organization-specific interpretations of SB 159/339 as applied to long-acting injectable PrEP can be favorable

* Early assessment/testing of HIT infrastructure needed for “clinic-based” pharmacist vs. “pharmacy-based” pharmacist

* General SOP plus detailed step-by-step workflow should be created for optimal staff support

* Must procure drug/supplies not usually in pharmacy earlier

* Slow pilot is helpful for workflow, but info and patterns regarding revenue will be slow in returning

* A pharmacy-led injectable HIV PrEP service can support patient access and reduce clinic burden

* Planning requires active teamwork and facile communication across clinics, subject matter experts, health IT, medical center leadership, and pharmacy staff.
